# MEMORANDUM

**TO:** Reviewers and Readers of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan

**FROM:** Joe Mantione, Comprehensive Planner

**SUBJECT:** Production of the Revised Comprehensive Plan Document

All text changes adopted as part of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan (BVCP) ten-year update are now incorporated into this document. We plan to produce two versions of the revised plan; this version, to be distributed during late fall 1990, and a second version to be distributed by a target date of mid-to-late 1991. The Fall 1990 version provides a new readable format for the text, but it does not include any photos or illustrations. The purpose of the Fall 1990 BVCP document is to provide all users of the Comprehensive Plan with a document that has all newly adopted changes in it.

As part of the ten-year update and the 1989-90 annual changes, new maps were also adopted. The official version of these maps consists of a large-scale set of individual maps on file in the City Planning Department (and a duplicate set in the County Land Use Department). Small blackline prints of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map and the Areas I, II, III Map are inserted in this version and are exact reproductions of the official maps. Also, there are 21 smaller maps representing the remaining maps and are included in this version for illustrative purposes.

The purpose of the proposed 1991 Comprehensive Plan document is to provide a more elegant document, one that includes photos and illustrations, as well as much improved color maps. In that version, new maps will be produced using the County Geographic Information System and will be much more accurate and readable. These maps will be reviewed by the City, the County, and the public before they are incorporated into the 1991 Comprehensive Plan document. We hope that by providing these two new versions of the Comprehensive Plan users will be provided with the most up-to-date document now and enhancements to the final document in the future.

City and County staff are currently working on digitizing the maps and developing a format for the 1991 Comprehensive Plan document. We would appreciate input from any reviewers or readers to help us produce the 1991 document. Specifically, any ideas on revising the format, developing the new digitized maps, or producing photos or illustrations would be very helpful. In addition, if any mistakes requiring corrections are identified, please let us know. If you have any ideas to offer, please call me at the City Planning Department at 441-3270 or call Pete Fogg at the County Land Use Department at 441-3930.

Thanks in advance for your help in making the Comprehensive Plan the best document possible.

## Section 1:

## GENERAL POLICIES

**The City and the County share responsibility for planning land use and development in the Boulder Valley. The City-County Intergovernmental Agreement of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is a unique feature, and strengthens the basis for implementing the Plan. The City and the County recognize also that implementation of the Plan may affect and be affected by decisions of other agencies and jurisdictions in the County and metropolitan area.**

### 1.01

The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, hereafter referred to as the Comprehensive Plan, shall be based on specific and nonconflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and Colorado Revised Statutes, as amended (1973) §29-1-203, §29-20-101 et seq., §30-28-106 through 31-23-211. The Comprehensive Plan, consisting of a series of maps together with explanatory statements, shall be derived to the maximum extent possible from the policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the Boulder City Charter, Colorado Revised Statutes as amended, §29-1-203, §29-20-101 et seq., §30-28-106 through 30-28-109, §31-12-101 et seq., and §31-23-206 through 31-23-211.

### 1.03

The length of the planning period is approximately fifteen years. This term has been determined to be reasonable and has been arrived at after weighing a combination of facts and policy considerations: land use and land use projections, Service Area projections, the capacity of the City to fund capital improvements, the growth rate, and the desires of the community in regard to accommodating growth. Each 5-year review of the plan shall extend the planning period approximately five years.

### 1.04

Projections of land use in the Comprehensive Plan are sufficiently detailed to be definitive and directional, although not limiting.

### 1.05

Joint planning and close cooperation among the University, the School Districts, the City, the County, regional organizations, and other policy-making bodies (e.g., the County Board of Health, utility and fire service entities) influencing the physical and social development of the Boulder Valley shall be aggressively pursued so as to avoid conflicts and to provide a means by which each entity may more fully benefit from the presence of others.

### 1.02

The Comprehensive Plan shall generally encompass the Boulder Valley which is defined as those areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Davidson Mesa and the Coal Creek drainage on the southeast, the south county line on the south, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir and land west of Left Hand Valley Reservoir on the north, and as specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map.

## 1.06

Development referrals and responses of the City and the County shall be coordinated through the respective planning departments according to a system which has been developed by the planning departments and approved by the legislative bodies of the City and the County. The responses of each department shall specifically identify those requirements which a proposed development meets, or fails to meet, under the Comprehensive Plan.

## 1.07

The City and the County may enter into intergovernmental agreements with other municipalities and agencies when appropriate to foster intergovernmental planning and cooperation.

## 1.08

It is recognized that the acquisition of open space lands in the Boulder Valley is based upon the City's overall Open Space Plan in which prioritization of need and available revenues are considered. From time to time, however, requests for development are made for parcels of land privately held which are designated as Open Space on the Comprehensive Plan. In such cases it is the obligation of the City to make decisions with regard to the contemplated acquisition of such lands in a timely and responsible fashion. In the event that a negotiated agreement cannot be reached and the City does not wish to initiate condemnation action, then the property owner may proceed to request development consistent with other applicable policies of the Comprehensive Plan.

## 1.09

With regard to public facilities owned and operated in the other's jurisdiction, the City and the County shall respect and abide by existing land use regulations insofar as reasonably practicable.

For the purpose of policy construction:

Where the "County" alone is referred to within the policy text, said policy may be amended by the County, after referral to the City.

Where the "City" alone is referred to within the policy text, said policy may be amended by the City, after referral to the County.

All other policies shall be construed to be joint City and County statements of policy, and are to be amended by joint action.

Where a particular "area" is not specified in the policy text, the policy shall apply to all areas.

The City and the County shall recognize the rights of citizens to play a role in governmental decisions which affect their lives and property, through continual efforts to maintain and improve open and public communication and conduct of business. ## Section 2:

# COMMUNITY DESIGN

**In Boulder County, and not unlike most other areas throughout the country, land use regulations have traditionally permitted urban uses and development in areas where adequate urban facilities and services are not yet provided, coordinated, or planned. It is uniformly and universally agreed that the resulting patterns of leapfrog remote urban development are inefficient, wasteful, and seriously contrary to the public interest, health, safety, and welfare. One of the most important objectives of the Comprehensive Plan is the reduction, if not elimination, of this urban sprawl. In order to achieve this objective, the City intends to provide, on a phased basis over the planning period, the facilities and services to accommodate urban development in areas contiguous to the City or contiguous to developed properties in the Service Area.**

**The Comprehensive Plan seeks an urban development pattern that is compact and efficient and that permits the most effective and cost efficient provision of city facilities and services. Such a development pattern enhances the liveability of the community for its residents by increasing accessibility to employment, recreation, shopping and other amenities and by reducing auto travel and air pollution. From an urban design perspective, it allows a strong, understandable image of Boulder as a separate community to emerge. Redevelopment and infill, when sensitive to the character of existing neighborhoods, are encouraged as a means of maintaining a compact community.**

### 2.01

For the purposes of Comprehensive Plan structure, the Boulder Valley Planning Area is divided into three major areas. Area I is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate urban facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development. Area II is the area now under County jurisdiction planned to accommodate urban development, and new urban development is to occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services and not otherwise. This area is projected to be provided required facilities and services by the City by being annexed to the City during the planning period; Area IIIA is the area of immediate focus within the first three years, and Area IIIB is available to accommodate development within the balance of the planning period.

Area III is the remaining area in the Boulder Valley, generally under County jurisdiction. This area is not planned to accommodate urban development during the planning period for the following reasons: it is not projected that the City will provide adequate facilities and services in this area within the planning period; no other facilities and services agency comparable to the City is expected in the area; and it is primarily a rural and agricultural area and its character should be preserved and protected.

### 2.02

It is intended that throughout the Boulder Valley, new urban development, including all development within the City and those developments regulated in the unincorporated areas of the Valley by Boulder County pursuant to Colorado Revised Statutes, as amended, §30-28-133, not occur until and unless adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development as set out in Policy Section Three and in the Urban Service Criteria and Standards Section of the Comprehensive Plan.

New urban development is hereby defined to include:

- (a) All residential, commercial and industrial development within the City; or
- (b) The subdivision of land pursuant to Colorado Revised Statutes as amended §30-28-133, and the County's subdivision regulations; excepting those developments which could proceed under the County's agricultural (non-urban) density transfer policies; or

(c) Any proposed residential, commercial, business or industrial development subject to County rezoning or special use hearings and processes; provided the County determines that any of the following factors applies to the proposed use(s):

- i. is (are) inconsistent with the Area II and land use projections contained within the Comprehensive Plan Map; or
- ii. adversely and materially affect(s) the adopted Capital Improvements Program of the City of Boulder, and thereby affect(s) the projected adequacy or availability of urban services to the immediate area of the proposed use(s) and to Area II in general; or
- iii. would require a level of urban facilities and services equal to or greater than those defined in Policy 3.02; or
- iv. is (are) inconsistent with the Comprehensive Plan policies and intent.

(b) Annexation of existing substantially developed areas will be offered in a manner and on terms and conditions which respect existing life styles and densities, and the City will expect these areas to be brought to City standards only where necessary to protect the health and safety of the residents of the subject area or of the City. The City, in developing annexation plans of reasonable cost, may phase new facilities and services. The County, which now has jurisdiction over these areas, shall be a supportive partner with the City in annexation efforts.

(c) The Gunbarrel-Heatherwood Subcommunity, which is unique because of its size, developed at an urban density with city water and sewer service. Some services, such as road maintenance and law enforcement, are now provided by the County. Area residents tax themselves to pay for fire protection. Although interest in voluntary annexation has been limited, the City and the County continue to support the eventual annexation of Gunbarrel-Heatherwood. Both governments agree that the area should remain as Area III on the Comprehensive Plan map. The City is committed to offering reasonable annexation terms within the limits of available appropriations, providing an urban level of services to the area in the near term, and supporting any resident or landowner annexation initiatives.

(d) For undeveloped areas, the City will impose terms and conditions to protect the public interest and attempt not to impose terms such that annexation is unrealistic or impracticable.

(e) There will be no annexations of areas outside the boundaries of the Boulder Valley Planning Area, with the exception of annexation of acquired open space.

(f) Area II is anticipated to become part of the City within the planning period. Area III is not anticipated to become part of the City within the planning period. However, publicly owned property located in Area III and intended to remain in Area III may be annexed to the City if the property requires less than a full range of urban services or requires inclusion under City jurisdiction for health, welfare and safety reasons.

Major entryways into the Boulder Valley shall be identified, protected and enhanced in order to emphasize and preserve the natural setting and appearance of the community. Future strip commercial development shall be discouraged along major arterials.

## **2.15**

## **2.16**

New residential, commercial, and industrial development and redevelopment shall be encouraged to follow sound and innovative land use planning. The goals are to provide a liveable built environment and, through the judicious use of landscaping, materials and human scale, to respect the character of the surrounding area.

## **2.17**

Incentives shall be provided to encourage all types of housing, lessen monotony in site design, provide for more functional open space, achieve greater variety in housing choice, encourage alternative transportation modes, and accommodate more attractive and economical landscaping.

## **2.18**

The City shall encourage, consistent with other land use policies, a variety of land uses, provided that the predominant land use is consistent with the Comprehensive Plan land use designation, including but not limited to neighborhood shopping facilities or services.

## **2.19**

The unique and powerful community identity and sense of place as recognized by the citizens of the Boulder Valley and characterized by its setting shall be respected by policy decisionmakers.

## **2.20**

The City and the County shall strive to maintain and enhance an open land buffer that separates the Boulder Valley from surrounding communities and contributes to a distinct community identity for the City.

## **2.21**

In its community design planning, the City shall support and strengthen its residential neighborhoods. The City shall seek appropriate building scale and compatible character of new development or redevelopment, desired public facilities and mixed commercial uses, and sensitively designed and sized rights-of-way.

## **2.22**

The City shall continue to develop and implement strategies which preserve the human scale and the historical and architectural character of the City's older neighborhoods. When redevelopment is proposed, sensitivity to the existing context will be required. Many of these residential neighborhoods are adjacent to commercial areas. Special attention to protect the quality of these interface areas shall be encouraged.

## **2.23**

The City shall encourage a neighborhood concept for new development which includes a variety of residential densities, opportunities for shopping, nearby support services and conveniently sited public facilities, including roads and pedestrian connections, parks, libraries, and schools.

# Section Three:

## FACILITIES AND SERVICES

**The City and the County agree that urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services is available to serve the development. The City and the County find that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.**

### 3.01

The City and the County intend that new urban development not occur until adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of urban facilities and services by a municipality is preferable to provision of urban facilities and services by special districts and private groups in part because municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of adequate urban facilities and services. Therefore it is hereby presumed that adequate facilities and services can be provided only by the City of Boulder. The City shall extend, furnish or provide such services at such time as it can provide them all as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) below and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. However, it is not the intent to preclude the development and use of alternative facilities and service systems for new urban development so long as they are adequate as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) and the Urban Service Criteria and Standards section of this plan.

(c) The availability and adequacy of urban facilities and services as set forth in subparagraphs a. and b. above shall be determined based upon the following criteria and as further defined in the Urban Service Criteria and Standards section of this plan. Determinations of facilities and services adequacy shall be based upon the following criteria:

- i. Responsiveness to public objectives;
- ii. Sufficiency and dependability of financing;
- iii. Operational effectiveness;
- iv. Adequacy of equipment and facilities; and
- v. Proficiency of personnel.

# Section Four:

# ENVIRONMENT

**The fragile environment which characterizes the Boulder Valley is a critical asset that must be preserved and protected. It is the framework within which growth and development may be permitted to take place.**

**Information on environmental features and programs, including maps for implementing environmental policies, can be found in the Environmental Resources and Programs section of the Comprehensive Plan.**

## 4.01

The environmental effects of public and private projects shall be considered in the public decisionmaking process. Local, state, and federal environmental review processes may be followed when appropriate.

## 4.05

Hazardous areas which present danger to life and property from flood, forest fire, steep slopes, erosion, unstable soil, subsidence, or similar geological development constraints shall be delineated, and development in such areas carefully controlled or prohibited.

## 4.02

The Boulder Valley shall be planned in a manner which recognizes air quality as a critical health, economic, and aesthetic concern. Therefore, the City and the County shall encourage logical land use relationships, expanded use of public transportation and other alternate modes, use of pollutant control devices, stabilization of soils, appropriate monitoring of construction and mining operations, and such other means as may be feasible.

## 4.06

Hillside and ridge-line development shall be carried out in a manner which, to the extent possible, avoids both negative environmental consequences to the immediate and surrounding area, as well as the degrading of views and vistas from and of public areas.

## 4.03

The City and the County shall protect, maintain, and improve the quality of the Boulder Valley waterways through identification and control of indirect, as well as direct, sources of pollution. Particular attention shall be given to improved land use configurations, standards to control degradation of streams and lakes caused by storm runoff in urban and rural areas, and control and monitoring of direct sources of discharge, including those of gravel extraction and wastewater treatment facilities.

## 4.07

The functional and aesthetic qualities of drainage courses and waterways shall be preserved and enhanced. A noncontainment approach to flood management shall be used on Boulder Creek. A generally non-structural approach to flood control that emphasizes a natural appearance shall be used on all major water courses and drainage ways. In some cases a structural solution may be used, consistent with adopted master plans.

## 4.04

Noise abatement shall be a continuing environmental concern which shall be implemented through improved land use relationships, as well as by ordinance, with particular attention being given to the impact of transportation and industrial facilities and proper acoustical design.

# Section Six:

# TRANSPORTATION

As in other cities throughout the United States, the dominant mode of transportation in the Boulder Valley is the automobile. Population densities are low, and the compact form of the City allows relatively quick auto driving time. However, continued primary reliance on the automobile to meet growing transportation needs is likely to worsen air pollution, increase traffic congestion and noise to levels that most residents consider unacceptable, require widening of streets, and increase the amount of land devoted to road rights-of-way.

The City is committed to improve travel modes that offer an alternative to the automobile and to provide adequate financing for these alternatives and the improvement and maintenance of the street network.

## 6.01

The Boulder Valley shall be served by an integrated all-mode transportation system. The City and the County shall strive to eliminate conflicts between auto, transit, bicycle, and pedestrian users and provide safe and convenient connections between modes.

## 6.02

The City and the County shall promote greater use of car pools and alternatives to single occupancy automobile travel with the target of achieving a 15 percent overall reduction in the ratio of single occupancy auto trips to all trips by 2010.

## 6.03

The City and the County shall strive to make bicycling, walking, and transit more convenient and safe. The City shall strive to provide accessible and affordable transit service, developed with the County, RTD, other agencies, and private providers.

## 6.04

The City shall work cooperatively with the County and other entities such as the University of Colorado, the Boulder Valley School District, and private employers to design complementary strategies for achieving the desired modal shift.

## 6.05

The City shall make special efforts to provide transportation services and facilities for mobility-impaired persons, as well as youth, elderly, and low-income persons.

## 6.06

In order to protect previous investments and ensure efficient use of the road system, the City and the County shall give high priority to ongoing maintenance, safety improvements, and capacity improvements for existing road facilities which are relatively small projects (such as signalization, adding turn lanes, and signage) that increase level of service without adding through-lanes.

## 6.07

The City and the County shall strive to maintain an acceptable level of service on roads, which generally corresponds to Level of Service D (LOS D). Maintaining this level of service does include some peak-hour auto congestion in order to minimize transportation facility costs, promote a shift to alternate modes, and minimize negative environmental and community impacts in the long run.

## 6.08

If traffic from a proposed development causes degradation below the planned road level of service (LOS D) or causes unacceptable community and environmental impacts, the developer will be required to adequately mitigate these negative impacts.

## 6.09

The City and the County shall design the transportation system to accommodate the planned land use pattern, which includes higher densities and mixed-use in the core area and activity centers, a variety of densities in the fringe areas, compact community size, and the possibility of one or more City auto-free zones in the future.

## Section Eight:

# ENERGY CONSERVATION AND RENEWABLE RESOURCES

**The conservation of non-renewable energy and the conversion to a sustainable energy economy must be promoted by local policies and programs because of the long term problem of adequate energy supply for the community and the short term deleterious effect on the local economy of rising energy costs. ### 8.03

### 8.04

The City shall continue its efforts to upgrade the energy efficiency of the City's existing housing stock, much of which was built in an era of abundant and inexpensive energy. ### 8.05

### 8.06

The City shall dedicate the maximum appropriate funds from new street and highway building to the construction of an efficient, convenient, and safe alternative transportation system that will support bicyclists, pedestrians and mass transit users. ### 8.07

The City and the County shall encourage the conservation of energy through their land use policies which affect placement, orientation, and density of housing, and through other housing policies and regulations. The conservation of energy is served by the development of more intense land use patterns, the provision of recreation, employment and essential services in proximity to housing, and the development of mass transit corridors. ## Section Nine:

# Section III

# BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN IMPLEMENTATION

## A. PLAN AMENDMENTS

**The Comprehensive Plan is an agreement between two governmental entities, as set forth in the Intergovernmental Agreement. Amendments to the plan may be made only by formal amendment procedure involving amendment agreements between the City and the County, unless other provision is made for amendment to the Comprehensive Plan. This section explains the processes and criteria for amendment of the Comprehensive Plan.**

**Areas I and II together form the Boulder Service Area. Area I is currently annexed by the City. In Area II, which is still under County jurisdiction, the City of Boulder projects to annex and provide the required urban services within the 15-year planning period. The distinction between Area IIA and IIB is based on the planned timing of City annexation and service provision. Area IIA is the unincorporated land that is projected for annexation and the provision of services within three years, based on existing urban facilities and services and those scheduled in the City's Capital Improvements Program (CIP). Area IIB is the unincorporated area available to accommodate new urban development within the balance of the planning period. Area III is unincorporated land in the Boulder Valley outside the Boulder Service Area, which is projected to remain non-urban within the planning period.**

# **D. URBAN SERVICE CRITERIA AND STANDARDS**

## **1. PURPOSES OF URBAN SERVICE STANDARDS**

The urban service standards set the benchmark for providing a full range of urban services in the Boulder Valley. A basic premise of the Comprehensive Plan is that "adequate urban facilities and services" are a prerequisite for new urban development and that, within the Boulder Valley, the City of Boulder is the provider of choice for urban services since it can meet all the service provision requirements embodied in the urban service criteria and standards.

These standards are intended to be minimum requirements or thresholds for facilities and services that must be delivered to existing or new urban development to be considered adequate. These adequacy standards allow the County to determine if an urban level of services is met prior to approving new urban development in the unincorporated area, and they provide the City a basis for linking the phasing of growth to the planned provision of a full range of urban services in Area IIB/IIA, annexation, and capital improvement decisions.

The urban service standards for defining adequacy of urban services are included in this section of the Comprehensive Plan. They provide a tool for implementing Policy 3.02 in the Facilities and Services section of the Comprehensive Plan. Adequacy standards are included for those urban services that are required for urban expansion in Area II. These include: public water, public sewer, flood control and drainage, urban fire protection and emergency medical care, urban police protection, urban transportation, and developed urban parks.

## **2. URBAN SERVICE CRITERIA**

Five criteria are to be used in the determination of the adequacy of proposed or existing urban facilities and services consistent with Policy 3.02. The urban service standards are written within the framework of these criteria. They include:

**a. Responsiveness to Public Objectives**

It is desirable and necessary that all urban service systems be coordinated and integrated with other service systems; evaluated periodically for need, efficiency, and cost effectiveness; and studied for possible duplication of other service systems so as to be responsive to local public objectives and general public need as determined by the governing body.

As public funds and resources are limited, primary emphasis must be given to an effective allocation system which, to the greatest extent possible, effects a consistency of legislative intent; public policy; urban service programs funding; a periodic assessment of the type, quantity and quality of various urban services; realistic estimates of revenue sources and future income; maximization of the availability of outside funding sources (state, federal, etc.); and consistency of the long-range program direction in accordance with a capital improvements plan. It has been determined that the municipal budget of the City of Boulder is such an allocation system.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

39

### **b. Sufficiency and Dependability of Financing**

Financing for each urban service program must be based, to the greatest extent possible, on predictable annual revenues which are broad enough to support initial improvements, maintenance, and extensions of facilities and services in relation to, at least, minimum program requirements and unexpected contingencies. Where financing is tied solely to users' fees, serious problems may result if and when the demand for such programs may diminish. At the same time, programs relying heavily on real property revenues or state and federal supplemental funding could be severely restricted if such sources of financing fall short of initial predictions. While no guarantees can be given, financing from a variety of potential sources and spread over a broad base, including sufficient latitude so that funding can be adjusted in the future as changing conditions occur, should be preferred over single-source revenue programs.

### **c. Operational Effectiveness**

Each urban service program may have distinct operational needs and a wide array of operational activities might be considered. The end result, though, is whether or not a given public program is able to function in a direct, efficient manner. As an example, a volunteer fire department, depending upon personnel employed in other endeavors and scattered throughout the area of service may not respond as quickly and effectively as firefighting personnel located where the fire suppression equipment is stored. Also a small sewage treatment plant receiving only limited flows might not be adequately financed to permit proper and continuous monitoring and management.

Measures of operational effectiveness include current and long range project forecasting, coordination with other urban service programs, maximization of economies of scale in urban service provision, and the incorporation of operational processes and organizational methods that have proven effective in similar situations.

### **d. Proficiency of Personnel**

Conditions and factors which may affect the competency of personnel include the following: 1) education and experience of personnel in meeting job demands; 2) interest and willingness of personnel to implement programs; 3) on-the-job training opportunities; 4) working conditions and fringe benefits related to employees' effectiveness; 5) the ability of the urban service agency to pay salaries commensurate with personnel requirements; and, 6) the effectiveness of recruitment programs.

As with other criteria, extremes may be involved, but, in general, the measure of whether or not urban services are being provided must, in part, be determined by the skills of individuals carrying out such programs. For each job a comparison should be made of qualifications of the individual employed or to be employed, against personnel standards established by generally recognized public or technical agencies for similar positions.

### **e. Adequacy of Equipment and Facilities**

This factor may be more easily measured than any of the other since "generally accepted standards" are involved. As an example, as residences are located in closer proximity to each other, interest in developing neighborhood parks will normally increase. As evidenced by recreation programs desired by similar population groupings, certain standards for park areas, spaces and facilities may be predicted. In a similar manner, most of the other public services and facilities described in this study have minimum locational space, equipment and building needs related to given population groupings.

# **E. CITY OF BOULDER PROGRAM AND MASTER PLAN SUMMARIES**

## **1. OPEN SPACE PROGRAM SUMMARY**

### **a. Background Information**

The Open Space Plan including controlled and proposed open space, as well as parkland, provides the basic structure for the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. The Boulder Valley Planning Area has several significant natural features: Marshall Mesa to the southeast, Davidson Mesa to the east, Gunbarrel Hill to the northeast, and Boulder Reservoir to the north, the Dakota Ridge to the northwest and the mountain backdrop to the west, that form the boundaries of influence and planning for the Valley. In addition there are other features within the Boulder Valley that have been designated as Open Space in order to protect them from future development and to provide subcommunity separation, including the major entryways to the City, certain agricultural areas, environmental preservation areas, and the creeks and ditches. The preservation of Open Space provides for the retention of aesthetic and recreational values of open land in proximity to and within the City to obtain a balance and harmony of Open Space and physical development.

Open Space lands will be acquired and preserved, consistent with Boulder's Open Space goals and criteria as specified in the City Charter. These goals and criteria provide an important framework for land use planning within the Valley.

### **b. Service Standards**

The City Charter and the Code define the purposes of Open Space. Open Space land shall be acquired, maintained, preserved, retained, and used only for the following purposes:

- (1) Preservation or restoration of natural areas characterized by or including: terrain; geologic formations, flora, or fauna that are unusual, spectacular, or historically important; water resources; scenic areas and/or vistas; or wildlife habitats or fragile ecosystems.
- (2) Preservation of water resources in their natural or traditional state, scenic areas or vistas, wildlife habitats, or fragile ecosystems;
- (3) Preservation of land for passive recreational use, such as hiking, photography, or nature studies, and, if specifically designated, bicycling, horseback riding, or fishing;
- (4) Preservation of agricultural uses and land suitable for agricultural production;
- (5) Utilization of land for shaping the development of the City, limiting urban sprawl and disciplining growth;
- (6) Utilization of non-urban land for spatial definition of urban areas;
- (7) Utilization of land to prevent encroachment on flood plains; and
- (8) Utilization of land for its aesthetic or passive recreational value and its contribution to the quality of life of the community.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

52

Open Space land as defined in the City Charter may not be improved after acquisition unless such improvements are necessary to protect or maintain the land or to provide for passive recreational, open agricultural, or wildlife habitat use of the land.

The Open Space Board of Trustees shall review the Open Space elements of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and make recommendations concerning any Open Space-related changes to the Plan. The Open Space Board of Trustees shall also pursue vigorously the implementation of the Open Space elements of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and the acquisition of other properties required to fulfill the goals of the Open Space program. Means of implementation may include, without limitation, the acquisition of land, the acquisition of development rights, density transfer, and gifts of land or interests therein to the program.

The "Environmental Preservation" designation overlays land with Open Space characteristics. It denotes an area of environmental concern generally in multiple ownership where several different preservation methods (including other governmental bodies' participation and private ownership) may need to be utilized. These lands will not be considered for control by the City Open Space Program, provided sufficient evidence exists that these lands are to be preserved in a natural state.

Land controlled by the Open Space Board of Trustees will be included in a land management plan for protection and preservation. On lands where agricultural uses are presently taking place, continuation of these activities will be encouraged. Forestry management programs are being developed to ensure a healthy forest for future generations to enjoy.

The Open Space Trails Plan is a conceptual portrayal of existing and proposed major trails, which forms the framework for a major trails system. Additional connector trails will be developed where possible in order to provide an expanding network of passive recreational opportunities for people in and around the City. Existing and proposed trails are shown on the Trails Map (E2-1). The Trails Map consolidates information on off-street open space and park trails and off-street bikeways.

## **2. PARKS AND RECREATION MASTER PLAN SUMMARY**

### **a. Background Information**

The main objective of the Boulder Parks and Recreation Master Plan is to provide parks and recreation programs to serve the needs of the citizens of the City of Boulder.

The basic fabric of the parks and recreation system is the neighborhood and community parks. Other components of the City's park and recreation system include regional parks, park corridors, preserves, athletic complexes, recreation centers and various special use facilities such as tennis courts, handball/ racquetball facilities, and golf courses.

### **b. Service Standards**

#### **(1) Neighborhood Parks**

The standard for neighborhood parks is a minimum of 5 to 10 acres located within 1/2 to 3/4 miles of the population served. The optimum location is in the center of the neighborhood served and adjacent to an elementary school site. Pedestrian and bicycle access is emphasized with minimal, if any, space allocated to the automobile. Typical types of elements that might be included in the design of a neighborhood park are children's play grounds, turf areas for informal open play space, picnic and passive areas, park shelter buildings, play courts, and landscaping. The size and distribution standards outlined above result in 1.5 acres per 1000 population in the neighborhood park category.

#### **(2) Community Parks**

Community parks are 50 to 75 acres in size, located within 1-1/2 to 3 miles of the population served, and in an area that is readily accessible by the major modes of transportation. The design should accommodate the recreational needs of all age groups within the community to be served, and provide a wide variety of both active and passive uses. Applying the size and distribution standards outlined above, will result in 1.5 acres per 1000 population in the community park category.

Typical elements of a community park design might include turfed and landscaped areas for picnicking, walking, sitting, and other passive enjoyment activities as well as the more active play fields, play courts, playgrounds, racquet facilities, a swimming pool, and a recreation center building. Unique and one of a kind facilities may also be included such as small lakes, natural features, community gardens, or a special recreational facility. Community parks also meet the neighborhood park needs for the residents within a 3/4 mile radius provided reasonable pedestrian access is available.

#### **(3) Regional Parks**

Regional parks serve the entire city, and to some degree, the population outside of the City within an hour driving time of such parks. These parks are normally located to protect or preserve some valuable recreational resource, such as a lake or mountain environments, and the recreational use normally centers around these resources. Other recreational facilities such as picnic areas, trails, nature centers, golf courses, botanical gardens, and playfields may also be accommodated if the site is suitable for such development.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

54

#### **(4) Park Corridors**

These areas are typically linear in character, generally following sensitive environmental corridors such as streams and ridgelines. Park corridors provide linkages between other park areas and serve as pedestrian ways with trails to link various areas within the community. In addition, they provide some recreational opportunities for picnicking and spontaneous recreational activities. There is no applicable standard for park corridors as they are responsive to the location and amount of environmentally sensitive land. Since many of these sensitive areas have already been designated as Open Space under the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, these two programs should be closely coordinated.

#### **(5) Preserves**

Preserves are environmentally sensitive areas which are representative of natural or fragile wildlife or plant habitats. Selected park areas or portions of a park may be designated as natural preserves. The Parks and Recreation Department presently controls preserve areas in the Mountain Parks, Boulder Reservoir, and Sawhill Ponds. These areas receive only minimal maintenance to eliminate hazards, remove trash and debris, and accommodate minimal public access for hiking and nature study. There are no set standards for the provision of preserve areas as they are subject to the availability of significant natural areas.

#### **(6) Athletic Complexes**

Outdoor athletic playfields (i.e., softball, soccer, baseball, rugby, and football fields), because of their intense use, should not be located in neighborhood and most community parks. These playfields should be in athletic complexes away from residential areas and separate from most park areas. Athletic complexes may be compatible with both community parks (provided that additional land is acquired specifically for this purpose) and regional parks. Due to the potential impacts of such athletic facilities, it is usually desirable to locate athletic complexes close to arterial transportation linkages to facilitate ease of access to and from the complex. Multi-purpose facilities with maximum flexibility should be provided whenever possible to accommodate full year use and to provide for possible fluctuations in demand for athletic programs.

Due to fluctuating demand for some athletic activities, establishing a specific standard is not practical. Therefore, the standard for athletic complexes shall be to provide as a minimum the same relative level of service in the future that is demanded by the community at the present time.

#### **(7) Recreation Centers**

Recreation centers provide indoor recreational spaces such as gyms, pools, handball/ racquetball courts, class activity rooms, and multi-purpose activity areas. Recreation centers should be located in community parks because of the complementary recreational opportunities provided by the community park and the ease of access which should be present. Based on current usage of the existing centers, a standard of one recreation center per 25,000 to 30,000 population (exclusive of the University population) is the minimum desirable standard. This standard takes into account the use of the recreation centers by non-resident populations and the availability of the University's Recreation Center for the 24,000 University students, faculty and staff.

Neighborhood and community parks are not normally suitable locations nor do they provide sufficient space. Regional parks and athletic complexes might be able to accommodate some of these facilities. ## **b. Service Standards**

One of the major standards for the Fire Department is to have a response time to the location of an emergency that is normally less than six minutes from the time the call is received by the dispatch center (see Fire Facilities and Response Map, E3-1). Based upon this standard, the Fire Department's level of service is adequate throughout most of the City. However, there are a few areas where service problems occur and/or additional facilities are needed. The approach to providing emergency services is based on the following principles:

### **(1) Shared Responsibility Between Private Sector and City Fire Department**

The City emphasizes private sector self-protection through code regulations and design incentives. Sprinklering is now required by ordinance for many uses.

## **(2) Balance Between Manual Fire Protection and Built-in Fire Protection**

Municipal fire protection requires a balance between fire protection provided by stations, apparatus and personnel and fire protection provided by built-in automatic fire systems. While automatic systems provide a high degree of protection, a city requires a minimum number of stations, apparatus and personnel to protect the city from fires not originating in protected spaces and to provide acceptable levels of service for non-fire emergencies and events.

## **(3) Protection Placed Close to the User**

Small stations are located throughout the City so a quick response time can be achieved. Light and fast vehicles, "attack squad pumpers," are supplied to peripheral stations for quick response time to primarily residential areas.

## **(4) Generalist Theory of Operations**

The Fire Department believes that the apparatus should have diverse supplies and equipment and the fire fighters should be generalists rather than specialists. Due to this philosophy, every piece of fire equipment has fire fighting, medical and rescue supplies and equipment and all fire fighters are trained in numerous skills. Each fire fighter must pass a comprehensive training program which focuses on fire fighting, emergency medical training, hazardous materials response, rescue training for vehicles, ice, water and fire emergencies, and each fire fighter must be a state certified emergency medical technician.

## **(5) Basic Life Level of Emergency Medical Service**

In addition to providing fire suppression, fire prevention and rescue services, the Fire Department is responsible for providing Basic Life Support (BLS) services. The philosophy behind this decision is based upon the facts that the Fire Department often arrives on the scene of an emergency before an ambulance and that life support care within the first few minutes of a serious injury can save many lives. Therefore, the Fire Department is charged with supplying BLS care until the first A-1 Ambulance arrives. Once the ambulance arrives, the patient is given treatment at the paramedic level and taken to a hospital for further medical treatment.

The system of emergency medical care is a multi-tiered system involving the City Fire Department, the A-1 Ambulance service, and the Boulder Community Hospital, each offering a more advanced degree of life support.

## **(6) TeleSquirt Units in High Density, Commercial, and Industrial Areas with Attack Squad Pumpers on the Outskirts**

Stations within high density, industrial, or commercial areas should be equipped with TeleSquirt units, while stations on the periphery of the City, where the majority of the growth is residential, should have residential stations supplied with attack squad pumpers. This philosophy for station type and equipment allows the most appropriate protection based on the land use pattern of the City and provides an excellent back-up system for the stations. Stations with TeleSquirt units can receive fast back up from residential stations, while residential areas can receive TeleSquirt back up to help control large or fast spreading fires.

# 4. POLICE SERVICE PROGRAM SUMMARY

## **a. Background Information**

Police service is defined comprehensively to include all patrol, investigative and support functions of the Police Department. Police service includes a broad range of crime prevention and law enforcement responsibilities.

The Boulder Police Department presently has 123.5 commissioned officers, eighty-one of whom are serving in specialized patrol units in eight patrol districts. Other commissioned officers work in crime prevention, investigation, and other support functions. The Department's 57.5 civilians serve in a variety of responsibilities ranging from investigating traffic accidents to dispatching in communications.

## **b. Service Standards**

Boulder's Police Department has established a baseline ratio of staffing to population (1986/87 population and service levels). The ratio assumes that police service was satisfactory at that time and is an appropriate test of adequacy. The service ratio proposed as the standard is 2.0 per 1,000 inhabitants for total Department staffing or 1.4/1,000 for commissioned personnel and 0.6/1,000 for civilian support staff. Relying on the mobile delivery of service through its patrol officers and appropriate patrol districts, separate sub-stations, each with overhead and management costs, will not be cost-effective for a city the size of Boulder, even at projected buildout. ## 5. TRANSPORTATION MASTER PLAN SUMMARY

### a. Background Information

The Transportation Master Plan reconciles two often conflicting goals. The first goal is to provide mobility and access within the Boulder Valley in a way that is safe and convenient. The second goal is to preserve Boulder's quality of life by minimizing the impacts of auto traffic such as air pollution, congestion, and noise.

The Transportation Master Plan balances these goals by creating a transportation system that provides for not only good auto transportation, but also for alternative forms of transportation such as walking, bicycling, and transit. The Plan proposes strategies to maintain and actually improve the auto system while at the same time creating new opportunities for other modes by completing the bicycle and sidewalk system and providing new types of transit options. The Plan proposes that 15 percent of trips now made by single-occupant autos be shifted to some other form of transportation. The Plan also provides a funding mechanism to maintain and complete the auto, bicycle, and pedestrian systems.

Maintaining this level of service accepts some peak hour congestion in order to minimize transportation facility costs, provide a shift to alternate modes, and minimize negative environmental and community impact costs.

# 6. RAW WATER MASTER PLAN SUMMARY

## a. Background Information

Efficiently planning for future conditions requires an understanding of the water system, and an ability to evaluate the system's performance that is nearly impossible to achieve without the aid of computer-based decision support tools. The purpose of the Raw Water Master Plan is to provide, through compiled information and support tools, such an understanding and the beginning of such evaluations.

The City gets its water from three primary sources: Boulder Creek water rights, Colorado-Big Thompson (CBT) project water deliveries, and Windy Gap project water deliveries.

### (1) Boulder Creek Water Rights

The City's water rights in the Boulder Creek basin include direct use and storage rights on both Middle and North Boulder Creek, and an exchange right. The City's exchange right allows the City to release water into lower Boulder Creek near 75th Street from Boulder and Baseline Reservoirs in exchange for increased diversion at the City's direct use and storage points on Middle and North Boulder Creeks. All of the City's rights are absolute except for conditional storage rights for the Park and Bradley reservoir sites in the North Boulder Creek basin.

### (2) The Colorado-Big Thompson (CBT) Project

Boulder also receives water delivered by the CBT project from the Colorado River into Boulder Reservoir via reservoirs, tunnels and canals. The City owns 20,785 CBT project shares out of a total of 310,000 shares. CBT deliveries to Boulder can only be made from April through October of each year due to winter operating limitations on canals. Boulder uses CBT water for direct treatment at the Boulder Reservoir plant and as a source of exchange water.

### (3) The Windy Gap Project

Boulder's newest water source, the Windy Gap project, is expected to deliver 48,000 acre feet (AF)/year of Colorado River water to municipal and industrial water users on the East Slope via CBT facilities. The City has an allotment contract for one-sixth (8,000 AF/Year) of the project's yield. Windy Gap water is unique among Boulder's supplies because the return flows (wastewater effluent and lawn watering runoff) from this source can be reused. This makes this source relatively more valuable because the reuse rights to the water can be sold to others. Operation of Windy Gap has begun only in token fashion because demands for the water have not yet materialized.

Flows in the Boulder Creek and Colorado River basins are highly variable from year to year. Because of this, deliveries from Boulder's three major water sources into its system also vary. From a water rights viewpoint, the City has sufficient water supply holdings to meet its ultimate municipal water needs, given the extent of the current BVCP planning area.

Boulder's water supply system includes several storage, conveyance and treatment facilities. The City owns approximately 7,200 AF of storage rights in its North Boulder Creek watershed, and has contracted for rights to 8,000 AF and 8,500 AF in Barker and Boulder Reservoirs, respectively. Boulder's two water treatment facilities include the Betasso plant at 46.5 million gallons per day (MGD) capacity and the Boulder Reservoir plant at 10 MGD.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

70

The Middle Boulder Creek facilities include storage rights at Barker Reservoir. Water is transported by pipeline to Kossler Reservoir, and then by pipeline to the Public Service Company hydroelectric plant (5 miles west of Boulder in Boulder Canyon). A bypass pipeline at the Public Service Company hydroelectric plant delivers water to the Betasso Water Treatment Plant.

The North Boulder Creek facilities include eleven mountain dams and reservoirs within the City's watershed. Water is transported via open channel and the Silver Lake Pipeline to Lakewood Reservoir, a small reservoir north of Nederland adjacent to the Peak-to-Peak Highway. From Lakewood Reservoir water is transported via the 9-mile long Lakewood Pipeline to the Betasso Water Treatment Plant.

In setting service standards with respect to reliability of water supply and treatment, the City attempts to achieve a balance between the costs of increased reliability and the consequences of temporary water supply shortages. Water provided by the City currently serves a variety of purposes ranging from those uses which require an assured supply such as drinking water and firefighting, to those uses which can tolerate occasional restrictions to supply such as lawn irrigation and car washing. It is recognized that no municipal water supply can ever be 100% reliable against all risk factors and that the economic and environmental opportunity costs of reducing the risks of occasional water shortages are significant.

Setting of service standards is a political decision that should take into account both economic and non-economic factors. The City's standards will be reviewed periodically as additional information is gained concerning the nature of water supply and demand. Based upon currently available information, the following standards apply:

- (a) For those water uses deemed essential to the maintenance of basic public health, safety and welfare such as indoor domestic, commercial, and industrial uses and firefighting uses, the City shall make every effort to ensure reliability of supply against droughts with occurrence intervals of up to 1000 years.
- (b) For that increment of water use needed to provide continued viability of outdoor lawns and gardens, the City shall make every effort to ensure reliability of supply against droughts with occurrence intervals of up to 100 years.
- (c) For that increment of water needed to fully satisfy all municipal water needs, the City shall make every effort to ensure reliability of supply against droughts with occurrence intervals of up to 20 years.

The raw water master plan commits the City to multiple-purpose use of its raw water. In addition to residential and commercial consumptive uses, the City's raw water supply will be used for such diverse uses as maintaining or enhancing stream habitat, facilitating water-based recreation, and preserving irrigated agriculture and open space.

The master plan also commits the City to maintaining a diversity of water supply sources (both east and west slope sources) in order to hedge against droughts and increase water supply reliability. At the same time, the City is pursuing a strategy of selling a portion of its Windy Gap water supply and replacing it by acquiring water rights in the Boulder Creek watershed. This strategy will provide a cheaper water supply source and will provide more control to the City over its local water supply. At the same time, this strategy will provide an interruptable water supply for irrigated agriculture and other non-utility purposes.

## 7. TREATED WATER FACILITIES MASTER PLAN SUMMARY

### a. Background Information

The treated water facilities include the water treatment plants, covered reservoirs, and the transmission/distribution lines (water mains).

The City of Boulder's Water Utility operates two water treatment plants - the Betasso Water Treatment Plant and the Boulder Reservoir Water Treatment Plant. Mountain water from the City's watershed and Barker Lake is treated at Betasso, which has a capacity of 46 million gallons per day (MGD). Water available from the Colorado-Big Thompson Project and the Windy Gap Project is treated at Boulder Reservoir, which has a capacity of 10 mgd. Site constraints at the mountainous Betasso plant preclude the realistic expansion of this facility, so all future water treatment expansion will occur at Boulder Reservoir.

The City operates five covered reservoirs, ranging in capacity from 2.0 million gallons(MG) to 9.5 MG, with a total storage capacity of 33.9 MG. There is also a clearwell at each of the treatment plants.

Due to the geographic location of Boulder, the Water Utility serves low areas adjacent to Boulder Creek in the eastern section to high areas against the mountains in the western section. To adequately serve this wide variation in ground elevation, the water distribution system is divided into four pressure zone areas.

There are 11 pressure regulating stations in the system that regulate pressure and flow between pressure zones or into storage reservoirs.

There are four booster pumping stations in the system, as well as a high service pumping station at the Boulder Reservoir Water Treatment Plant. These booster pumping stations are able to pump water from a lower elevation pressure zone into a higher elevation pressure zone.

The City operates four hydroelectric facilities on treated water transmission lines (one raw water transmission line and two more are in the planning stages). The hydroelectric facilities use the power generated from the elevation differential to produce electricity, which is then sold to Public Service Company.

# 8. WASTEWATER TREATMENT MASTER PLAN SUMMARY

## a. Background Information

Wastewater quantity and characteristics are dependent upon population levels, land use and raw water characteristics, and these are monitored on a regular basis. The Wastewater Treatment Facilities Plan, completed in 1986, was based on population figures provided by the Planning Department.

All domestic and industrial wastewater generated within the City of Boulder is processed at the City's 75th Street Wastewater Treatment Plant. Septic wastes, hauled to the facility by private haulers, are also processed at the facility. Treated liquid effluent is discharged to Boulder Creek, and anaerobically digested sludge generated at the wastewater treatment plant is hauled away and is surface applied to local farmland and open space. Currently, an average of 13.5 million gallons a day are processed at the plant.

The Facilities Plan evaluated the existing wastewater treatment process (headworks, primary sedimentation, trickling filters, secondary clarification, disinfection, and dechlorination). Present wastewater flows were in excess of 80 percent of the design capacity of the liquid treatment system, and so an expansion was necessary. In addition, the 1987 National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit imposed more stringent effluent standards than previously in effect, as well as stricter standards for the removal of ammonia from the discharge flow. As a result, some new processes are needed to be added to the treatment plant.

An additional study evaluated existing sludge treatment and disposal facilities, and assessed alternative approaches to sludge disposal to meet the future needs to the year 2010. The conclusion of the study was that the approach of sludge stabilization through anaerobic digestion, followed by disposal through land application to agricultural sites or open space, was the most beneficial and cost-effective approach. A series of public meetings and City Council sessions also supported this approach.

## b. Service Standards

The wastewater treatment plant is currently undergoing major improvements to meet effluent standards as required by the City's 1987 NPDES permit. In compliance with U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) guidelines, the City has completed plans for both the sewage sludge (solids) and liquid stream (effluent to Boulder Creek).

State stream classifications set the amount of various chemical substances allowed in streams, and discharge permits are issued to assure compliance by point sources such as wastewater treatment plants. Boulder's treatment plant is required to be in compliance with state standards, specifically those for un-ionized ammonia, by November 1, 1989. To this end, the City has conducted extensive facilities planning and is currently implementing design and construction of an approved ammonia reduction facility at its 75th Street plant.

75

### **c. Future Service Projections and Programs**

The wastewater treatment plant is currently undergoing a major improvement to accommodate the sludge management process. Improvements in excess of \$22 million will be completed by the end of 1989. The next level of substantial improvement will not be needed until the population level approaches 131,000 (approximately year 2010), unless substantial changes are made to Federal/State regulations, or the City's NPDES Permit. The improvements being completed include the following: 1) modification of existing facilities, 2) incorporation of the trickling filter/solids contact process, and 3) addition of nitrification trickling filters for ammonia removal. The facilities have been designed to accommodate future expansion or new processes, when required.

Of the planned facilities improvements, approximately \$4 million will be spent to further accommodate sludge treatment and disposal. The improvements being completed include: 1) a predigestion gravity thickener, 2) a digested sludge transfer pump station, 3) thickening centrifuges, 4) some polymer feed equipment, 5) a truck fill pump station, 6) a thickened sludge storage tank, and 7) a supernate/centrate equalization tank. Improvements to enhance land application include the acquisition of a nurse truck and a wide-tire applicator vehicle.

In the process of evaluating ammonia in Boulder Creek, the City conducted a "Use-Attainability" study to assess the relative impact of ammonia on fish life. It was found that the lower sections of Boulder Creek, specifically those between one half mile east of the 75th Street plant and the Coal Creek drainage, were highly disturbed by land use practices. Agriculture, livestock grazing, and gravel mining in particular had resulted in severe disruption of the natural stream bed and related aquatic zone, which had reduced the stream's ability to support aquatic life. In studying remedial measures required to restore the ecosystem, consultants found that required improvements coincided with those which would reduce the impact of un-ionized ammonia within the Creek.

Creek restoration would include a variety of management practices to re-establish a riparian corridor paralleling the stream, including fencing, stream bank terracing, stabilization of eroded banks, and revegetation. A thalweg, or deep central channel, would also be constructed to accommodate low flow. Reaeration structures would increase oxygen content of the water.

Creek restoration would decrease the wide swings of temperature and pH in the water, thus reducing the conversion of ammonia to its toxic un-ionized form. It would also restore the Creek's ability to support a variety of fish, as well as a diverse riparian ecosystem.

Modeling of the proposed process will be completed and, if shown to be viable, a program will be developed. Non-point, or runoff sources of pollutants in the lower creek system have also been identified as a problem. To reduce these sources, the feasibility of retention and filtration of return waters through the creation of wetlands is being considered. The City has obtained federal funds to assist with the control of non-point and agricultural source runoff.

Stream enhancement can potentially extend the useful life of the improvements presently being made to the wastewater treatment plant. The total project cost is estimated to be between \$5.3 and \$6.7 million. Phasing has not yet been determined.

# 9. COMPREHENSIVE DRAINAGE UTILITY MASTER PLAN SUMMARY

## a. Background Information

The City of Boulder established a Storm Water and Flood Management Utility in 1973 to focus responsibility and accountability for storm water management and flood control programs and provide a means of funding. Monthly service charges were instituted in 1974 and have provided the primary sources of funding since that time, enabling the City to plan, regulate, maintain, and improve the drainage system.

Over the years maintenance efforts have gradually increased with funding and knowledge of system needs. A substantial amount of time and money has been invested in storm water and flood management planning. Drainage policies and plans have been completed for Boulder Creek, major tributaries, the Boulder Gunbarrel area, and the storm water collection system. The City is an active member in the Urban Drainage and Flood Control District as well as participating in the Federal National Flood Insurance Program. Major capital improvements have been made to a few of the drainage systems, but much more remains to be accomplished.

In addition, the Federal Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) is now preparing rules and policies for the issuance of storm water discharge permits intended to reduce the environmental impacts of storm water discharges on receiving waters. These rules are expected to place new obligations on the City.

The completion of the plans, each suggesting needed projects, and impending EPA rules emphasizing water quality, led the Storm Water and Flood Management Utility to prepare the Comprehensive Drainage Utility Master Plan (CDUMP). Through CDUMP, drainage projects are prioritized, a funding source is defined, programs for water quality and maintenance are outlined, and administration and staffing are proposed. CDUMP was adopted by the City Council in 1989.

## b. Service Standards

A reduction to life-safety hazards and property damage, as well as improving water quality, are the main purposes of CDUMP. The City will regulate, to some degree, the use and construction within the area which could be expected to be inundated by a 100-year flood. This floodplain, for purposes of regulation, as well as for determining capital project priority, is divided into the "flood storage area," the "flood conveyance zone" and the "high hazard area."

For purposes of designing capital projects, the City will apply an additional cost-benefit standard. For example, the City may consider improvements to less than a 100-year standard in some cases depending upon the cost of the project compared to the risk to lives or property.

## c. Future Service Projections and Programs

The City of Boulder and the County of Boulder, with the Urban Drainage and Flood Control District, have completed a number of drainage reports for creeks, tributaries, and storm water collection systems within the Boulder Valley. (1) The program to manage the City's floodplain has four elements: 1) a strong ordinance regulating use and construction; 2) the promotion of federally-subsidized flood insurance; 3) a flood hazard early warning system for South Boulder and Boulder Creeks; and 4) the maintenance of existing drainageway capacities, modification of man-made obstructions, and restoration of historic drainage courses.

## 10. LIBRARY PROGRAM SUMMARY

### a. Background Information

The 4,000 square foot Carnegie Library was dedicated in 1907 and served as the Boulder community's sole library until 1961. Today, the restored Carnegie Branch is home for the Local History Resource Center. It houses documents, books, recorded oral histories, and photographs related to Boulder's history.

Following a positive bond issue vote, the present 23,000 square foot north building of the Main Library opened in 1961. Its opening marked the end of an era when the library was simply a home for books. From that time forward, the library has been a focus for many cultural, artistic, and community activities.

In 1968, the 3,800 square foot George F. Reynolds Branch was opened to serve the southern sector of the community and in 1974, a 15,000 square foot addition to the Main Library was dedicated.

A computer system was installed in 1979 to automate library functions for more efficient handling of materials. New software was purchased in 1986. The public access catalog became available to users both in the library, and later to those with computers at home.

The presence of a premier state university, co-location of federal research facilities, and high technology rather than smokestack industries yields a greater demand for quality library service than is found in many other communities of similar size.

The Boulder Public Library (BPL) has been undergoing a radical transformation, rapidly evolving from traditional book-oriented services into those typical of a modern, urban information center, including cable television production. The challenge in attempting to keep pace with social and technological changes is enormous. Further, over the last five years there has been an approximate ten percent per year average increase in circulation. This growth rate is significantly higher than the national average for circulation growth and well in excess of area population growth during the same period. This phenomenon has planning implications that relate to the projected demand for and sizing of facilities and services throughout the next planning period.

The BPL system has developed a wide range of programs well suited to the diverse requirements of its unique and rapidly expanding service population. Highlights of the existing program include: provision of a full range of information formats; a strong children's collection; a municipal-access cable channel; unique Boulder and Colorado history collections; a volunteer network that contributes more than 22,800 hours of support per year; and an innovative range of special services serving the homebound, the visual and hearing impaired, and the undereducated.

Fulfilling the role of a regional public library, the Boulder Public Library provides reciprocal use to residents of other incorporated cities in the County, unincorporated Boulder County, and the larger region. In 1979, an agreement was adopted between the City and the County. ## **b. Service Standard**

The basic BPL philosophy of service is to further the personal development of Boulder's citizens by meeting their informational, educational, and intellectual needs. This purpose is based on a recognition of the worth of each person, the need for human dignity, and the individual's capacity for self-improvement. It also recognizes the community's need for a well-informed citizenry.

- improve the basis for public and private decisions

Because of the diversity of needs and the differences in the composition of widely varying communities, library guidelines are usually viewed as models to be achieved or followed rather than codes to be enforced.

## 11. HOUSING PROGRAM SUMMARY

### a. Background Information

Beginning in the 1960s and through the 1980s, a variety of private and public nonprofit agencies began to explore options for providing affordable housing for low income residents of the City. These efforts were successful in producing 1,888 subsidized units, of which approximately 350 are for elderly and handicapped persons. Most of these units were the direct result of funding from the federal government through the Department of Housing and Urban Development.

Although this is a significant number of assisted units, there is evidence that the need for affordable housing continues. For the past five years, the Housing Authority has had a consistent waiting list of up to 500 households seeking assistance. The Boulder County Human Service Needs Assessment for 1989 found lack of affordable housing to be among the top four problems experienced by Boulder residents. There are an increasing number of homeless, which brings with it the need to create transitional housing as well as emergency shelters. The City is anticipating the potential displacement of up to 150 families living at San Juan del Centro. San Juan del Centro was originally built as a low-income housing project but has been sold to a private owner and may be redeveloped.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

80

Further, federal subsidies for housing are dwindling. An effective housing program, one that continues to provide housing for the needy at the present level, will need to be innovative in creating a variety of housing solutions and funding approaches.

### **b. Service Standards**

Using the 1987 Citizen survey, it is estimated that 9.37% of Boulder's two-or-more person households earn less than \$16,000 per year, thus qualifying for some form of housing assistance.

Presently, 3.8% of Boulder's current housing stock is subsidized, while communities such as Denver have up to 8% of their housing stock subsidized. While there is no nationally accepted standard for the amount of subsidized housing in a given community, it should reflect the number of low income households in the community. The Housing Authority believes an appropriate goal for housing Boulder's lower income population is to have five (5%) percent of the housing stock assisted in some manner.

### **c. Future Service Projections and Programs**

Housing is one area which cuts across all economic levels — it is something everyone in the community needs, although with different types and affordability requirements. This drives a housing program which is comprehensive, although targeted primarily to low income households.

Future programs should include rental housing for very low income, first time homebuyers assistance, and foreclosure assistance and prevention. Another important component of a comprehensive housing program is rehabilitation of existing units.

Perhaps the most important future need, however, is to develop sustainable housing programs which can function without much federal assistance. New financing techniques, ways to reduce the cost of existing programs, and collaboration with private and quasi-public groups, as well as reducing any duplication of services by public groups, are to be implemented.

Consideration should be given to the displacement of low income persons when their housing is redeveloped by the private sector. The impacts of displacement should be minimized by requiring relocation assistance, which can take the form of a rent subsidy, moving assistance, payment of utility hook ups, and referral to affordable housing.

## 1. SIGNIFICANT MINERAL RESOURCES

### a. Background

In June of 1973, the Colorado Legislature amended portions of Title 34 of the Colorado Statutes by enacting House Bill 1529. Part 3 of Article I states the legislative intent of the bill: "The General Assembly hereby declares that: a) The State's commercial mineral deposits are essential to the State's economy; b) The populous counties of the State face a critical shortage of such deposits; c) Such deposits should be extracted according to a rational plan, calculated to avoid waste of such deposits and cause the least practicable disruption to the ecology and quality of life for the citizens of the populous counties of the State."

Colorado Statutes define "commercial mineral deposit" as "a natural mineral deposit of limestone used for construction purposes, coal, sand, gravel and quarry aggregate, for which extraction by an extractor is or will be commercially feasible and regarding which it can be demonstrated by geologic, mineralogical, or other scientific data that such deposit has significant or strategic value to the area, state or nation."

House Bill 1529 instructed the planning commission for each city and county, city or town, within each populous county of the State, to conduct a study of the commercial mineral deposits located within their jurisdictions and develop a master plan for their extraction. Pursuant to the legislation, the City of Boulder prepared a Master Plan for Mineral Extraction in 1977. Commercial mineral deposits were inventoried throughout the Boulder Valley. The inventory was then combined with planning criteria, resulting in the final master plan.

### b. Limestone

Within the Boulder Valley, limestone has only been mined and kilned for agricultural purposes. The intent of the State legislation was to protect deposits of limestone used as concrete aggregate. A master plan for limestone extraction, therefore, was not prepared for the Boulder Valley.

### c. Coal

The mining of sub-bituminous coal has taken place in the area surrounding Marshall since the mid-1880s. Marshall lies at the southwestern terminus of the larger Boulder-Weld County coal field. Relatively low energy-yielding coals have been extracted by small underground mines as recently as 1946.

The Master Plan for Mineral Extraction found no coal extraction occurring in the Boulder Valley and also came to the conclusion that the coals of the Boulder Valley were not commercially extractable in 1977 or within a fifteen year time period.

## **2. GEOLOGICAL DEVELOPMENT CONSTRAINTS**

### **a. Background**

Several constraints to development due to geological hazards exist in the Boulder Valley. In 1977 the City of Boulder Urban Geology program identified hazards which could represent a potential threat to property and life safety.

### **b. Potential Mass Movement Hazard**

#### **(1) Material Movement**

Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of material movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.

#### **(2) Slope Instability**

Land slides are evident throughout Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure if modified by development.

#### **(3) Alluvial Fans**

Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, evident along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to twenty feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating event, such as the Big Thompson flood of August 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan.

#### **(4) Earth Flows**

Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Examination of the deposits on the sediment surface bordering the foothills has discovered boulders exceeding twenty-five feet in diameter.

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to twenty-nine feet in diameter in silt and clay-sized mud.

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation. Saturated accumulations of debris, along and within the foothills, liquify and flow down stream channels and out across the mesa and valley surfaces. An earth flow would be extremely destructive in developed areas.

#### **(5) Slope Debris**

The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

89

## **(6) Swell Potential**

**Swelling soils** are soils or soft bedrock which increase in volume as they get wet and shrink as they dry out. They are also commonly known as bentonite, expansive, or montmorillonitic soils.

**Swelling soils** contain a high percentage of certain kinds of clay particles which are capable of absorbing large quantities of water. Soil volume may expand 10 percent or more as the clay becomes wet. The powerful force of expansion is capable of exerting pressures of 20,000 pounds per square foot or greater on foundations, slabs, or other confining structures. Subsurface Colorado swelling soils tend to remain at a constant moisture content in their natural state and are usually relatively dry at the outset of construction disturbance. Exposure to natural or man-caused water sources during or after development results in swelling. In many instances, the soils do not regain their original dryness after construction, but remain somewhat moist and expanded due to the changed environment.

The Colorado Geological Survey estimates that damage to taxpayer financed structures in Colorado annually exceeds \$16 million. In the Boulder area, damage to private structures is estimated at over \$200,000 per year. More homes in Boulder are affected by expansive soils than by locations in the floodplain.

## **(7) Consolidation Potential**

Consolidation of soils occur when relatively low density materials shrink in volume when they become wet or are subjected to great weight from buildings or road fill or other construction activity. These are also known as collapsing and settling soils.

**Collapsing and settling soils** have considerable strength when dry and generally are not a problem to structures and improvements. When they become wet, they are subject to rapid collapse and can be reduced in volume as much as 10 to 15 percent. Surface ground displacement of several feet can result.

Human activities, such as irrigation, installation of utilities, impoundment of water and blockage of natural drainageways, as well as construction of buildings, result in the collapse and settlement of these soils. This can result in damage to private property and public improvements. Similar processes frequently affect old landfills or poorly placed earth fills.

## **(8) Potential Mine-Related Subsidence**

Ground subsidence due to the collapse of coal mine voids has occurred in the Boulder Valley. Potentially devastating effects of collapse are graphically evident on the City-owned Richardson parcel northeast of the intersection of Cherryvale Road and Marshall Road. The surrounding lands are underlain by the old Eagle Mine, which closed in 1936. Linear trench-like depressions are evident where collapse has occurred subsequent to mine abandonment. A large percentage of the land in the Davidson Mesa area has been undermined.

### **3. UNIQUE GEOLOGICAL FEATURES**

The following geological features are significant because they demonstrate the geological history of the Boulder Valley:

**1. The Flatirons**

The large, flat triangular slabs exposed on the east side of Green Mountain and Bear Peak. Nicknamed the "Flatirons" by early settlers for their resemblance to old-fashioned irons. Rock is the Fountain Formation, a red arkosic sandstone and conglomerate dated at 280 million years. Formed from eroded material from a long-gone ancestral Rocky Mountain range that existed 30 to 50 miles west of the Boulder area. Initially deposited as nearly flat lying layers, the rocks have been tilted to their present position by more recent uplift of the present Rocky Mountain range.

**2. Contact Corner**

Located on the last hairpin curve on Flagstaff Road before reaching the turnoff that leads to Flagstaff summit. Here, the sedimentary Fountain Formation (dated at 280 million years) lies directly on the much older precambrian igneous (dated at 1.7 billion years). An area where two different kinds of rock come together is called a "contact" by geologists.

**3. Flagstaff Sill**

Approximately 64 million years ago during a phase of uplift of the present Rocky Mountain range, a layer of molten igneous rock was placed parallel to the beds of the surrounding sedimentary rock in parts of the Boulder area. This igneous sill can be seen in the vicinity of Crown Rock on Flagstaff Mountain, where it has been intruded into the much older Fountain Formation.

**4. Red Rocks**

Spectacular slabs of once nearly flat-lying sedimentary Fountain Formation which have been tilted to a nearly vertical position by the uplift of the Rocky Mountains.

**5. Echo Rocks**

The resistant ridge forming Echo Rocks is made up of the Dakota sandstone - a rock laid down when a huge inland sea covered the area nearly 135 million years ago. Extensive beaches formed from an almost pure quartz sand, and later hardened into the Dakota Formation. Ripple marks can be seen in the sandstone near the water tank west of the National Center for Atmospheric Research. The Dakota Ridge can be traced from north of Colorado Springs to the Wyoming border.

**6. Eldorado State Park**

The park is the site of a spectacular and sheer canyon that South Boulder Creek has carved into the sedimentary rock layers as well as into the harder and much older (1.7 billion year old) precambrian rock. It is one of the most popular rock climbing areas in the country. In addition, the park contains a very pure outcrop of a precambrian metaquartzite rock that is embedded in surrounding metamorphic rocks. This rock is unusual in that it formed from a very quartz-rich pure sand, as compared to the surrounding metamorphic gneiss and schist, which form from materials higher in mud and clay content. Its origin is not completely understood.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

92

- 7. Marshall Mesa and
- 8. Davidson Mesa

Probably a single mesa, Marshall Mesa represents several geological ages and rock types, and contains a number of beautifully preserved plant fossils and “petrified wood” in underlying formations. In addition, the Laramie Formation exhibits the “turtlebacks” described in 9. below. The Laramie Formation has been extensively mined in the Marshall Mesa area for coal, and numerous abandoned mine shafts can still be seen here. Davidson Mesa in the site of the Highway 36 (Boulder-Denver Turnpike) scenic overlook and provides a spectacular view of the Boulder Valley and mountains to the west.

- 9. White Rocks

A 60 acre protected natural area of outcropping sandstone cliffs. White Rocks is made up of the Fox Hills Sandstone and Laramie Formation, formed when a huge inland sea began to retreat from this area approximately 75 million years ago. Beach sands and a swampy landscape were left behind, and later hardened to form these formations. The sandstone has unique joints (breaks in the rock) that form a polygon pattern resembling turtle backs. White Rocks is not open to the general public.

- 10. Richardson 1, City of Boulder Open Space and
- 11. Private Area Near Baseline Reservoir

These areas feature the “turtlebacks” as described above. Again, the Laramie and Fox Hills Formations outcrop in these areas, and the rock in places resembles the pattern seen on the shells of turtles. This probably resulted from the expansion and contraction of the relatively high clay content found in the sandstone.

- 12. Boulder Diatreme

A deeply eroded conduit of a former volcano that contains fragments of Silurian fossils dating from approximately 380 million years ago, and the only local area where these have been found. Also contains some microscopic diamonds.

- 13. Valmont Dike

Valmont Dike is approximately 60 feet wide and nearly 3 miles long, and is made up of a nearly vertical sheet of a hard igneous rock called basalt. The dike formed nearly 65 million years ago when hot molten material beneath the earth's surface pushed its way upward into a crack in the overlying Pierre Shale. Since the basalt is harder than the surrounding Pierre Shale, it has not been eroded as rapidly as the shale and stands up in vertical relief. The hardest part of the dike rises 200 feet above the floodplain.

- 14. Six-Mile Fold

The area contains interesting geological structural features that include anticlines (upward folds of sedimentary rock), synclines (downward folds of sedimentary rock), drag folds (folding of rock layers from pressures of movement of adjacent rock layers), faults (breaks in rock along which movement has occurred) slickensides (polished, scratched surfaces on rock that result from friction along a fault plane), and oyster fossils and clam fossils up to ten inches in diameter. Fascinating and complex area.

# 4. WETLANDS

## a. Background

The City of Boulder, Colorado and the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) have jointly funded a project to delineate and evaluate wetlands in the Boulder Valley. The purpose of the joint City/EPA project was to identify, map, describe and evaluate the functions being performed by wetlands occurring within the City limits and within the area planned for incorporation into the City within 15 years.

Wetlands are defined as those areas that are inundated or saturated by surface or groundwater enough to support vegetation typically adapted to wet soil conditions. Wetlands are now known to be critical in the function of: (a) groundwater recharge; (b) groundwater discharge; (c) flood control; (d) shoreline anchoring; (e) sediment trapping; (f) nutrient retention; (g) food chain support; (h) fish and wildlife habitat; and (i) active and passive human recreation. Not all wetlands provide all of these functions, and most provide only a few to a very high degree. Each wetland area identified in the Boulder Service Area has been evaluated as to the degree each wetland area performs each functional value.

Sixty wetlands were identified in Areas I and II of the Boulder Valley. These wetlands were evaluated as to origin. They were divided into three categories: 1) natural; 2) created by agricultural practices and gravel mining; or 3) created by urban runoff. Natural wetlands are 36% of the total wetland acreage identified, while agricultural and gravel mining wetlands are 56% of the area. The remaining 8% of wetland area originated from urban runoff.

Most natural wetlands occur along the natural stream systems in oxbows, along shores, and in overflow areas. Many of these streams once had wide floodplains and meandered extensively. Abandoned or seldom used channels usually supported wetlands. However, most of these backwater areas, particularly along Boulder Creek, have been filled and the wetlands lost. Only one natural pond, Sombrero Marsh, occurs in the study area.

Almost two-thirds of the wetlands identified in the study are apparently a result of human activity, particularly agriculture and gravel mining. Some of the wetlands created by leaking reservoirs and ditches are among the largest and most biologically diverse wetlands in the study area.

It may seem that because more than one half of the study area's wetlands have been produced by human activity that more wetlands occur in the Boulder Valley now than prior to Euro-American settlement, but this is probably not true. Pre-settlement wetlands are probably found along streams and rivers and in landscape depressions. All streams and rivers in the study area have been partially or totally channelized and their flows are now regulated.

Agricultural and urban development has clustered along Boulder Creek throughout the Boulder Valley and most natural wetlands have been destroyed. Spring runoff which once inundated floodplains at the mountain front and on the great plains is retained in mountain reservoirs for municipal uses or diverted in ditches and canals for agricultural use. Seeps from ditches and reservoirs and extensive flood irrigation have created high water tables and wetlands in many parts of the Boulder Valley that were dry uplands in pre-settlement times. Municipal water ends up in sewer lines and is transported to wastewater treatment plants and as street runoff. Urban wetlands are the result of the redistribution of street runoff, much of which has been diverted from streams.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. III

94

The diversion of water for municipal and agricultural uses has created completely different patterns of water distribution, resulting in wetlands in different locations and of different types than occurred naturally in the Boulder Valley. Most likely, the area of wetlands supported now is similar to that in pre-settlement conditions, however, the types and functions of wetlands are probably very different. In pre-settlement conditions the majority of wetlands occurred in floodplains while now the majority are located away from floodplains. Important wetland functions that have been lost due to this change are flood storage and desynchronization, native fish and wildlife habitat, water quality control, food chain support, and shoreline anchoring. Two functional values that have increased due to this change are increased habitat for waterfowl (created by open reservoirs) and active recreation.

Information on the wetlands of the Boulder Valley has been taken from the report, "Advanced Identification of Wetlands in the City of Boulder Comprehensive Planning Area" by Dr. David J. Cooper.

## **5. BOULDER CREEK, ITS TRIBUTARIES AND FLOODPLAINS**

### **a. Background**

The streams and creeks within the City of Boulder were formed by nature to provide channels for flood flows resulting from heavy snow melt and rainfall. Boulder's flood problems began with the initial development of the community adjacent to Boulder Creek. This development established a pattern for similar residential, commercial, or industrial construction along other portions of Boulder Creek and within other flood areas in the Boulder Valley. As a result of private and public development over the floodplain, flood heights and velocities have increased and the potential for economic loss and concern for personal safety has increased.

Boulder is particularly vulnerable to flood damage because of its unique geographic location. In addition, because of its proximity to the mouth of Boulder Canyon, the Central Area of the city is highly susceptible to the particular hazards of flash floods.

The conveyance zone is the area within the floodplain required for the passage or conveyance of the one-hundred year flood where an increase in flood depths of one-half foot will result by encroachment from the edges of the floodplain. ## **6. AGRICULTURAL LANDS**

### **a. Background**

Significant agricultural land consists of three subparts: agricultural land of national importance; agricultural land of statewide importance, and agricultural land of local importance. These classifications are based on the Colorado Important Farmland Inventory, published in October, 1982 by the Soil Conservation Service, U.S. Department of Agriculture.

Public Law 95-87 charged the Soil Conservation Service with identifying and locating Prime and Unique Farmlands throughout the Country. Prime farmland is available land that has the best combination of physical and chemical characteristics for producing food, feed, forage, fiber, and oil seed. It must have the soil quality, growing season, and moisture supply needed to economically produce sustained high yields of crops when treated and managed according to acceptable farming methods.

Unique farmland is land other than prime farmland that is used for the production of specific high value food and fiber crops. There are no unique farmlands of national importance in Boulder County. In addition, the law encouraged the identification of agricultural lands of national importance including requirements dealing with irrigation, length of growing season, depth of water table, specific drainage characteristics, soil profiles and slope.

The identification of agricultural lands of statewide importance was completed by the State Experiment Station in cooperation with the Colorado Department of Agriculture and Department of Natural Resources-Colorado State Soil Conservation Board. Lands of statewide importance are those agricultural lands which could not meet the national criteria for prime farmland but are still of statewide importance for the production of food, feed, fiber, forage and oil seed crops. These fall into one of three categories: irrigated lands, irrigated lands with inadequate water supply, or high potential dry cropland.

The three main types of agriculture in the County serve as the categories for lands of local importance: irrigated cropland, dry cropland, and rangeland. These lands were identified by Boulder County in cooperation with the Boulder Soil Conservation District and are those lands which, based on their current and historic land use and soil properties are the County's most important agricultural lands.

Information on significant agricultural land in the Boulder Valley is taken from the Environmental Resources Element of the Boulder County Comprehensive Plan, 1983.

The City and the University have entered into a shared use and funding agreement for performing arts facilities on the campus. These agreements have signalled an era of cooperation and represent an acknowledgement of mutual reliance.

### **b. University Hill Business District**

The business district that most serves the University students has been upgraded. The City and area property owners have expanded the number of parking spaces, added trees and benches, and improved the appearance and functioning of the area.

## **4. OTHER AREAS OF SPECIAL INTEREST**

### **a. Boulder Creek**

Boulder Creek is recognized as an environmentally sensitive area which is dramatic as a scenic open space element, now preserved and enhanced through public investment. Following a plan adopted for the Creek in 1983, a continuous 4.7 mile off-street bicycle and pedestrian trail extending from Eben G. Fine Park at the mouth of Boulder Canyon to 55th Street has been built. Obstacles to rafting and kayaking have been removed and fish habitat improvements are in place. The riparian areas along the Creek's banks have been restored in some reaches, and several pocket parks and improvements to existing parks have been completed. Future investments will focus on maintenance, safety improvements, and connections to trails now proposed for the tributaries to Boulder Creek.

### **b. The Pearl Street Corridor**

The Pearl Street Corridor links the high level activity center of the Downtown Business District with the Boulder Valley Regional Center. It is presently a transitional area where the zoning has ranged from industrial to high density residential to the current mixed-use zone.

The long range vision for the Pearl Street Corridor is as an interesting and varied shopping area which provides opportunities for urban-density housing, with some office space as well. The scale of new buildings will be comparable to the traditional buildings in downtown Boulder. Understanding that Pearl Street will become an important automobile and mass transit link between the two activity centers, the planning challenge is to create and strengthen the pedestrian element along the corridor. To this end, in 1984, the mixed-use zone (MU-X) was adopted by Council specifically for this area.

Opportunities for a shuttle or other connector along Pearl are obvious and should be explored as a part of transit innovations for the Central Area. Redevelopment of the area shall be a City priority.

### **c. Medical Facilities Area**

Community Hospital, at Balsam and Broadway, and the nearby related facilities, provide specialized and significant Boulder Valley medical facilities and services. As a result of the unusual impact of the hospital on adjacent residential areas, special considerations are required. Adequate public transportation is a continuing priority. As expansions to the hospital or other medical facilities are planned, neighborhood transportation and visual impacts must be considered and negative effects mitigated where possible.

### **d. High Density Overlay Zone**

In the older high density residential areas that surround the Downtown Business District, the Boulder Valley Regional Center, and the University, the City recognizes the dual responsibility of permitting selective redevelopment to occur while, at the same time, utilizing sufficient control in order to protect the basic character and integrity of these neighborhoods. To this end, the City Council has adopted the High Density Overlay Zone. In the Central Area high density residential zones, a review for design compatibility is mandatory if a development is to reach full allowed density. Special concern for parking needs, for the scale and detail of new development and for input from neighborhood residents is built into the requirements of this zone.

Transportation improvement districts, co-funded by the City and area property owners have been completed for the high density zones of Goss-Grove, Whittier, West Pearl and South Whittier. In addition to paving and sidewalk reconstruction, these projects have included street closures and redesign to slow or inhibit through traffic, pocket parks, landscaped medians, bicycle lanes, bus pull-outs and shelters, and neighborhood identification signs. These capital projects have served to beautify and improve the living environment for residents of these areas.

Additionally, rehabilitation and energy conservation loans and grants have been provided by a combination of federal funds, city funds, and funds from a consortium of local banks.

### **e. Older, Stable Residential Neighborhoods**

The remaining downtown residential neighborhoods are considered stable residential environments, realizing some occasional redevelopment and scattered additional new units are likely. The City is committed to maintaining this stability and working with area residents to design programs to improve livability.

Some of the downtown neighborhoods have the potential to be historic districts, and each has individual homes of landmark quality. The downtown neighborhoods have been surveyed through a grant from the Colorado Historical Society and potential district boundaries and landmark structures have been identified.

In 1984, the Mapleton Hill Historic District was established. It is the largest historic district in the City and includes more than 200 structures.

A residential parking permit program has been adopted by Council for use in residential areas impacted by parking from activity centers. Neighborhoods in proximity to the University, Boulder High School, and the Downtown may find the program helpful.

Transportation improvement districts, identified as associated with the High Density Overlay Zone, have also served to improve the aesthetics and functioning of the street system in these neighborhoods. Improvements to the North Mapleton Hill neighborhood are planned for the near future.

# H. CITY OF BOULDER CAPITAL IMPROVEMENTS PROGRAM

## 1. DESCRIPTION OF TABLES

The City of Boulder's Capital Improvements Program (CIP) is a listing of projected funds available for capital projects, and all proposed City capital projects and their estimated costs, over a six year period. The City CIP is an essential implementation document for carrying out the Comprehensive Plan policies of orderly and efficient provision of urban facilities and services. The CIP provides both a short-term and a multi-year plan for improving facilities and services. It schedules projects that correct current facility deficiencies and provides for facility expansion needs within the existing city limits. The CIP also schedules the necessary improvements to extend urban facilities and services to Area II, the City's future annexation area.

Projects are described in detail in the CIP notebook, available through the City of Boulder Department of Community Planning and Development. The CIP is updated annually and adopted as an annual change to the Comprehensive Plan. The first year of the CIP is adopted by the City as part of its annual budget.

The County CIP consists primarily of road improvement projects and to the degree possible is coordinated with the City's program. It is recognized that it is the primary responsibility of the City to provide required urban services in Area II. Therefore significant improvements to Area II roads remaining under County jurisdiction should be made by both the City and the County, based on reasonable and equitable funding participation. Maintenance of Area II roads remains the County's responsibility.

### a. Projected Funds

- (1) For the General Fund, Open Space, Transportation and Permanent Parks Funds, - the "Funds Available 1/1" are the fund balance as of the end of the previous year. For the Utilities, Water, Wastewater and Flood Funds, this figure is working capital as of the end of the previous year. Working capital is current assets less current liabilities.
- (2) "Funds Available for Capital Projects" are the excess revenues that result after taking total revenues produced for a given year and deducting operating expenses and debt service expenses for that year.
- (3) "Funds from Other Sources" are identified and cross-referenced to the project each will fund by the number in parentheses to the right of the estimated project cost.
- (4) "Total Funds Available" include all of numbers 1 through 3 described above.

### b. Proposed Projects

Those projects that are on-going, as in the case of contracts or established City programs, or those that are in progress as of 12/31, have funds identified in multiple years. The projects are randomly listed and have equal status.

"Funds Available 12/31" are the result of deducting total proposed projects for each year from the total funds available for that same year. This figure then becomes the Fund Available figure for 1/1 of the following year.

### A. BACKGROUND STATEMENT ON URBAN GROWTH AND SERVICES

#### 1. CONTEXT FOR THE COMPREHENSIVE PLAN

The Boulder Valley (including the City of Boulder, and generally encompassing those land areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, and Davidson Mesa and the Coal Creek drainage on the southeast, the south County line on the south, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir and land west of Left Hand Valley Reservoir on the north—as more specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map) between the early 1950s to the mid 1970s experienced unprecedented and rapid growth with respect to population, housing, land development, and the utilization of natural and community resources growing from 21,500 people in 1950 to 104,000 people in 1988. The citizens of Boulder have become seriously concerned about the effects of uncontrolled growth on their city and the quality of life. Voters of the City of Boulder in November, 1971 approved a resolution directing local government to "take all steps necessary to hold the rate of growth in the Boulder Valley to a level substantially below that of the 1960s." In 1976, the Growth Limitation Ordinance, which was designed to control the rate of new population increases and to increase the density of the Central Area, was approved by the citizens of Boulder. Figures derived from the 1970 and 1980 censuses indicate that in 1970 the Boulder Valley population was approximately 78,000 and that in 1980, there were an estimated 94,000 people living within the Boulder Valley. The growth limitation ordinance was replaced in 1981 by the residential growth management ordinance, also designed to control the growth rate, allowing a long term population growth rate of no greater than 2% per year in the City.

Transportation, water, sewerage, schools, libraries, parks and recreation, police and fire protection, and other necessary public facilities and services are being planned to meet the needs of the Boulder Valley's growing population, but the City and the County are experiencing an increasing inability to fund and provide the demanded facilities and services at a pace which will keep abreast of the ever-growing public need.

Residents of the Boulder Valley, and a growing number of fellow Americans, are increasingly challenging the ethic that all growth is good growth. No longer is unconditional growth being accepted unquestioningly as a necessary premise of progress and an enhanced standard of living. Growing environmental concerns, a recent and renewed awareness of the energy crisis confronting us at the national and local levels, together with a growing sense of loss of sense of community, have combined with increased significance to make us realize that we do not have unlimited room or resources. We are only beginning to see that our energy, environmental, and community ills stem, essentially, from the same source: from patterns of uncontrolled and illogical urban growth and development that waste our energy resources just as liberally as they lay waste our natural environment. We no longer live in a time when we were few and the land was wide and waiting, but have reached the point where the natural resources and amenities once regarded as endlessly available and expendable are becoming increasingly scarce.

Perhaps one of the most difficult changes which must be made is in the patterns of urban growth. No longer can we accept or tolerate leapfrog or urban sprawl development which scatters jobs, homes, shopping centers, and recreation areas across the length and breadth of the landscape. The spreading patterns of settlement and development that characterize our urban areas are the legacy of our old illusion that we had endless acres of land to build on and unlimited energy to burn. The Citizen's Advisory Committee on Environmental Quality and the Task Force on Land Use and Urban Quality issued a report, The Use of Land: A Citizen's Policy Guide to Urban Growth, which begins with the finding that Americans must begin to view land as a resource, as limited as minerals, water and clean air, and indicates that there is a new mood in America. Increasingly, people are asking what urban growth will add to the quality of their lives; they are questioning the way relatively unconstrained, piecemeal urbanization is changing their communities, and are rebelling against the traditional processes of government and the marketplace which, they believe, have inadequately guided development in the past.<sup>2</sup>

This new attitude toward growth has spread to many communities across the country. In previous years, any reasonably good developer could enter a zoning, subdividing, or permit hearing confident that, if the project was well-designed, anything the developer wanted in the way of zoning modifications, sewer permits, and other special requirements would be granted. Local governments were more than happy to remove any potential roadblocks in the name of encouraging growth — or progress. Today, the picture is quite different. With the intent of reducing urban sprawl and resulting traffic congestion, numerous local citizen initiatives have been passed that place new restrictions on growth. Local governments are responding to public pressure to control growth, to stop the inefficient use of land, and to safeguard their public monies from waste on urban sprawl. Studies have indicated that growth does not pay for itself and urban sprawl or leapfrog development is very costly for local governments. ## 2. PLAN ASSUMPTIONS ABOUT THE FUTURE

Based on trends analysis and research, several working assumptions about Boulder's future provide a conceptual framework for the Comprehensive Plan. These working assumptions include:

**a. Reduced Population Growth Pressures and Changing Demographics**

Based upon growth projections jointly developed by the Denver Regional Council of Governments (DRCOG) and the City of Boulder, the population growth rate of the Boulder Service Area is expected to continue to slowly but steadily decline to the year 2010. Between the late 1980s to 2010, population growth will average approximately 1%, compared to 2% in the 1970s and 1.5% in the 1980s. The Boulder Service Area has approximately 100,000 people in 1989 and is projected to have 121,000 in 2010. Even with a reduced growth rate, this projected population growth averages approximately 1,000 new residents per year. This growth will require substantial infrastructure investments and sustained efforts to mitigate growth impacts.

Factors assumed responsible for reduced residential growth include:

- (1) Less in-migration to the Denver metropolitan region because of reduced economic opportunities compared with other parts of the country and fewer people nationwide in the age brackets that typically migrate.
- (2) Reduced rates of household formation, due to fewer numbers of persons in age categories that form new households and stabilization in household size and percentage of non-family households.
- (3) A continued shift in residential growth from Boulder to surrounding communities and other parts of the Denver metropolitan area, as more communities aggressively compete for growth.

Factors that will influence the type of housing needed include:

- (1) The aging of the baby boom generation (children born between 1946 and 1964) will result in a dramatic change in the average age of the population throughout the nation. In Boulder, it is anticipated that the average age will shift from 27 years old in 1980 to over 35 by the year 2010 and that the senior population over 55 years will more than triple by 2010 (to almost a third of the population).
- (2) Small average household size remains the norm and birth rates remain low, with the consequence that households with children remain a comparatively smaller share of total households.

### **b. Reduced Rate of Economic Growth and Changing Structure of the Economy**

Boulder is expected to remain a strong regional employment center but capture a smaller share of the total jobs in Boulder County and the region as other cities mature and develop their own employment and retail bases. Even so, Boulder will see significant employment growth with an average of about 900 new jobs each year in the 1990s and after 2000. Reasons for Boulder's economic strength are related to Boulder's ability to: maintain a desirable quality of life; continue a strong economic base of high-tech, research and development, and scientific jobs; and capitalize on University-related economic opportunities.

A changing economic structure with a shift from manufacturing to services will continue, mirroring national and regional trends.

### **c. Regional Development Assumptions**

Assumptions about Boulder's changing population and economy are related to circumstances that are projected for the greater Denver area. In the Denver region it is assumed that:

- (1) Regional population and employment growth to 2010 will slow down considerably, compared to growth in the 1970s and early 1980s.
- (2) Boulder City and Boulder County will face increasing economic competition from other communities in the metro area for new jobs.
- (3) The Denver region will not experience any major loss of economic competitive advantage, due to air and water quality problems or constraints on transportation and water supply.
- (4) The new international airport will be built northeast of Stapleton and will be operational by the mid-1990s.
- (5) The W-470 highway, if built, will be located east of the Rocky Flats plant and will be completed sometime after 2000.
- (6) New expansive urban growth plans at the fringes of the Boulder Valley will result in new annexations and development pressures, threatening the loss of open lands separating the Boulder Valley from surrounding communities.

## **b. Alternative 2% Growth Scenarios**

In order to recognize other possible growth futures for Boulder, a higher growth scenario for both population and employment is identified in Table 7 and Table 8. Higher growth scenarios for Boulder are possible if key assumptions significantly change. Boulder has little control over the ability of the Denver metro region to reverse the current and projected decline in the number of people and jobs that migrate in from other parts of the country. Achieving higher growth beyond the DRCOG projections will likely hinge on Boulder's ability to maintain its historic regional growth share in spite of increased competition and an already declining share. Higher growth than the projected average 1000 people and 900 jobs per year would likely require a much more aggressive role for Boulder in attracting growth.

The higher growth scenario for population assumes that Boulder will consistently grow at the 2 percent growth rate allowed under the Growth Management Ordinance. This is an aggressive growth assumption since the City has never grown that much in the ten years of experience under growth management. In addition, the slump in building activity in the last two years has resulted in a population growth rate of less than 1/2 percent. The higher growth scenario for employment also assumes a 2 percent growth rate for employment, which represents about twice as much growth as the DRCOG projections.

The Comprehensive Plan assumes reasonable rates of growth given the regional context, but the growth rates that actually occur may be higher or lower. The community needs to be prepared to monitor growth trends, revise projections as needed, and adapt growth management strategies to whatever level of growth actually occurs.

### PLANNING IMPLICATIONS OF POSSIBLE BUILDOUT OF THE BOULDER VALLEY<sup>1</sup>

#### SERVICES

- Buildout of the Boulder Valley will require significant expansion of services (such as police, fire, water and wastewater, parks, open space, and library), and their associated facilities.

- There will be negative impacts on wildlife habitat, but they could be mitigated by acquisition of proposed open space.

- Degradation of air quality appears to be one of the critical impacts of potential buildout of the Boulder Valley and could be a limiting factor on how much growth will actually take place.

# **B. BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN MAP DESIGNATIONS**

**The BVCP Map provides a sketch plan of the desired land use pattern in the Boulder Valley. Land use categories include residential, business, industrial, public/semi-public, open space, and park use. The BVCP Map also shows the location and functional classification of roads. The following descriptions are meant to be used in interpreting the BVCP Map.**

## **1. LAND USE**

### **a. Residential Land Use and Densities**

Residential land use areas on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, for the most part, reflect the existing land use pattern or current zoning for an area. The highest density areas are generally located close to the three major activity areas of the Boulder Valley: the Central Business District, Crossroads Area, and the University of Colorado. Medium density areas are generally situated near community shopping areas or along some of the major arterials of the City. Lower density areas in the older section of the City consist predominantly of single-family detached structures. Many of the areas developed in the City and the County over the last twenty years are characterized by a mixture of housing types ranging from single-family detached to cluster and patio homes, townhouses and apartments. The variety of housing types in the developing areas of the Boulder Valley shall continue to be encouraged during the planning period of the Comprehensive Plan.

Residential densities under the Comprehensive Plan range from very low density (two units or less per acre); low density (two to six units per acre); medium density (six to fourteen units per acre); to high density (more than fourteen units per acre). It is assumed that variations of densities on a small area basis may occur within any particular classification, but that an average density will be maintained for that classification.

Within certain residential areas, there is also the potential for small neighborhood shopping facilities and related offices and government facilities through special review. Although the neighborhood market ceased to function in most areas of the City during the late 1960s as supermarkets took over the trade, the increased cost of gasoline may make a neighborhood market or other convenience facility more popular in the future.

### **b. Business Land Use and Intensity**

Within the Boulder Valley there are four categories of business land use, based on the intensity of development and the particular needs of the residents living in each subcommunity. The four categories are: Regional, Community, General, and Arterial Business. The Comprehensive Plan does not advocate strip commercial zoning of major arterials in the Valley, but it indicates particular areas where concentration of commercial activity has taken place or might best occur in the future.

The two major Regional Business areas of the Boulder Valley are the Downtown and the Crossroads Area. Within these areas are located the major shopping facilities, offices, financial institutions, and government and cultural facilities serving the entire Boulder Valley and abutting communities. These areas will continue to be refurbished and upgraded and shall remain the dominant focus for major business activities in the region.

Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan / Sec. IV

193

A Community Business area is the focal point for commercial activity serving a subcommunity or a collection of neighborhoods. These are designed to serve the daily convenience shopping and service needs of the local populations, and are generally under 150,000 to 200,000 square feet in area. Offices within the Community Business areas should be offices designed specifically for residents of the subcommunity. Where feasible, multiple uses will be encouraged within these centers.

The General Business areas are located, for the most part, at junctions of major arterials of the city where intensive commercial uses have already been allowed. The Plan proposes that these areas continue to be used without expanding the strip character already established.

The Arterial Business designation is shown along certain major streets of the Valley. These are areas usually zoned for less intensive business uses than in the General Business areas, and they often provide a transition to residential areas.

### **c. Industrial Land Use and Intensity**

The Comprehensive Plan projects three classifications of industrial use within the Boulder Valley: General, Light and Performance. The Boulder Laboratories of the Department of Commerce, the National Center for Atmospheric Research, and several private research-oriented industries are the major employers in the Boulder Valley. Therefore, the Valley's basic industries are primarily scientific research, educational, or service facilities, and Boulder should continue to be a technological center of Colorado. The amount of land classified as industrial in the Comprehensive Plan is more than adequate to support the needs of the community.

The General Industrial classification is shown where the more intensive and heavy industries are located within the Valley. While these areas will probably continue to exist in the future, additional land for general industrial usage beyond that presently zoned is not proposed on the Plan. The greatest concentration of such uses is shown in the "East Pearl" area, ranging from 35th Street on the west to 63rd Street on the east. It is anticipated that an upgrading of this sector will occur as market demand increases.

The industrial uses considered as "Light" and "Performance" Industrial on the Comprehensive Plan are primarily research and development, light manufacturing, printing and publishing, electronics, or other intensive employment uses. These uses are concentrated primarily in "industrial parks" located within the Gunbarrel Hill area along the Longmont Diagonal, and along Arapahoe Avenue between 33rd and 55th Streets. Performance Industrial uses require high-quality site plans and must meet performance criteria for how on-site and off-site impacts are handled.

### **d. Public / Semi-Public Land Use Designations**

Public/Semi-Public land use designations encompass a wide range of public and private non-profit uses that provide a community service. This category includes municipal and public utility services such as the municipal airport, water reservoirs, and water and wastewater treatment plants. Public/Semi-Public also includes: educational facilities, including public and private schools and the University; government offices such as City and County buildings, libraries, and the jail; and nonprofit facilities such as cemeteries, churches, hospitals, and retirement complexes.

## 2. OPEN SPACE AND PARKS

### a. Open Space

Open Space designations on the BVCP Map include land already acquired by the City, or other governmental entities, and land planned for future acquisition. Open Space designations are not intended to limit acquisition, but to be indicative of the broad goals of the program. Other property which meets Open Space purposes and functions should be considered and may be acquired. Open Space designations indicate that the long-term use of the land is planned to serve one or more open space functions. However, Open Space designations may not reflect the current use of the land while in private ownership.

### b. Environmental Preservation

This designation overlays land with open space characteristics and includes areas of environmental concern generally in multiple ownership where several different preservation methods (including other governmental bodies' participation and private ownership) may need to be utilized. These lands will not be considered for control by the City Open Space Program, provided sufficient evidence exists that these lands are to be preserved in a natural state. The five areas currently designated as Environmental Preservation include the White Rocks, land along South Boulder Creek between Baseline Road and Arapahoe Road, land along Fourmile Creek to the west of and through Woodbourne Hollow residential subdivision, South Boulder Canyon Ditch, and Sombrero Marsh.

### c. Non-Urban Parks

Non-urban parks, generally identified as regional parks or wildlife habitat areas, are large, mostly natural areas used primarily for passive recreational use, such as hiking, picnicking, fishing, and bird watching, although more active uses including boating, swimming, beach activities, and large user groups are allowed in select areas. The three regional parks currently in the Boulder Valley include the Boulder Mountain Park, Boulder Reservoir, and Eldorado Canyon State Park. The wildlife habitat area designated as a Non-Urban Park is Sawhill Ponds (owned by the State of Colorado and managed by the City).

### d. Urban and Other Parks

Urban and Other Parks serve a variety of functions. Three types of parks are in this category: Community Parks, Neighborhood Parks, and Other Parks. Boulder currently has three Community Park sites that are developed or planned to be developed: Harlow Platts, East Boulder, and North Boulder Community Parks. The North Central Community Park site in Area III is land set aside for a new park if Area III land in North Boulder ever develops. Community parks provide a wide range of both active and passive park uses for large areas of the community.

Neighborhood Parks are ideally planned to be located in the center of the neighborhood or adjacent to an elementary school site. The Park should serve all age groups within the neighborhood and provide a wide variety of recreational activities. Typical elements of a Neighborhood Park include children's playgrounds, turf areas for informal open play space, picnic and passive areas, park shelter buildings, play courts, and landscaping. There are numerous Neighborhood Parks located throughout the City.

Other parks fill a variety of specialized needs and are affected by a variety of ownerships and management. For more information on different types of parks, see the City of Boulder Parks and Recreation Master Plan.

## 3. ROADS

Definitions for highway and street functional classifications are as follows:

**a. Freeway/Expressway**

Highways serving the longest trip distances designed to provide convenience for higher speed traffic. Access is only at interchanges (freeways) or occasionally at intersections (expressways) with other streets. Street laneage is four or more through lanes with additional turning lanes at intersections.

**b. Parkway**

Similar in function and design to an expressway. There is no land access to either the mainline or ramps. Differs from expressway in that more attention is paid to landscaping, berming, and physical attractiveness. Street laneage is four or more through lanes with additional turning lanes at intersections.

**c. Principal Arterial Street**

Streets serving major activity centers, high traffic volume corridors, long trip distances, and a high proportion of total urban area travel on a minimum of mileage. Service to abutting land is subordinate to the provision of travel service to major traffic movements. Street laneage is four to six through lanes as well as additional turning lanes.

**d. Minor Arterial Street**

Streets interconnecting with and augmenting the principal arterial system and providing service to trips of moderate length at a somewhat lower level of travel mobility. The system places more emphasis on land access and distributes travel to geographic areas smaller than those identified with the principal arterial system. Street laneage is two to four through lanes, with additional turning lanes where appropriate.

**e. Collector Street**

Streets serving neighborhoods, or commercial and industrial areas, collecting traffic from local streets in neighborhoods, and channeling it into the arterial systems. A minor amount of through traffic may be carried on collector streets, but the system primarily provides land access service and carries local traffic movements within residential neighborhoods, commercial areas, and industrial areas. Street laneage is two through lanes, with additional turning lanes where appropriate.

**f. Local Street**

Streets that provide direct access to abutting land and access to higher street systems. Local Streets are designed to service only low traffic volumes. The function of Local Streets is not intended to carry through traffic.

For more information on roadway functional classifications, see the City Transportation Master Plan.
