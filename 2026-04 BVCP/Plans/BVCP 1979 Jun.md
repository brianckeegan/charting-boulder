### **Section I: Application.**

**1.01** The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall be based on specific and nonconflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and *Colorado Revised Statutes, C.R.S.*, as amended (1973) S29-1-203, S29-20-101 et seq., S30-28-106 through 31-23-211, as amended. The Comprehensive Plan, consisting of a series of maps together with explanatory statements, shall be derived to the maximum extent possible from the policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to City Charter, *Colorado Revised Statutes C.R.S.* (1973) S29-1-203, S29-20-101 et seq., S30-28-106 through 30-28-109, S31-12-101 et seq., and S 31-23-206 through 31-23-211, as amended.

**1.02** The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall generally encompass the Boulder Valley which is defined as those areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa to the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north, and as specifically delineated on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map.

**1.03** The length of the planning period is approximately fifteen years. This term has been determined to be reasonable and has been arrived at after weighing a combination of facts and policy considerations: land use and land use projections, service area projections, the capacity of the City to fund capital improvements, the growth rate experienced in the 1970's, and the desires of the community in regard to accommodating growth. It is anticipated that during this fifteen year period the Boulder Valley population will increase from 95,000 to between 126,000 and 140,000.

**1.04** Projections of land use in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan are sufficiently detailed to be definitive and directional, although not limiting.

**1.05** Joint planning and close cooperation among the University, the School Districts, the City, the County, regional organizations, and other policy-making bodies (e.g. the City/County Health Department, utility and fire service entities) influencing the physical development of the Valley, shall be aggressively pursued so as to avoid conflicts and to provide a means by which each interest may more fully benefit from the presence of others.

**1.06** Development referrals and responses of the City and County shall be coordinated through the respective planning departments according to a system which has been developed by the planning departments and approved by the legislative bodies of the City and County. The responses of each department shall specifically identify those requirements which a proposed development meets, or fails to meet, under the Comprehensive Plan.

**1.07** The installation and extension of all utilities shall be consistent with the provisions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and with the responsibilities of the respective utility providers.

**1.08** It is recognized that the acquisition of open space lands in the Boulder Valley is based upon the City's overall open space plan in which prioritization of need and available revenues are considered. From time to time, however, requests for development are made for parcels of land privately held which are designated as open space on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. In such cases it is the obligation of the City to make decisions with regard to the contemplated acquisition of such lands in a timely and responsible fashion. **1.09** With regard to public facilities owned and operated in the other's jurisdiction, the City and County shall respect and abide by existing land use regulations insofar as reasonably practicable.

**1.10** The City and County shall consider the impacts of open space management and utility installation on abutting property.

## Section II: Community Design.

An urban area is land intended to accommodate concentrations of people who conduct their many and varied living activities on that land. These people need and depend upon receiving various facilities and services. Governments, usually cities, provide these services.

In Boulder County, and not unlike most other areas throughout the country, land use regulations have traditionally permitted urban areas and development in areas where adequate urban facilities and services are not yet provided, coordinated, or planned. It is uniformly and universally agreed that the resulting patterns of leapfrog remote urban development are inefficient, wasteful, and seriously contrary to the public interest, health, safety, and welfare. One of the most important objectives of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is the reduction, if not elimination, of this urban sprawl.

The basic outlines and approach of the Plan can be concisely stated. The areas immediately surrounding the City can most efficiently and effectively be provided facilities and services by the City. These areas are the most logical areas for urban development. The City intends to provide, on a phased basis over the planning period, the facilities and services to accommodate this urban development. This context should be kept in mind when considering the policies and other statements that follow.

**2.01** For the purposes of Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan structure, the Boulder Valley is divided into three major areas. Area I is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development. Area II is the area now under County jurisdiction planned to accommodate urban development, and new urban development is to occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services and not otherwise. This area is projected to be provided required facilities and services by the City by being annexed to the City during the planning period; Area IIA being the area of immediate focus, the first three years, and Area IIB being accommodated within the balance of the planning period. Area III is the remaining area in the Valley, generally under County jurisdiction and which is not now planned to accommodate urban development for the following reasons: it is not projected that the City will there provide adequate facilities and services within the planning period; no other facilities and services agency comparable to the City is expected in the area; and it is primarily a rural and agricultural area and its character should be preserved and protected.

**2.02** It is intended that throughout the Boulder Valley, new urban development, including all development within the City and those developments regulated in the unincorporated areas of the Valley by Boulder County pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-133, as amended, not occur until and unless adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development as set out in Policy Section III and in the Service Standards of the Comprehensive Plan.

New urban development is hereby defined to include:

- a. All residential, commercial and industrial development within the City; or
- b. The subdivision of land pursuant to *Colorado Revised Statutes* (1973) §30-28-133, as amended, and the County's subdivision regulations; excepting those developments which could proceed under the County's agricultural (non-urban) density transfer policies; or
- c. Any proposed residential, commercial, business or industrial development subject to County special use hearings and process; provided the County determines that the proposed use(s):
  - i. is (are) inconsistent with the Area II and land use projections contained within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map; or
  - ii. adversely and materially affect(s) the adopted capital improvements program of the City of Boulder, and thereby affect(s) the projected adequacy or availability of urban services to the immediate area of the proposed use(s) and/or to Area II in general; or
  - iii. would require a level of urban facilities and services equal to or greater than those defined in Policy 3.02; or
  - iv. is (are) inconsistent with the Comprehensive Plan policies and intent.

**2.03** The City is an adequate provider of facilities and services. These facilities and services will continue to be supplied to Area I and the City will make them available to Area II within the planning period pursuant to the City's annexation policies and Capital Improvements Program.

**2.04** The policies in regard to annexation to be pursued by the City are:

- a. Annexation will be required before adequate facilities and services are furnished;
- b. Annexation of existing substantially developed areas will be offered in a manner and on terms and conditions which respect existing life styles and densities, and the City will expect these areas to be brought to City standards only where necessary to protect the health and safety of the residents of the subject area or of the City.
- c. For undeveloped areas, the City will impose terms and conditions to protect the public interest and attempt not to impose terms such that annexation is unrealistic or impracticable.
- d. There will be no annexations of areas outside the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan boundaries.
- e. Area II is anticipated to become part of the City within the planning period.

**2.05** Progress by the City on both annexation and capital improvements shall be reviewed annually by the City and County, as any substantial failure in performance shall be deemed cause for substantial amendment of the Plan by the City and County. The Plan shall also be reviewed at least every five years for possible amendments to reflect changes in circumstances and community desires.

**2.06** The City and County will each analyze and evaluate existing land use regulations and make changes, if any, required to bring said regulations into compliance with the Comprehensive Plan and at the same time to consider the rights of affected property owners.

**2.07** Where the provider(s) of the facilities and services is (are) other than the City, the County and City will take all reasonable and legal steps to the end of facilitating assimilation by the City upon annexation.

**2.11** Major entryways into the Boulder Valley shall be identified, protected and enhanced in order to emphasize and preserve the natural setting and appearance of the community.

**2.12** Agricultural areas shall be identified and evaluated to insure, to the greatest extent possible, their long-term existence and protection from development.

### Section III: Facilities and Services.

In the foregoing policies, the City and County find that new urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services is available to serve the development. It has been determined that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.

**3.01** The City and County intend that new urban development not occur until adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of urban facilities and services by a municipality is preferable to provision of urban facilities and services by special districts and private groups in part because municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of adequate urban facilities and services. Therefore it is hereby presumed that adequate facilities and services can be provided only by the City of Boulder. The City shall extend, furnish or provide such services at such time as it can provide them all as provided under Paragraph 3.02 (c) below and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. However, it is not the intent to preclude the development and use of alternative facilities and service systems for new urban development so long as they are adequate as provided under Paragraph 3.02 (c) and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan.

**3.02** a. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban residential development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, urban transportation, parks and schools based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

b. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban industrial and commercial development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, and urban transportation, based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

### **Section IV: Environmental Features.**

The fragile environment which characterizes the Boulder Valley is a critical asset that must be preserved and protected. It is the framework within which growth and development may be permitted to take place.

- 4.01** The environmental impact of public and private projects shall, where legally necessary, be determined and the statement made available for public inspection and considered by governmental agencies before any discretionary decisions are made.
- 4.02** The Boulder Valley is to be planned in a manner which recognizes air quality as a critical concern. In light of this, logical land use relationships, expanded use of public transportation, use of pollutant control devices, stabilization of soils through use of vegetation, appropriate monitoring of urban development and mining operations, and such other means as may be feasible, shall be implemented.
- 4.03** The City and County shall seek to protect and improve the quality of the Boulder Valley waterways through identification and control of indirect, as well as direct, sources of pollution. ## **BACKGROUND STATEMENT ON URBAN GROWTH AND SERVICES**

The Boulder Valley (including the City of Boulder, and generally encompassing those land areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa on the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gun-barrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north - as more specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map) has been experiencing unprecedented and rapid growth with respect to population, housing, land development, and the utilization of natural and community resources since the early 50's; growing from 21,500 people in 1950 to 95,000 people at present. The citizens of Boulder have become seriously concerned about the effects of uncontrolled growth on their city and the quality of life. Voters of the City of Boulder in November, 1971 approved a resolution directing local government to "take all steps necessary to hold the rate of growth in the Boulder Valley to a level substantially below that of the 1960's." In 1976, the Growth Limitation Ordinance, which was designed to control the rate of new population increases and to increase the density of the Central Area, was approved by the citizens of Boulder.

Transportation, water, sewerage, schools, parks and recreation, police and fire protection, and other necessary public facilities and services are being planned to meet the needs of the Boulder Valley's growing population, but the City and County are experiencing an increasing inability to fund and provide the demanded facilities and services at a pace which will keep abreast of the ever-growing public need.

Residents of the Boulder Valley, and a growing number of fellow Americans, are increasingly challenging the ethic that all growth is good growth. No longer is unconditional growth being accepted unquestioningly as a necessary premise of progress and an enhanced standard of living. Growing environmental concerns, a recent and renewed awareness of the energy crisis confronting us at the national and local levels, together with a growing sense of loss of sense of community, have combined with increased significance to make us realize that we do not have unlimited room or resources. We are only beginning to see that our energy, environmental, and community ills stem, essentially, from the same source: from patterns of uncontrolled and illogical urban growth and development that waste our energy resources just as liberally as they lay waste our natural environment. We no longer live in a time when we were few and the land was wide and waiting, but have reached the point where the natural resources and amenities once regarded as endlessly available and expendable are becoming increasingly scarce.

Perhaps one of the most difficult changes which must be made is in the patterns of urban growth. No longer can we accept or tolerate leapfrog or urban sprawl development which scatters jobs, homes, shopping centers and recreation areas across the length and breadth of the landscape. The quantitative and qualitative comparison of alternative systems indicated it to be the best short- and medium-range solution, by providing Boulder with a substantially expanded transit service at an acceptable cost, with minimal adverse impacts, and in a manner acceptable to the majority of citizens. The patronage model predicted almost 84,000 transit riders in the year 2000 which would represent almost 12 percent of the total trips made in the area. **Mine Fire.** An area of approximately 150 acres south and east of Marshall evidences active combustion of in-place coal, surface subsidence and cracking, and surface denudation. **Subsidence of Undermine Areas.** Ground subsidence due to the collapse of coal mine voids has occurred in the Boulder Valley. Potentially devastating effects of collapse are graphically evident on the City-owned Richardson parcel northeast of the intersection of Cherryvale and Marshall Roads. A large percentage of the land in the Davidson Mesa area has been undermined. In addition, large areas are still underlain by mineable coal, the rights to which are held by potential extractors. Development should not be allowed in areas designated as subsidence hazards unless extensive analysis and remedial engineering have been performed. **Engineering Constraints.**

**Material Movement.** Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The Foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of material movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.

**Slope Instability.** Land slides are evident throughout Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure if modified by development. Open space buffers should be obtained on, above and below steep slopes. These safe zones should prevent slope disturbance and remove citizens from areas of hazard.

Areas of active landsliding should be off-limits to any development. Areas designated as "Potentially Unstable" require thorough slope stability analysis before decisions concerning development can be made.

**Alluvial Fans.** Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, evident along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to twenty feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating event, such as the Big Thompson flood of August 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan. Because of their hazardous character, alluvial fans are basically unsuitable for development.

**Earth Flows.** Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Examination of the deposits on the sediment surface bordering the foothills has discovered boulders exceeding twenty-five feet in diameter.

Exhibit 2.B.1

32

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to twenty-nine feet in diameter in silt and clay-sized mud.

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation. Saturated accumulations of debris, along and within the foothills, liquify and flow down stream channels and out across the mesa and valley surfaces. An earth flow would be extremely destructive in developed areas. Areas judged susceptible to earth flows are unsuitable for development.

**Slope Debris.** The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes. Anyone proposing development within the area shown as susceptible to debris damage should be required to provide analysis and mitigative engineering.

**Expansive Soil and Rock.** The Pierre Shale, Laramie Formation, and Fox Hills Formation each contain potentially disruptive expansive clays. The greater part of the Boulder Valley is underlain by one of these bedrock types. Proper engineering can mitigate the potential hazard. Anyone proposing development within an area designated as "Potentially Expansive" should be required to supply appropriate soil testing and mitigative engineering.

**Hydrocompatible Loess.** Hydrocompaction refers to sediment volume decrease by hydration. During Neo-Glacial times, the Denver-Boulder metro area was a glacial desert. Little organic, ground-stabilizing cover existed. Prevailing westerly winds winnowed silt-sized material from the valley floors and redeposited it atop and to the leeward side of mesas and other high land forms.

When exposed to development and resultant drainage changes, these materials tend to be compacted, decreasing their volume. This volume decrease can result in destructive surface subsidence.

The potential for hydrocompaction damage exists in numerous areas within the undeveloped southern and eastern portions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan areas. Anyone proposing development within areas designated as "Potentially Hydrocompatible" should be required to perform appropriate tests and to supply mitigative engineering.

**Flood Control.** The streams and creeks within the City of Boulder were formed by nature to provide channels for flood flows resulting from heavy snow melt and rainfall. Boulder's flood problems began with the initial development of the community adjacent to Boulder Creek. This development established a pattern for similar residential, commercial, or industrial construction along other portions of Boulder Creek and within other flood areas in the Boulder Valley. As a result of private and public development over the flood plain, flood heights and velocities have increased and the potential for economic loss and concern for personal safety has increased.

Boulder is particularly vulnerable to flood damage because of its unique geographic location. In addition, because of its proximity to the mouth of Boulder Canyon, the Central Area of the city is highly susceptible to the particular hazards of flash floods. In an effort to provide an overall, coordinated program to control flood situations, and to protect life and property, the City of Boulder has adopted a comprehensive Flood Control Program.

There are two main elements in Boulder's Flood Control Program. The primary effort has been the adoption of a City Flood Plain Ordinance which regulates construction within the area subject to 100-year floods. Each flood plain within the Boulder Valley ultimately will be divided into two parts: first, the flood storage area, which is basically the slow-moving, storage area for flood water where development is allowed to take place if adequate flood-proofing measures are taken; and, second, the floodway or flood hazard district, which is the primary channel of the 100-year flood, including the areas where waters will flow at significant depths and velocities.

Exhibit 2.B.1

34

The second major flood control effort of the City of Boulder is the Flood Control Master Plan, which calls for the enlargement of natural drainageways to contain peak flows of 100-year floods. Conceptual design has been adopted for most of the major creeks and drainageways in the City of Boulder, and additional design detail of those areas is in progress. As development occurs, each land developer is required to construct a portion of the drainage facility included within, or abutting, his property to insure adequate drainage and control of flood and storm water.

The aspects of the two flood control programs are interrelated; progress in one area affects the classification of the other.

The Flood Control Program provides a sound regulatory basis for guiding private and public development in flood areas, taking into account both the most desirable uses of land from the viewpoint of the community as a whole and the needs and rights of private landowners.

The County of Boulder has flood plain regulations which are generally compatible with those of the City of Boulder; therefore, transition from County to City jurisdiction as it relates to the Flood Control Program will not cause great difficulties.

Sensitive in-fill and private redevelopment which meet the above criteria shall be considered positive alternatives to urban sprawl, an asset to the Downtown and regional shopping corridor revitalization, as well as an important energy conservation approach.
2. The City shall encourage existing sound residential structures and mature landscaping to be retained and improved as an integral part of any new development.
3. No large scale urban renewal and residential relocation programs to implement residential redevelopment will be undertaken. Under certain circumstances, limited clearance of certain structures and non-conforming uses, which are themselves blighted or are deteriorating influences on the neighborhood, may be considered. In such cases, adequate compensation and relocation expenses would be provided to affected parties.
4. Rehabilitation assistance for residential structures may be provided to certain qualified residents within the area where long-term tenure and stability appears likely.

**Older, Stable Residential Neighborhoods.** The remaining, predominantly residential, neighborhoods situated generally between Iris Avenue and Baseline Road, the Front Range and Folsom Road, shall be considered "stable" residential environments. 2. Because of the number of young adults living within this area, public and private recreational activities and facilities will be encouraged to serve these residential units. **Urban Design Element.** Each of the sub-areas of the Central Area have interrelationships which must be recognized and maintained for the entire area to remain a healthy and viable entity. The critical issues involved in determining the appropriate range of land uses for each area, the relative intensity of such uses, and the degree and rapidity with which any particular area might undergo redevelopment or change in the future have been described in the preceding policies. These policies are important determinants of Boulder's Central Area revitalization programs, be they public or private, and be they physical, social, and/or economic in their effects. Inherent in these policies and programs are recognition of, and sensitivity to, the underlying characteristics of the particular area or neighborhood under consideration.

Of additional importance is the development of adequate linkages between the subcommunities. Elements which define these linkages include the following:

1. Enhance Boulder Creek as a greenway, serving as common open space, bikeway and pedestrian thread which has the potential of unifying each subcommunity, as well as providing an attractive and functional buffer between the Downtown Area and the residential neighborhoods to the south.
2. Promote development of an improved mass transit system which directly links the major activity nodes of the Central Area, the Downtown, Crossroads, and University neighborhoods. With certain reasonable cost-benefit constraints and other impacts, such a system should represent the technological state of the art.
3. Promote development of an inviting, safe, and convenient bikeway and pedestrian system to serve and interconnect each section of the Central Area.
4. Improve the capacity of major arterial collectors in the Central Area to handle as much through traffic demand as practical in order to preserve, to the greatest extent feasible, the quiet residential character of local neighborhood streets.
5. Future strip commercial development shall be discouraged along major arterials.

**Implementation of Central Area Goals.** A partnership of the public and private sectors must be retained throughout the planning process. Several techniques to implement the Central Area goals currently exist, such as zoning, capital improvements programming, State and Federal assistance. The City shall strive to utilize these tools in an effective, innovative, and sensitive manner. The city will explore, as well, the use of other potential techniques not presently available. Possible new tools include: impact or incentive zoning in redeveloping areas; development rights transfers; tax increment financing or creation of development authorities; economic development bonds; urban design plan for Central Area; creation of historically- or architecturally-significant landmarks and districts.

The relative size, intensity, and scale of the Central Area to the total community is vital to the entire concept of enhancing community identity and focus and the combatting of urban sprawl.

It is further recognized that, in time, the boundaries and size of the Central Area, particularly its transitional and stable older neighborhoods, may change. As these neighborhoods change, the programs most relevant to their status will be modified accordingly.

### **AREA III ANNEXATION POLICIES.** Exhibit 3.A

It is anticipated in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan that Area IIIA shall be annexed to the City of Boulder within three years, consistent with the phased expansion of the City's capacity to provide adequate urban facilities and services. The following statements describe very briefly and very generally the City's present annexation policies and procedures, both of which may be modified from time to time without requiring an amendment of the Comprehensive Plan. The City's annexation process may vary somewhat to fit the circumstances of any particular annexation, but shall be consistent with Policy 8.2.04.

- A. State statutes: Among other requirements, all annexations shall comply with requirements of the Colorado State Statutes which include the following provisions:
  - 1. At least one-sixth of the perimeter of the area to be annexed shall be contiguous to the city limits.
  - 2. A petition must be presented by landowners of more than fifty percent of the area to be annexed, except for enclaves and areas of two-thirds contiguity where the City may take the initiative.
  - 3. Under certain conditions, an election may be held by the property owners with the area to be annexed.
- B. Relationship to existing City boundaries and consolidation of Petitions: The City may consolidate individual properties requesting annexation into larger aggregate parcels when the request areas are adjacent and such grouping is desirable because of contiguity, compactness, or logical extensions of then-existing city boundaries, or for the more efficient processing of numerous annexation requests.
- C. Zoning and land use: While zoning classifications in the County will be recognized, annexation into the City will need to conform to City zoning categories and the land use designations of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.
- D. Environmental features: Though detailed environmental impact statements generally will not be expected for lands within Area IIIA, commitments may be required at the time of annexation to protect parcels having special or unique natural features or hazards.
- E. Public improvements: The following topics indicate the general terms and conditions that normally will be imposed for existing development at time of annexation. These conditions may vary to some degree depending upon the annexation of a particular area.
  - 1. Water and Ditch Rights: The City shall follow the procedures outlined in Ordinance No. 4351 concerning the acquisition of first right of refusal of water and ditch rights.
  - 2. Streets: The City will permit flexibility in the design and improvements of local streets when desired by local residents. As a condition of annexation, the City shall require the improvement of streets to levels which will insure that the City will not incur extraordinary maintenance costs as a result of new annexations. The City will consider petitions from property owners to initiate a street improvement project at any time after annexation, so long as such project will not conflict with other scheduled capital improvements.

3. Street Lights: Following annexation, street lights may be installed, upon petition of the residents of the area, subject to Public Service Company standards and approval.
4. Water and Sewer: At the time of annexation, a property owner of property other than a single family residence shall connect to the City water and sewer if the lines abut the property. For a property owner of a single family residence that has an existing water and sewer system with no health-related problems, the property owner may continue use of the individual system until said system fails or needs upgrading.

### **REFERRAL PROCESS.** Exhibit 3.C

As part of the cooperative planning process, the City of Boulder and County of Boulder have established the following referral process for certain types of land use and public improvements activity within the Boulder Valley. For proposed new urban development as defined in Policy 2.02, replats, rezoning, special use review, and major public improvements in the unincorporated areas of the Boulder Valley, the County Planning Department shall submit a referral to the City of Boulder Planning Department for review and comment. For proposed rezonings, annexations, capital improvements and open space acquisitions by the City, the City Planning Department shall submit a referral to the Boulder County Planning Department for review and comment. A referral is a written communication from the Planning Department of either the City or County to the Planning Department of either the County or City, respectively, in which there is contained either a request for or a response to a request for review and comment on the above-described matters relevant to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

The following guidelines shall apply to the referral process:

1. The referral process shall be a communication procedure whereby referral requests are mandatory and referral responses are optional. The receipt of all referrals shall be formally acknowledged.
2. Responses to all referrals shall be based upon the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, including all applicable policies, maps, and implementation documents, and applicable codes, ordinances and resolutions of the respective jurisdictions.
3. All referral requests and responses of departments of the City and County shall be received, reviewed, and communicated through the respective planning departments, with the understanding that referral responses may be reviewed by the Planning Board or Planning Commission and/or the City Council or Board of County Commissioners of the referee as may be determined by the referee in its discretion or as required by law.
4. Complete referral responses shall be made promptly and in no case later than fifteen working days from date of referral receipt unless the referee notifies the referor in writing that it intends to extend the referral response time to a date certain and the referee states the reasons for the extension. In no case shall a referral extend beyond 90 days. Failure to respond within 90 days in the case of an extension may be regarded by the referor as a response with no conflict. The referor shall consider all referral responses or the fact that there has been no referral response before proceeding with the activity proposed in the referral request.
5. For all new urban development proposed outside the City, the City shall not accept any applications for water and sewer permits until the sketch plan or special use review has been approved by the County. The County shall not hold public hearings concerning new urban development proposed in the Boulder Valley unless it has first received a referral response from the City, or unless the City has failed to make a referral response as required by paragraph (4) above.

## URBAN SERVICE AND CRITERIA STANDARDS. Exhibit 3.E

### I. Criteria.

The following criteria are to be used in the determination of the adequacy of proposed or existing urban facilities and services :

**A. Responsiveness to Public Objectives.** It is desirable and necessary that all urban service systems be coordinated and integrated with other service systems; evaluated periodically for need, efficiency, and cost effectiveness; and studied for possible duplication of other service systems so as to be responsive to local public objectives and general public need as determined by the governing body.

As public funds and resources are limited, primary emphasis must be given to an effective allocation system which, to the greatest extent possible, effects a consistency of legislative intent; public policy; urban service programs funding; a periodic assessment of the type, quantity and quality of various urban services; realistic estimates of revenue sources and future income; maximization of the availability of outside funding sources (state, federal, etc.); and consistency of the long-range program direction in accordance with a capital improvements plan. It has been determined that the municipal budget of the City of Boulder is such an allocation system.

**B. Sufficiency and Dependability of Financing.** Financing for each urban service program must be based, to the greatest extent possible, on predictable annual revenues which are broad enough to support initial improvements, maintenance, and extensions of facilities and services in relation to, at least, minimum program requirements and unexpected contingencies. Where financing is tied solely to users' fees, serious problems may result if and when the demand for such programs may diminish. At the same time, programs relying heavily on real property revenues or state and federal supplemental funding could be severely restricted if such sources of financing fall short of initial predictions. While no guarantees can be given, financing from a variety of potential sources and spread over a broad base, including sufficient latitude so that funding can be adjusted in the future as changing conditions occur, should be preferred over single-source revenue programs.

**C. Operational Effectiveness.** Each urban service program may have distinct operational needs and a wide array of operational activities might be considered. The end result, though, is whether or not a given public program is able to function in a direct, efficient manner. As an example, a volunteer fire department, depending upon personnel employed in other endeavors and scattered throughout the area of service may not respond as quickly and effectively as firefighting personnel located where the fire suppression equipment is stored. Also a small sewage treatment plant receiving only limited flows might not be adequately financed to permit proper and continuous monitoring and management.

Measures of operational effectiveness include current and long-range project forecasting, coordination with other urban service programs, maximization of economies of scale in urban service provision, and the incorporation of operational processes and organizational methods that have proven effective in similar situations.

Exhibit 3.E

58

**D. Proficiency of Personnel.** Conditions and factors which may affect the competency of personnel include the following: 1) education and experience of personnel in meeting job demands; 2) interest and willingness of personnel to implement programs; 3) on-the-job training opportunities; 4) working conditions and fringe benefits related to employees' effectiveness; 5) the ability of the urban service agency to pay salaries commensurate with personnel requirements; and, 6) the effectiveness of recruitment programs.

As with other criteria, extremes may be involved, but, in general, the measure of whether or not urban services are being provided must, in part, be determined by the skills of individuals carrying out such programs. For each job a comparison should be made of qualifications of the individual employed or to be employed, against personnel standards established by generally recognized public or technical agencies for similar positions.

**E. Adequacy of Equipment and Facilities.** This factor may be more easily measured than any of the other since "generally accepted standards" are involved. As an example, as residences are located in closer proximity to each other, interest in developing neighborhood parks will normally increase. As evidenced by recreation programs desired by similar population groupings, certain standards for park areas, spaces and facilities may be predicted. In a similar manner, most of the other public services and facilities described in this study have minimum locational space, equipment and building needs related to given population groupings.

**Introduction.** In 1970, Black & Veatch presented to the City of Boulder a study of, and a long-range plan of improvements for, its sanitary sewer system. Since 1970, revisions have been made to the projected population, service area, and treatment requirements which cause the need for an updated plan of improvements.

The scope of this study was limited to the following:

1. Using the population data, land use patterns, service area boundary and per capita loadings provided by the City, analyze the sanitary sewer system for existing, 1983, and ultimate loadings.
2. Develop a system of main, trunk, relief, and interceptor sewers that will provide good and reliable service to the ultimate service area.
3. Develop and schedule a plan of improvements to the sewer system that will be required to serve the 1983 service area and will fit into and become a part of the ultimate system.
4. Briefly discuss the improvements required for sewage treatment.
5. Schedule the improvements and capital requirements through 1983.

**Treatment.** The City currently operates two wastewater treatment plants, the 75th Street Plant and the East Pearl Plant. The main plant is the 75th Street Plant which has a design capacity of 15.6 mgd and is a secondary treatment facility utilizing the trickling filter process with sludge handling by lime stabilization, vacuum filtration, and landfill disposal. A project is currently under construction which includes the addition of anaerobic digesters, an administration building, a maintenance building, odor control facilities, and a new headworks to the 75th Street Plant. This project is scheduled for completion in December, 1979.

The East Pearl Plant is currently operated as a base-loaded primary treatment facility with effluent returned to the interceptor sewer and subsequently treated at the 75th Street Plant. Sludge is treated by anaerobic digestion and is injected into the soil on a site adjacent to the plant. It is anticipated that treatment at the East Pearl Plant will be discontinued when the anaerobic digesters at the 75th Street Plant are complete.

Future anticipated improvements to the 75th Street Plant are the installation of a sludge injection system similar to the system now being used at the East Pearl Plant and liquid stream improvements to improve the quality of the plant effluent. Both of these improvements are being held up pending evaluation by the Environmental Protection Agency.

The major purpose of the liquid stream improvements will be to provide nitrification. Two processes currently under consideration include infiltration-percolation basins and additional on-site biological treatment. The City, in conjunction with the University of Colorado, is currently running pilot studies on infiltration-percolation basins and three methods of additional biological treatment, namely, activated sludge, rotating biological contactors, and a biological fluidized-bed.

The infiltration-percolation basins have provided a high quality effluent and were indicated by the facility plan to be more economical than additional on-site biological treatment. Public concern over the additional land required for the basins and possible secondary impacts

have been items of concern in the evaluation of the infiltration-percolation basins.

Of the three methods of additional on-site biological treatment, the rotating biological contactors appear to be the most promising. The activated sludge and rotating biological contactors have both produced satisfactory effluent. The advantages of rotating biological contactors over activated sludge are reduced power consumption and greater suitability to the relatively shallow existing final settling basins.

BOULDER COUNTY BOARD OF COMMISSIONERS

ADOPTION OF THE REVISED AND UPDATED BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, AND BOULDER COUNTY BOARD OF COMMISSIONERS APPROVAL OF AN INTERGOVERNMENTAL CO-OPERATIVE AGREEMENT RELATING THERETO, SETTING FORTH DETAILS IN RELATION THERETO

WHEREAS, pursuant to Boulder County Planning Commission Resolution dated July 13, 1977 and Boulder County Board of Commissioners Resolution 77-45, Boulder County approved the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN on August 15, 1977; and

WHEREAS, over the course of the past several months the City and County planning and legal staffs have prepared numerous amendments and additions to the Plan in order to improve the capacity of the Plan to operate as an effective land use planning tool; and

WHEREAS, joint City-County execution of the "Intergovernmental Cooperative Agreement Between the City of Boulder and the County of Boulder for the Purpose of Planning and Regulating the Development of Land in the Boulder Valley", a copy of which is attached hereto as Exhibit A, will render the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan a more effective growth management tool, consistent with the intent and purposes thereof; and

WHEREAS, the City of Boulder Planning Board and the City Council have reviewed the proposed implementation documents and Plan amendments, revising and updating the Plan, including the proposed intergovernmental cooperative agreement and the exhibits thereto, and have approved the same subsequent to public hearings on April 27 and May 16, 1978; and

WHEREAS, the Boulder County Planning Commission has reviewed the proposed implementation documents and Plan amendments, revising and updating the Plan, including the proposed intergovernmental cooperative agreement and the exhibits thereto, and have approved the same subsequent to public hearing on May 24, 1978; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973), Section 29-20-105, as amended, local governments are authorized and encouraged to cooperate or contract with other units of government for the purposes of planning or regulating the development of land; and

WHEREAS, the Boulder County Planning Commission finds that:

1. The updated and revised BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN finally resolves several issues, among others, which have been outstanding for some time and which include:

- a. The means by which the Plan can be adjusted and amended;
- b. The City's annexation policy and procedures associated therewith;
- c. The scope and extent of development to be regulated pursuant to the Plan;
- d. The means by which the City will deal with out-of-City utility service requests;
