# Introduction

The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan provides a general statement of the desired long-term future development and preservation of the Boulder Valley. The Comprehensive Plan is comprehensive in scope, building from its foundation as a land use and urban service provision plan, incorporating physical, social, and economic concerns. Since 1970, the City and County have adopted a joint comprehensive plan to guide land use and development in the Boulder Valley. In 1978, the City and County adopted the Intergovernmental Agreement that serves as the basis for the current Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan contains policies, subcommunity and area plan summaries, city master plan and program summaries, a land use map with designations for land in the City's service area, and a map that identifies where the City may annex land and provide urban services. Major changes to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan require approval of the City Planning Board, the City Council, the County Planning Commission and the Board of County Commissioners. The last major update to the Plan was completed in 1990.

This major update of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan incorporates the results of four major planning projects conducted since 1990: the Area III Planning Study, the Gunbarrel Study, the Integrated Planning Project, and the North Boulder Subcommunity Plan. The first two were joint projects of the City and County, and the last two were City projects. Three of these projects are summarized below. A summary of the North Boulder Subcommunity Plan is included in the Subcommunity and Area Plan section of this document.

**The Area III Planning Study** (1990-92) identified the land in Area III (areas located outside of the City's Service Area but within the Boulder Valley), that is suitable for possible service area expansion. The study proposed eliminating the following categories of lands as unsuitable for expansion of the City's Service Area: prime agricultural land, flood zones and other hazardous areas, and wetlands and other sensitive environments. In addition, the study examined the feasibility of extending City services, as well as areas that would be logical service area expansions, considering the goal of maintaining a compact urban form. The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan was amended in 1994 to incorporate the results of the Area III Planning Study. The amendments included the definition and designation of the Rural Preservation Area and the Planning Reserve, and procedures for amending the boundaries of these areas as well as a new process for considering service area expansions. In 1995, the City and County signed an Intergovernmental Agreement creating a transfer of development rights (TDR) program, an important tool for implementing the Area III project.

**The Gunbarrel Study** (1991) addressed the status of the residential portion of the Gunbarrel Subcommunity, which is primarily located in Unincorporated Boulder County. These areas are served with City water and sewer, are developed at urban densities, and are contained within the City's Service Area. Concerns surfaced in 1990 regarding a series of proposals by private landowners to expand the City's Service Area adjacent to the Gunbarrel Subcommunity, particularly along Jay Road. The study concluded that the residents of Gunbarrel did not support annexation under the City's proposed annexation package that allowed the City to recoup the costs associated with providing services. The County was concerned about their ability to provide these services. The result was the formation of a Gunbarrel General Improvement District that will purchase and improve land for parks and open space purposes and improve and maintain streets in the County residential areas.

**Boulder's Integrated Planning Project** (IPP) was a community visioning exercise undertaken in 1993 to provide direction for planning Boulder's land use, transportation, housing, economy and environment for the next 30 years. The Area III Planning Project dealt with issues related to the City's future service area, while the IPP addressed "what's best for what's left." Scenarios of the future were used to generate community discussion and choices regarding the tradeoffs the community was willing to make in the areas of population and employment, transportation, environmental quality and affordable housing. The result of the IPP was a set of goals and action items adopted by the City Council. This major update to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan represents one piece of implementing the IPP. The policy section of the Plan has been revised based on the IPP goals and action items, and several changes to the land use map are related to the population and employment targets initiated as part of the IPP.

# I. Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Policies

## A. Policies

### 1. General Policies

*The City and the County share responsibility for planning land use and development in the Boulder Valley. The City-County Intergovernmental Agreement of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is a unique feature, and strengthens the basis for implementing the Plan. The City and the County recognize that implementation of the Plan may affect and be affected by decisions of other agencies and jurisdictions in the County and metropolitan area.*

### Scope and Application of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan

#### 1.01 Joint City-County Plan Adoption and Implementation.

The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, hereafter referred to as the Comprehensive Plan, shall be based on specific and nonconflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and Colorado Revised Statutes, as amended (1973) §29-1-203, §29-20-101 et seq., §30-28-106 through 31-23-211. The Comprehensive Plan, consisting of a series of maps together with explanatory statements, shall be derived to the maximum extent possible from the policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the Boulder City Charter, Colorado Revised Statutes as amended, §29-1-203, §29-20-101 et seq., §30-28-106 through 30-28-109, §31-12-101 et seq., and §31-23-206 through 31-23-211.

#### 1.02 Definition of the Boulder Valley Planning Area.

The Comprehensive Plan shall generally encompass the Boulder Valley which is defined as those areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Davidson Mesa and the Dry Creek and Coal Creek

drainage on the east and southeast, the south county line on the south, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, lands north and west of the Boulder Reservoir and land west of Left Hand Valley Reservoir on the north, and as specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map.

**Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan:**  
Provides a general statement of the desired long-term future for the Boulder Valley. The BVCP is comprehensive in scope, building from its foundation as a land use and urban service provision plan, incorporating physical, social, and economic concerns. The Comprehensive Plan is an umbrella policy document, encompassing shared needs and aspirations in the community, within which subcommunity, area, and functional master plans are developed.

**Subcommunity Plans:**

Are a tool for applying the BVCP over-arching vision and policy framework to the areas called subcommunities. Subcommunities are functionally-related, distinct groupings of neighborhoods and related commercial subareas defined for planning purposes. Subcommunity plans provide more detailed land use, community facility, and urban design planning and implementation. Subcommunity plans also allow more detailed examination of the interrelationships among different physical, social, economic, and service systems.

**Area Plans:**

Address planning issues at a more detailed level than the Comprehensive Plan or subcommunity plans. Areas are distinct districts within subcommunities, consisting of predominantly residential uses or predominantly commercial or employment uses. Area plans will be developed for subareas with special problems or opportunities not adequately addressed by subcommunity plans. The broader context for area plans is provided by the Comprehensive Plan and relevant subcommunity plans.

**Master Plans:**

Deal with service delivery systems, facilities, and programs. The functional master plans are devised consistent with the policy and implementation frameworks in the Comprehensive Plan and subcommunity plans. However, more specific service delivery policies, concepts, and implementation schemes are provided in the functional master plans.

**1.03 Planning Time Frame.**

The length of the planning period is approximately fifteen years. This term has been determined to be reasonable and has been arrived at after weighing a combination of facts and policy considerations: land use and land use projections, Service Area projections, the capacity of the City to fund capital improvements, the growth rate, and the desires of the community in regard to accommodating growth. Each five-year review of the plan shall extend the planning period approximately five years.

In addition, growth projections to the year 2020 will be used to evaluate long-term trends, analyze problems and opportunities that could occur during this period, project long-term infrastructure needs, and coordinate Boulder Valley growth projections with regional and countywide projections.

**1.04 Plan Update.**

The Plan shall be reviewed at least every five years for possible amendments to reflect changes in circumstances and community desires.

**1.05 Land Use Projections.**

Projections of land use in the Comprehensive Plan are sufficiently detailed to be definitive and directional, although not limiting.

**1.06 Plan Integration.**

A variety of community plans exists to guide day-to-day decision making in a long-term context. The City is committed to achieve a high level of coordination and integration of plan development and update processes, plan content and substance, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, subcommunity plans, area plans, and functional master plans.

## Intergovernmental Cooperation

**1.07 Regional Cooperation.**

Many of the most significant problems and opportunities faced by Boulder and other jurisdictions can only be dealt with effectively through regional cooperation and

solutions. Therefore, the City and the County shall aggressively pursue joint planning and close cooperation with each other and among other cities, unincorporated communities, the University of Colorado, the School Districts, regional organizations, and other policy-making bodies (e.g., other Counties, the County Board of Health, utility and fire service entities). These entities should address issues of mutual concern, in order to avoid conflicts, provide a means by which each entity may more fully benefit from a multi-jurisdictional perspective, and achieve mutually beneficial solutions.

**1.08 Development Referrals and Responses.**

Development referrals and responses of the City and the County shall be coordinated through the respective planning departments according to a system which has been developed by the planning departments and approved by the legislative bodies of the City and the County. The responses of each department shall specifically identify those requirements which a proposed development meets, or fails to meet, under the Comprehensive Plan.

**1.09 Intergovernmental Agreements.**

The City and the County may enter into intergovernmental agreements with other municipalities and agencies when appropriate to foster intergovernmental planning and cooperation.

**1.10 Compliance with Land Use Regulations.**

With regard to public facilities owned and operated in the other's jurisdiction, the City and the County shall respect and abide by existing land use regulations insofar as reasonably practicable.

## Growth Management

**1.11 City's Role in Managing Growth and Development.**

In order to achieve community goals and policies, the City will implement growth management tools that control the scale, location, type, intensity, and timing of new development and redevelopment. Where appropriate, the County will work with the City in developing and implementing growth management tools.

### 1.12 Adapting to Limits on Physical Expansion.

As the community expands to its planned physical boundaries, the City and the County will increasingly emphasize preservation and enhancement of the physical, social, and economic assets of the community. Cooperative efforts and resources shall be focussed on maintaining and improving the quality of life within defined physical boundaries, with only limited expansion of the city.

### 1.13 Growth Projections.

In order to ensure that past and projected growth impacts can be better mitigated or avoided, and to maintain a desirable community size, the City shall set projections for population and dwelling units for the year 2020. Residential growth projections include a range of 3,350-4,900 additional new dwelling units within the 1994 City boundaries. Projected growth will be limited unless sufficient progress is made in reducing the cumulative negative growth impacts to an acceptable level and other significant community benefits can be achieved.

### 1.14 Growth Requirements.

The overall effect of urban growth must be to add significant value to the community, improving quality of life. The City will require development and redevelopment as a whole to provide significant community benefits and to maintain or improve environmental quality as a precondition for further housing and employment growth.

### 1.15 Open Space Referrals.

It is recognized that the acquisition of open space lands in the Boulder Valley is based upon the City's overall Open Space Plan in which prioritization of need and available revenues are considered. From time to time, however, requests for development are made for parcels of land privately held which are designated as Open Space on the Comprehensive Plan. In such cases it is the obligation of the City to make decisions with regard to the contemplated acquisition of such lands in a timely and responsible fashion. In the event that a negotiated agreement cannot be reached and the City does not wish to initiate condemnation

action, then the property owner may proceed to request development consistent with other applicable policies of the Comprehensive Plan.

## Framework for Annexation and Urban Service Provision

### 1.16 Definition of Comprehensive Planning Areas I, II, and III.

The Boulder Valley Planning Area is divided into three major areas.

**Area I** is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate urban facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development.

**Area II** is the area now under County jurisdiction, where annexation to the City can be considered consistent with Policies 1.12 and 1.14 and 1.20. New urban development may only occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services and not otherwise. Departmental master plans project the provision of services to this area within the planning period.

Area IIA is the area of immediate focus within the first three years, and Area IIB is available to accommodate development within the balance of the planning period.

### 1.18 Definition of New Urban Development.

It is intended that throughout the Boulder Valley, new urban development, including all development within the City and those developments regulated in the unincorporated areas of the Valley by Boulder County pursuant to Colorado Revised Statutes, as amended, §30-28-133, not occur until and unless adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development as set out in Section (II D) Urban Service Criteria and Standards.

New urban development is hereby defined to include:

- (a) All residential, commercial and industrial development within the City; or
- (b) The subdivision of land pursuant to Colorado Revised Statutes (as amended) §30-28-133, and the County's subdivision regulations; excepting those developments which could proceed under the County's non-urban planned unit development process; or
- (c) Any proposed residential, commercial, business or industrial development subject to County rezoning or special hearings and processes; provided the County determines that the proposed use(s):

i. is (are) inconsistent with the Area II and land use projections contained within the Comprehensive Plan Map; or

ii. adversely and materially affect(s) the adopted Capital Improvements Program of the City of Boulder, and thereby affect(s) the projected adequacy or availability of urban services to the immediate area of the proposed use(s) and to Area II in general; or

iii. would require a level of urban facilities

iv. is (are) inconsistent with the Comprehensive plan policies and intent.

### 1.19 City Service Provision/ Location of Urban Facilities.

The City is an adequate provider of facilities and services. These facilities and services will continue to be supplied to Area I and the City will make them available to Area II within the planning period pursuant to the City's annexation policies and Capital Improvements Program. Due to size, locational, and other unique requirements, some City facilities that do not require the full range of urban services will be located in Areas II and III, subject to County review.

### 1.20 Annexation.

The policies in regard to annexation to be pursued by the City are:

- (a) Annexation will be required before adequate facilities and services are furnished;
- (b) Annexation of existing substantially developed areas will be offered in a manner and on terms and conditions which respect existing lifestyles and densities, and the City will expect these areas to be brought to City standards only where necessary to protect the health and safety of the residents of the subject area or of the City. The City, in developing annexation plans of reasonable cost, may phase new facilities and services. The County, which now has jurisdiction over these areas, shall be a supportive partner with the City in annexation efforts.
- (c) In order to reduce the negative impacts of new development in the Boulder Valley, the City shall annex Area II land with significant development or redevelopment potential only on a very limited basis.

Such annexations will be supported only if the annexation resolves an issue of public health or provides a special opportunity or benefit to the City.

### 1.26 City/County Policy Applicability.

Where a particular "area" is not specified in the policy text, the policy shall apply to all areas.

## 2. Community Design

*The Comprehensive Plan seeks an urban development pattern that is compact and efficient and that permits the most effective and cost efficient provision of city facilities and services. Such a development pattern enhances the livability of the community for its residents by increasing accessibility to employment, recreation, shopping and other amenities and by reducing auto travel and air pollution. From an urban design perspective, it allows a strong, understandable image of Boulder as a separate community to emerge. Redevelopment and infill, when sensitive to the character of existing neighborhoods, are encouraged as a means of maintaining a compact community.*

#### 2.01 Unique Community Identity.

The unique and powerful community identity and sense of place as recognized by the citizens of the Boulder Valley and characterized by its setting and history shall be respected by policy decision makers.

#### 2.05 Open Space Preservation.

The City and the County will preserve lands with unique natural features and characteristics as permanent open space by purchase of development rights, fee simple gifts or purchases, and other measures as appropriate and financially feasible.

#### 2.02 Physical Separation of Communities.

The City and the County shall strive to maintain and enhance an open land buffer that separates development in the Boulder Valley from surrounding communities and contributes to a distinct community identity for the City.

#### 2.06 Design of Community Edges.

Well-defined edges for the City's boundaries are important as they support an understanding and appreciation of the City's image and create a clear sense of arrival and departure. Natural features are most effective as edges, but public open land, major roadways or heavy tree planting also can function as community edges. As new areas are developed, the definition of a community edge shall be a design priority.

#### 2.03 Community/Regional Design.

The City and the County support improved design of individual urban areas, rural areas, and the region through design features such as: clear urban boundaries, open land buffers separating compact communities, vital activity centers, preservation of critical natural areas and vistas, appropriate connection of trail systems, efficient multi-modal travel corridors, a balanced distribution of housing and job opportunities, provision of diverse housing, and conservation of physical and social resources.

#### 2.07 Design of Major Entryways.

Major entryways into the Boulder Valley shall be identified, protected and enhanced in order to emphasize and preserve the natural setting and appearance of the community. Future strip commercial development shall be discouraged.

#### 2.04 Compact Land Use Pattern.

The City and the County will, by implementing the Comprehensive Plan, ensure that development will take place in an orderly fashion which will take advantage of existing urban services and shall avoid, insofar as possible, patterns of leapfrog, noncontiguous, scattered development within the Boulder Valley. # 3. Facilities and Services

*The City and the County agree that urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services is available to serve the development. The City and the County find that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.*

## Framework for Provision of Urban Facilities and Services

### 3.01 Provision of Urban Services in the Boulder Valley.

The City and the County intend that new urban development not occur until adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of the full range of urban facilities and services by a municipality is preferable to provision of urban facilities and services by special districts and private groups in part because municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of adequate urban facilities and services. Therefore it is hereby presumed that adequate facilities and services can be provided only by the City of Boulder. The City shall extend, furnish or provide such services at such time as it can provide them all as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) below and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. However, it is not the intent to preclude the development and use of alternative facilities and service systems for new urban development so long as they are adequate as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) and the Urban Service Criteria and Standards section of this plan.

### 3.02 Definition of Adequate Urban Facilities and Services.

(a) "adequate facilities and services" for new urban residential development means the availability of public water, public sewer, flood control and drainage, urban fire protection and emergency medical care, urban police protection, urban transportation, developed urban parks, and schools based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph (c) below.

(b) "adequate facilities and services" for new urban industrial and commercial development means the availability of public water, public sewer, flood control and drainage, urban fire protection and emergency medical care, urban police protection, and urban transportation, based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph (c) below.

(c) The availability and adequacy of urban facilities and services as set forth in subparagraphs (a) and (b) above shall be determined based upon the following criteria and as further defined in the Urban Service Criteria and Standards section of this plan. Determinations of facilities and services adequacy shall be based upon the following criteria:

- i. Responsiveness to public objectives;
- ii. Sufficiency and dependability of financing;
- iii. Operational effectiveness;
- iv. Adequacy of equipment and facilities; and
- v. Proficiency of personnel.

# 4. Environment

*The fragile environment which characterizes the Boulder Valley is a critical asset that must be preserved and protected. It is the framework within which growth and development may be permitted to take place. ## Plan within an Ecosystem Framework

### 4.01 Incorporating Ecological Systems into Planning.

Planning and policy decisions in the Boulder Valley shall be approached through an ecosystem framework, in which natural regions like airsheds and watersheds are incorporated into planning, and an appropriate relationship between the built environment and air, water, and land quality is considered.

### 4.02 Regional Outreach.

The City and the County shall take a regional approach to environmental planning and resource management, support consideration of environmental impacts in local and regional decisions, and work with governmental entities outside the Boulder Valley on issues of common concern.

### 4.03 Environmental Education.

The City and the County shall promote public education about issues of local and regional environmental concern, and seek to engage all citizens in the goal of protecting the quality of the natural and built environment.

### 4.04 Assessment of Environmental Impact.

The community and environmental effects of public and private projects shall be considered in the public decision making process. Local, state, and federal environmental review processes shall be followed when appropriate.

### 4.05 Monitoring and Tracking.

The City and the County shall continue to improve monitoring and evaluation of air, and water quality, and shall track

progress made in maintaining and enhancing environmental quality in the Boulder Valley.

# 6. Transportation

*As in other cities throughout the United States, the dominant mode of transportation in the Boulder Valley is the automobile. Population densities are low, and the compact form of the City allows relatively quick auto driving time. However, continued primary reliance on the automobile to meet growing transportation needs is likely to worsen air pollution, increase traffic congestion and noise to levels that most residents consider unacceptable, require widening of streets, and increase the amount of land devoted to road rights-of-way. The City is committed to improve travel modes that offer an alternative to the automobile and to provide adequate financing for these alternatives and the improvement and maintenance of the street network.*

## 6.01 All-Mode Transportation System.

The Boulder Valley shall be served by an integrated all-mode transportation system. The City and the County shall strive to eliminate conflicts between auto, transit, bicycle, and pedestrian users and provide safe and convenient connections between modes.

## 6.02 Reduction of Single Occupancy Auto Trips.

The City and the County shall promote greater use of car pools and alternatives to single occupancy automobile travel with the target of achieving a 15 percent overall reduction in the ratio of single occupancy auto trips to all trips by 2010.

## 6.03 Convenience and Safety.

The City and the County shall strive to make bicycling, walking, and transit more convenient and safe. The City shall strive to provide accessible and affordable transit service, developed with the County, RTD, other agencies, and private providers.

## 6.04 Employee Incentive Programs.

The City shall work cooperatively with the County and other entities such as the University of Colorado, the Boulder Valley School District, and private employers to design complementary strategies for achieving the desired modal shift.

## 6.05 Accessibility.

The City shall make special efforts to provide transportation services and facilities for mobility-impaired persons, as well as youth, elderly, and low-income persons.

## 6.06 Road System Improvements.

In order to protect previous investments and ensure efficient use of the road system, the City and the County shall give high priority to ongoing maintenance, safety improvements, and capacity improvements for existing road facilities which are relatively small projects (such as signalization, adding turn lanes, and signage) that increase level of service without adding through-lanes.

# B. Subcommunity and Area Plans

*In 1989, a gap in the City's mid-level planning was identified. Boulder had been successful in planning at the broad, comprehensive planning scale, and at the site-specific planning scale, but not at the "in-between", or mid-level planning scale. Subcommunity and area planning were recommended as a way to better address issues at this scale. Subcommunity and area planning add a new dimension to the City's long range planning efforts.*

## Subcommunities within the Service Area

### Subcommunity Plans

Included in the Service Area are nine subcommunity planning areas: Central Boulder, Crossroads, the University of Colorado, East Boulder, Southeast Boulder, South Boulder, North Boulder, Palo Park, and Gunbarrel (see Map 3 on the next page for subcommunity boundaries). The subcommunities are areas defined by natural or physical separation, or by existing character. While functionally interrelated, the subcommunities are somewhat independent areas for purposes of planning on a more detailed level than the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

## Areas within a Subcommunity

### Area Plans

Within each subcommunity are one or more distinct areas that may consist of neighborhoods, employment centers, retail centers or a combination of these. Area plans may address planning issues at a more detailed level than the Comprehensive Plan or the subcommunity plans or they may serve to explore new land use ideas for small areas or neighborhoods. Area plan boundaries will be defined as planning issues arise.

The following sections more specifically define subcommunity and area plans and summarize the purpose, contents, process, implementation, and adoption of the plans. The goals of the plans and criteria for selecting plans are also described.

# The Subcommunity Plan

### Definition

Subcommunities are distinct areas within the Service Area of the City as defined by physical boundaries such as roads, district development character, and common public facilities such as schools and parks.

### Purpose

Subcommunity plans provide an agreement among citizens and city departments on the future development of the subcommunity, create a common understanding among residents of expected changes in the area, and develop implementation methods for achieving the goals of the subcommunity plan. A plan will be developed for each subcommunity in the Service Area. Because of factors such as imminence of change expected in an area, however, an area plan or plans might be developed before its subcommunity is planned.

### Content/Process

A subcommunity plan will look at the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan land use designations, existing land uses, and current zoning for the area and evaluate the potential build-out of the subcommunity relative to the goals of the

Comprehensive Plan and desired future of the subcommunity. As part of subcommunity plans, techniques to provide stability in existing residential neighborhoods will be developed. Subcommunity plans will be developed with substantial input from those who reside in, conduct business in, and own property in the subcommunity. In areas involving County jurisdiction, subcommunity planning will be a joint effort by City and County staff.

The area chosen for an area plan has an identity that is distinct from other areas within the subcommunity and typically consists of predominantly residential uses or predominantly commercial or employment uses.

### Purpose

Area plans will be developed only for areas with special problems or opportunities which are not adequately

addressed by comprehensive planning, subcommunity planning or by existing land use regulations. They will be developed with substantial input from residents and property owners in and near the area.

### Content/Process

Area plans will address a finer grain of planning issues than subcommunity plans. An area plan may be preceded by an analysis to explore options and define possible objectives.

Implementation techniques will be identified for the area and will include but not be limited to: neighborhood improvement projects, trail, park or street projects, changes to the land use regulations or zoning districts.

Area plans will be adopted by Planning Board and City Council, in the same fashion as the subcommunity plans. ## **The Pearl Street Corridor**

The Pearl Street Corridor links the Downtown Activity Center with the Boulder Valley Regional Activity Center, between 18th Street and Folsom. The Corridor is half a block wide along Pearl Street, and is separated by alleys from residential neighborhoods to the north and south.

The vision for the Pearl Street Corridor is to become an interesting and varied mixed use area, combining urban-density housing with small-scale retail uses and office space. The scale of new buildings will be sensitive in use and design to the adjacent residential uses. The present zoning allows a mix of uses, although changes which provide stronger incentives for mixed use projects should be considered.

The HOP shuttle has provided improved access and visibility to the Pearl Street Corridor. The planning challenge is now to create and strengthen the pedestrian environment along the street.

## **Community Hospital/Community Plaza**

The Community Hospital/Community Plaza neighborhood is just north of the downtown on Broadway. The hospital, and nearby related facilities, provide both general and specialized health services to the Boulder Valley. Two small shopping centers across Broadway from the hospital provide day-to-day shopping and restaurants for neighborhood residents, nearby workers, and visitors to the area, providing a lively neighborhood center. As a result of the impact of the medical facilities and shopping centers on adjacent residential areas, special considerations must be incorporated in the design of commercial expansions and physical improvements to adjacent residential streets.

## **Mixed Density Residential Neighborhoods**

Among the conclusions of the Integrated Planning Project was a commitment to preserve downtown neighborhoods at their present character and density. To this end, the 1995-96 update to the Comprehensive Plan introduces the concept of mixed density neighborhoods.

Although the majority of the housing surrounding the downtown and the University was built as single-family units in the early part of this century, most of the downtown neighborhoods have been zoned for medium and high density residential use. Some of the original homes have been demolished and replaced with apartments; others have been renovated into several units; and many remain as single family homes.

The goal of the mixed density neighborhood concept is to maintain the mix of densities in most of the older residential neighborhoods. The intent is to develop a new zoning district for the area. This change may ease parking problems and help to maintain the desirable mix of renter and owner occupancy that makes these neighborhoods liveable and special.

The stability and liveability of these neighborhoods is a priority as the City considers requests for Residential Permit Parking districts and a variety of traffic mitigation projects. The parks bond issue approved by voters in 1995 will provide funding for additional neighborhood and pocket parks to serve these older, established neighborhoods.

## **High Density Zone (HZ-E)**

High density zoning will remain in some of the neighborhoods of the downtown and University Hill. In these areas, there is the dual intention of allowing selective redevelopment to occur while protecting the basic character and integrity of the neighborhoods. To this end, the City Council has adopted the HZ-E Zone. A review for design compatibility and the adequacy of parking is mandatory if a development is to reach the full allowed density.

Transportation improvement districts, co-funded by the City and area property owners, have been completed for the high density areas of Goss-Grove, Whittier, West Pearl and South Whittier. In addition to paving and sidewalk reconstruction, these projects have included street closures and street redesign to slow traffic, pocket parks, landscaped medians, bike lanes, bus shelters and neighborhood identification signs.

## I. Open Space Program Summary

### Background Information

The City's Open Space Program was established in 1967 to acquire, preserve, protect, and manage the open space Boulder was buying on behalf of its citizens. As a result of a 1967 municipal election, Boulder became the first city in the country to tax itself specifically for the acquisition, management and maintenance of open space. The measure to add 4/10 of one cent sales tax passed by a 57 percent majority, an overwhelming demonstration of support in an era of citizen tax concern. Shortly after the election, the City Manager and City Council appointed an Advisory Committee on Open Space, and in 1973, Council created the Open Space Board of Trustees to set policies and priorities for acquisition of greenbelt lands. In 1986, a charter amendment providing more permanent protection for open space lands was adopted with passage by 79 percent of the voters. In 1989, 3/10 of one cent was added to the sales tax for a period of 15 years to accelerate open space preservation. This measure was supported by 76 percent of the voters.

The Open Space Board of Trustees reviews and makes recom-

mendations concerning open space-related acquisition and management activities. The Open Space Board of Trustees vigorously pursues the implementation of the open space elements of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and the acquisition of properties required to fulfill the goals of the Open Space Program. Means of implementation may include, without limitation, the acquisition of land, development rights, density transfer, and gifts of land or interests in land to the program.

The Open Space charter defines the purposes of open space. Open space land shall be acquired, maintained, preserved, retained, and used only for the following purposes:

- (1) Preservation or restoration of natural areas characterized by or including: terrain; geologic formations, flora, or fauna that are unusual, spectacular, or historically important; water resources; scenic areas and/or vistas; or wildlife habitats or fragile ecosystems.
- (2) Preservation of water resources in their natural or traditional state, scenic areas or vistas, wildlife habitats, or fragile ecosystems;

*A new Parks and Recreation Master Plan was adopted in December 1996. This summary will be updated as part of the next Comprehensive Plan Annual Update.*

### Background Information

The Parks and Recreation Master Plan was adopted in 1980 and revised in 1988. The main objective of the Boulder Parks and Recreation Master Plan is to provide parks and recreation programs to serve the needs of the citizens of the City of Boulder. The basic fabric of the parks and recreation system is the neighborhood and community parks. Other components of the City's park and recreation system include regional parks, park corridors, preserves, athletic complexes, recreation centers and various special use facilities such as tennis courts, handball/racquetball facilities, and golf courses.

### Service Standards

#### Neighborhood Parks

The standard for neighborhood parks is a minimum of 5 to 10 acres located within 1/2 to 3/4 miles of the population served. The optimum location is in the center of the neighborhood served and adjacent to an elementary school site. Pedestrian and bicycle access is emphasized with minimal, if any, space allocated to the automobile. Typical types of elements that might be included in the design of a neighborhood park are children's playgrounds, turf areas for informal open play space, picnic and passive areas, park shelter buildings, play courts, and landscaping. The size and distribution standards outlined above result in 1.5 acres per 1000 population in the neighborhood park category.

#### Community Parks

Community parks are 50 to 75 acres in size, located within 1-1/2 to 3 miles of the population served, and in an area that is readily accessible by the major modes of transportation. The

design should accommodate the recreational needs of all age groups within the community to be served, and provide a wide variety of both active and passive uses. Applying the size and distribution standards outlined above, will result in 1.5 acres per 1000 population in the community park category.

Typical elements of a community park design might include turfed and landscaped areas for picnicking, walking, sitting, and other passive enjoyment activities as well as the more active play fields, play courts, playgrounds, racquet facilities, a swimming pool, and a recreation center building. Unique and one of a kind facilities may also be included such as small lakes, natural features, community gardens, or a special recreational facility. Community parks also meet the neighborhood park needs for the residents within a 3/4 mile radius provided reasonable pedestrian access is available.

#### Regional Parks

Regional parks serve the entire city, and to some degree, the population outside of the City within an hour driving time of such parks. These parks are normally located to protect or preserve some valuable recreational resource, such as a lake or mountain environments, and the recreational use normally centers around these resources. Other recreational facilities such as picnic areas, trails, nature centers, golf courses, botanical gardens, and playfields may also be accommodated if the site is suitable for such development.

These areas are typically linear in character, generally following sensitive environmental corridors such as streams and ridgelines. Park corridors provide linkages between other park areas and serve as pedestrian ways with trails to link various areas within the community. ## Background Information

The City of Boulder Fire Department is responsible for the protection of life and property through fire prevention, fire suppression, and emergency medical and rescue services. The Fire Department also provides conceptual review and long-range and short-range planning for fire service needs. The Department has 83 full-time positions and six fire stations, strategically located to serve the entire City. The stations are staffed and equipped to respond either to all types of fires or emergencies or solely to residential fires or emergencies.

Central Station - 2441 13th Street: full service

Station 2 - 2225 Baseline: full service

Station 3 - 1580 30th Street: full service

Station 4 - 4100 Darley: residential service

Station 5 - 2200 Norwood: residential service

Station 6 - 5145 North 63rd Street: full service

Additionally, the Fire Department participates in a countywide Joint Training Center which has many different training facilities. The training tower includes stairways which reverse, rooms that can be subdivided differently, different building structure and roof types, classrooms with projectors for fire and rescue simulations, a support building with a flammable liquid pit, an old tanker for flammable liquid spill training, and a pumping pit with underground water storage.

The annual number of fire calls is reduced from the decade of the 1970s. Medical calls now number more than twice the actual fire calls, and account for 58 percent of all calls in 1988.

## Service Standards

One of the major standards for the Fire Department is to have a response time to the location of an emergency that is normally less than six minutes from the time the call is received by the dispatch center (see Fire Facilities and Response Map). Based upon this standard, the Fire Department's level of service is adequate throughout most of the City. However, there are a few areas where service problems occur and/or additional facilities are needed. The approach to providing emergency services is based on the following principles:

## Shared Responsibility Between Private Sector and City Fire Department

The City emphasizes private sector self-protection through code regulations and design incentives. Sprinklers are now required by ordinance for many uses.

## Balance Between Manual Fire Protection and Built-in Fire Protection

Municipal fire protection requires a balance between fire protection provided by stations, apparatus and personnel and fire protection provided by built-in automatic fire systems. While automatic systems provide a high degree of protection, a city requires a minimum number of stations, apparatus and personnel to protect the city from fires not originating in protected spaces and to provide acceptable levels of service for non-fire emergencies and events.

## Protection Placed Close to the User

Small stations are located throughout the City so a quick response time can be achieved. Light and fast vehicles, "attack squad pumpers," are supplied to peripheral stations for quick response time to primarily residential areas.

## Generalist Theory of Operations

The Fire Department believes that the apparatus should have diverse supplies and equipment and the fire fighters should be generalists rather than specialists. Due to this philosophy, every piece of fire equipment has fire fighting, medical and rescue supplies and equipment and all fire fighters are trained in numerous skills. Each fire fighter must pass a comprehensive training program which focuses on fire fighting, emergency medical training, hazardous materials response, rescue training for vehicles, ice, water and fire emergencies, and each fire fighter must be a state certified emergency medical technician.

## Basic Life Level of Emergency Medical Service

In addition to providing fire suppression, fire prevention and rescue services, the Fire Department is responsible for providing Basic Life Support (BLS) services. The philosophy behind this decision is based upon the facts that the Fire Department often arrives on the scene of an emergency before an ambulance and that life support care within the first few minutes of a serious injury can save many lives. Therefore, the Fire Department is charged with supplying BLS care until the first A-I Ambulance arrives. Once the ambulance arrives, the patient is given treatment at the paramedic level and taken to a hospital for further medical treatment.

The system of emergency medical care is a multi-tiered system involving the City Fire Department, the A-I Ambulance service, and the Boulder Community Hospital, each offering a more advanced degree of life support.

# 6. Water Utility Master Plans and Program Summary

## Background Information

The City gets its water from three primary sources: Boulder Creek water rights, Colorado-Big Thompson (CBT) project water deliveries, and Windy Gap project water deliveries.

(1) Boulder Creek Water Rights

The City's water rights in the Boulder Creek basin include direct use and storage rights on both Middle and North Boulder Creek, and an exchange right. The City's exchange right allows the City to release water into lower Boulder Creek near 75th Street from Boulder and Baseline Reservoirs in exchange for increased diversion at the City's direct use and storage points on Middle and North Boulder Creeks. All of the City's rights are absolute except for conditional storage rights for the Park and Bradley Reservoir sites in the North Boulder Creek basin.

(2) The Colorado-Big Thompson (CBT) Project

Boulder also receives water delivered by the CBT project from the Colorado River into Boulder Reservoir via reservoirs, tunnels and canals. The City owns 21,000 CBT project shares out of a total of 310,000 shares. CBT deliveries to Boulder can only be made from April through October of each year due to winter operating limitations on canals. Boulder uses CBT water for direct treatment at the Boulder Reservoir plant and as a source of exchange water.

(3) The Windy Gap Project

The Windy Gap project, can deliver 48,000 acre feet (AF)/year of Colorado River water to municipal and industrial water users on the East Slope via CBT facilities. The City has an allotment contract for 37 units which yields 3,700 AF/Year. Windy Gap water is unique among Boulder's supplies because the return flows (wastewater effluent and lawn watering runoff) from this source can be reused. This makes this source relatively more valuable because the reuse rights to the water can be sold to others.

Flows in the Boulder Creek and Colorado River basins are highly variable from year to year. Because of this, deliveries from Boulder's three major water sources into its system also vary. From a water

rights viewpoint, the City has sufficient water supply holdings to meet its ultimate municipal water needs, given the extent of the current BVCP planning area.

Boulder's water supply system includes several storage, conveyance and treatment facilities. The City owns approximately 7,200 AF of storage rights in its North Boulder Creek watershed, and has contracted for rights to 8,000 AF and 8,500 AF in Barker and Boulder Reservoirs, respectively.

The Middle Boulder Creek facilities include storage, direct flow and exchange rights at Barker Reservoir. Water is transported by pipeline to Kessler Reservoir, and then by pipeline to the Public Service Company hydroelectric plant (5 miles west of Boulder in Boulder Canyon). A bypass pipeline at the Public Service Company hydroelectric plant delivers water to the city-owned Betasso Hydroelectric Plant and Betasso Water Treatment Plant.

The North Boulder Creek facilities include seven mountain dams and reservoirs within the City's watershed. Water is transported via open channel and the Silver Lake Pipeline to Lakewood Reservoir, a small reservoir north of Nederland adjacent to the Peak-to-Peak Highway. From Lakewood Reservoir water is transported via the 9-1/2 mile long Lakewood Pipeline to the Betasso Water Treatment Plant. The treated water facilities include the water treatment plants, storage tanks, pump stations, hydroelectric facilities, pressure reducing station and the transmission/distribution lines (water mains).

## Master Plans

The City of Boulder's Water Utility operates two water treatment plants - the Betasso Water Treatment Plant and the Boulder Reservoir Water Treatment Plant. Mountain water from the City's watershed and Barker Lake is treated at Betasso, which has a capacity of 46 million gallons per day (MGD). Water available from the Colorado-Big Thompson Project and the Windy Gap Project is treated at Boulder Reservoir, which has a capacity of 10 mgd. Site constraints at the mountainous Betasso plant preclude the realistic expansion of this facility, so all future water treatment expansion will occur at Boulder Reservoir.

The City operates eight storage tanks, ranging in capacity from 2.0 million gallons(MG) to 9.5 MG, with a total storage capacity of 41.4 MG.

Due to the geographic location of Boulder, the Water Utility serves low areas adjacent to Boulder Creek in the eastern section to high areas against the mountains in the western section. To adequately serve this wide variation in ground elevation, the water distribution system is divided into three pressure zone areas.

There are eight pressure regulating stations in the system that regulate pressure and flow between pressure zones or into storage reservoirs.

There are four booster pumping stations in the system, as well as a pumping station at the Boulder Reservoir Water Treatment Plant. These booster pumping stations are able to pump water from a lower elevation pressure zone into a higher elevation pressure zone.

The City operates four hydroelectric facilities on treated water transmission lines (one raw water transmission line and two more are in the planning stages). The hydroelectric facilities use the power generated from the elevation differential to produce electricity, which is then sold to Public Service Company.

# 7. Wastewater Utility Master Plans and Program Summary

## Background Information

The City's Wastewater Utility is responsible for providing reliable wastewater service, as desired by the community, in a manner which emphasizes efficient management of fiscal and natural resources, and protects human and environmental health. The City's wastewater collection system consists primarily of gravity sewers of various sizes that collect and convey wastewater from residential, commercial and industrial customers to the City's 75th Street Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP). In addition, a pump station and force main is operated in the Gunbarrel area to convey wastewater that cannot flow by gravity to the WWTP.

Treated liquid effluent is discharged to Boulder Creek, and biosolids produced at the wastewater treatment plant are hauled away and are surface applied to farmland and open space.

The major master plans dealing with aspects of the wastewater utility include the following:

- Wastewater Collection System Master Plan - 1983
- Wastewater Treatment Facilities Plan - 1990

The Wastewater Collection System Master Plan provides for the logical extension of the sewer system in Planning Areas I and II when development occurs.

The Wastewater Treatment Facilities Plan evaluated the existing wastewater treatment process in response to the 1987 National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit which imposed more stringent effluent standards including ammonia.

In the process of evaluating the relative impact of ammonia, it was found that the lower sections of Boulder Creek, specifically those between one half mile east of the WWTP and the Coal Creek drainage, were highly disturbed by land use practices. Agriculture, livestock grazing, and gravel mining in particular had resulted in severe disruption of the natural stream bed and related aquatic zone, which had reduced the stream's ability to support aquatic life. In studying remedial measures required to restore the ecosystem, consultants found that these improvements coincided with those which would reduce the impact of un-ionized ammonia within the Creek, thus decreasing the need for additional treatment at the WWTP. Creek restoration would decrease the wide swings of temperature and pH in the water, thus reducing the conversion of ammonia to its toxic un-ionized form. It would also restore the Creek's ability to support a variety of fish, as well as a diverse riparian ecosystem.

An additional study evaluated existing sludge treatment and disposal facilities, and assessed alternative approaches to biosolids recycling or disposal to meet the future needs to the year 2010. The conclusion of the study was that the approach of sludge stabilization through anaerobic digestion, followed by recycling through land application to agricultural sites or open space, was the most beneficial and cost-effective approach.

The City's wastewater collection system is designed to convey all authorized wastewater to the WWTP at the flow rate required by the City's customers, precluding backups and spills that would endanger human health, personal property and the environment. In the case of certain commercial and industrial customers, pretreatment of the wastewater is required to reduce the concentration of chemical constituents that may adversely impact the treatment ability of the WWTP.

State stream classifications set the amount of various chemical substances allowed in streams, and discharge permits are issued to assure compliance by point sources such as wastewater treatment plants. The City has recently completed major improvements to the WWTP to assure compliance with the discharge permit, including restoration of lower Boulder Creek.

## Future Service Projections and Programs

There are no known major deficiencies in the City's wastewater collection system that require rehabilitation or improvement. Most existing sewers have sufficient reserve capacity to accommodate future growth and redevelopment. Infiltration of groundwater is prevalent in certain areas and inflow of rainwater causes major flow increases at the WWTP and surcharging of the collection system during wet weather periods. The Utilities Division has recently developed a computerized hydraulic model of the wastewater for collection system and is evaluating surcharging problems and the adequacy of this system on an on-going basis.

The recent improvements to the WWTP should provide adequate treatment of the City's wastewater through the year 2010 provided the current discharge permit does not change substantially. However, it is likely that the discharge permit requirements will become more stringent. The City's current permit expires in 1998 and a new permit will be issued by the State. It is anticipated that new limits for total ammonia will be more stringent and will require the City to further improve the treatment process. Potential concerns related to nitrate, phosphorus and heavy metals may also require additional treatment.

# 8. Flood Control and Drainage Utility Master Plan and Program Summary

## Background Information

The City of Boulder established a Storm Water and Flood Management Utility in 1973 to focus responsibility and accountability for storm water management and flood control programs and provide a means of funding. Monthly service charges were instituted in 1974 and have provided the primary sources of funding since that time, enabling the City to plan, regulate, maintain, and improve the drainage system.

Over the years maintenance efforts have gradually increased with funding and knowledge of system needs. A substantial amount of time and money has been invested in storm water and flood management planning. Drainage policies and plans have been completed for Boulder Creek, major tributaries, the Boulder Gunbarrel area, and the storm water collection system. The City is an active member in the Urban Drainage and Flood Control District as well as participating in the Federal National Flood Insurance Program.

In addition, the Federal Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has promulgated new rules and policies for the issuance of storm water discharge permits intended to reduce the environmental impacts of storm water discharges on receiving waters. These rules are expected to place new obligations on the City.

The completion of the plans, each suggesting needed projects, and EPA rules emphasizing water quality, led the Storm Water and Flood Management Utility to prepare the Comprehensive Drainage Utility Master Plan (CDUMP), which was adopted by the City Council in 1989. Through CDUMP, drainage projects are prioritized, a funding source is defined, programs for water quality and maintenance are outlined, and administration and staffing are provided.

A reduction to life-safety hazards and property damage, as well as improving water quality, are the main purposes of the plans and projects proposed by CDUMP. The City regulates, the use and construction within the area which could be expected to be inundated by a 100-year flood. This floodplain, for purposes of regulation, as well as for determining capital project priority, is divided into the flood storage area, the flood

conveyance zone and the high hazard area.

For purposes of designing capital projects, the City will apply an additional cost-benefit standard. For example, the City may consider improvements to less than a 100-year standard in some cases depending upon the cost of the project compared to the risk to lives or property.

## Future Service Projections and Programs

The City of Boulder and Boulder County, with the Urban Drainage and Flood Control District, have completed a number of drainage reports for creeks, tributaries, and storm water collection systems within the Boulder Valley. Approval of CDUMP authorized a new rate and fee structure to build the improvements recommended in the drainage reports. Future implementation activities include:

- (1) The program to manage the City's floodplain has four elements: 1) a strong ordinance regulating use and construction; 2) the promotion of federally-subsidized flood insurance; 3) a flood hazard early warning system for South Boulder and Boulder Creeks; and 4) the maintenance of existing drainage way capacities, modification of man-made obstructions, and restoration of historic drainage courses.
- (2) The highest priority capital improvement project is the completion of the Goose Creek Channel from 30th Street through Folsom Street. Other smaller, localized drainage problems will be addressed depending upon the availability of funds.
- (3) The utility is committed to being a partner with other city departments in the development of the Tributary Greenway project. Flood control, as well as transportation and recreation needs, will be served through the development of a trail system along the tributaries to Boulder Creek.
- (4) Property in the flood plain, especially the high hazard zone, will be purchased, within funding limitations, both pre-flood and post-flood modes.

# 3. Environmental Resources

## a. Significant Mineral Resources

### Background

In June of 1973, the Colorado Legislature amended portions of Title 34 of the Colorado Statutes by enacting House Bill 1529. Part 3 of Article I states the legislative intent of the bill: "The General Assembly hereby declares that: a) The State's commercial mineral deposits are essential to the State's economy; b) The populous counties of the State face a critical shortage of such deposits; c) Such deposits should be extracted according to a rational plan, calculated to avoid waste of such deposits and cause the least practicable disruption to the ecology and quality of life for the citizens of the populous counties of the State."

Colorado Statutes define "commercial mineral deposit" as "a natural mineral deposit of limestone used for construction purposes, coal, sand, gravel and quarry aggregate, for which extraction by an extractor is or will be commercially feasible and regarding which it can be demonstrated by geologic, mineralogical, or other scientific data that such deposit has significant or strategic value to the area, state or nation."

House Bill 1529 instructed the planning commission for each city and county, city or town, within each populous county of the State, to conduct a study of the commercial mineral deposits located within their jurisdictions and develop a master plan for their extraction. Pursuant to the legislation, the City of Boulder prepared a Master Plan for Mineral Extraction in 1977. Commercial mineral deposits were inventoried throughout the Boulder Valley. The inventory was then combined with planning criteria, resulting in the final master plan.

### Limestone

Within the Boulder Valley, limestone has only been mined and kilned for agricultural purposes. The intent of the State legislation was to protect deposits of limestone used as concrete aggregate. A master plan for limestone extraction, therefore, was not prepared for the Boulder Valley.

### Coal

The mining of sub-bituminous coal has taken place in the area surrounding Marshall since the mid - 1880s. Marshall lies at the southwestern terminus of the larger Boulder-Weld County coal field. Relatively low energy-yielding coals have been extracted by small underground mines as recently as 1946.

The Master Plan for Mineral Extraction found no coal extraction occurring in the Boulder Valley and also came to the conclusion that the coals of the Boulder Valley were not commercially extractable in 1977 or within a 15 year time period.

However, the Plan recognized that changes in technology and energy prices could affect future coal extraction profitability. The Plan recommended that, if the coals of the Boulder Valley do become commercially extractable, a Master Plan for Coal Extraction be prepared.

### Oil and Gas

Boulder has a history of oil production. Because of changes in technology and recent additional discoveries within Boulder County, it is logical to expect future rejuvenation of production activity within the Comprehensive Plan area. The Plan should not preclude the possibility of temporary drilling and longer-term production, storage, and transport situations.

### Quarry Aggregate

Three types of high quality potential crushed rock aggregate exist within the Boulder Valley:

- (1) Quartz Latite intruded into the foothills hogbacks;
- (2) Shoshonite intruded to form Valmont Dike; and
- (3) Metaquartzite in Eldorado Springs Canyon.

In each case, the environmental costs of production were judged too great and no plan for mineral extraction was prepared for quarry aggregate.

### Sand and Gravel Deposits Map

The one commercial mineral that is commercially and environmentally viable in the Boulder Valley is its sand and gravel deposits. The Master Plan for Mineral Extraction contains a plan for sand and gravel extraction. Part of this plan is a map of potentially commercial, first class sand and gravel deposits. This map was formulated by applying criteria for the evaluation of potentially extractable deposits to an inventory prepared for the Boulder Valley. These criteria are as follows:

- (1) A deposit must be:
  - (a) a high quality first grade deposit;
  - (b) larger than 20 acres in area;
  - (c) physically situated for effective reclamation and multiple-sequential use.
- (2) Extraction of a deposit must not:
  - (a) present a significant potential safety hazard to a major public transportation facility;
  - (b) increase capital facility costs to the public;
  - (c) damage neighboring potable surface or groundwater supplies;
  - (d) significantly increase the consumption of other natural resources, energy resources, or human resources;
  - (e) significantly reduce the quality of life for neighboring residents; or
  - (f) present a potential for significant unmitigable environmental impacts.

## b. Geological Development Constraints

### Background

Several constraints to development due to geological hazards exist in the Boulder Valley. In 1977 the City of Boulder Urban Geology program identified hazards which could represent a potential threat to property and life safety.

### Potential Mass Movement Hazard

- (1) Material Movement - Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of material movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.
- (2) Slope Instability - Land slides are evident throughout Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure if modified by development.
- (3) Alluvial Fans - Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, evident along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to 20 feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating event, such as the Big Thompson flood of August 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan.
- (4) Earth Flows - Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Boulders exceeding 25 feet in diameter have been deposited on the sediment surface bordering the foothills.

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to 29 feet in diameter in silt and clay-sized mud.

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation. Saturated accumulations of debris, along and within the foothills, liquefy and flow down stream channels and out across the mesa and valley surfaces. An earth flow would be extremely destructive in developed areas.

(5) Slope Debris - The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes.

(6) Swell Potential - Swelling soils are soils or soft bedrock which increase in volume as they get wet and shrink as they dry out. They are also commonly known as bentonite, expansive, or montmorillonitic soils.

Swelling soils contain a high percentage of certain kinds of clay particles which are capable of absorbing large quantities of water. Soil volume may expand 10 percent or more as the clay becomes wet. The powerful force of expansion is capable of exerting pressures of 20,000 pounds per square foot or greater on foundations, slabs, or other confining structures. Subsurface Colorado swelling soils tend to remain at a constant moisture content in their natural state and are usually relatively dry at the outset of construction disturbance. Exposure to natural or man-caused water sources during or after development results in swelling. In many instances, the soils do not regain their original dryness after construction, but remain somewhat moist and expanded due to the changed environment.

(7) Consolidation Potential - Consolidation of soils occurs when relatively low density materials shrink in volume when they become wet or are subjected to great weight from buildings or road fill or other construction activity. These are also known as collapsing and settling soils.

Collapsing and settling soils have considerable strength when dry and generally are not a problem to structures and improvements. When they become wet, they are subject to rapid collapse and can be reduced in volume as much as 10 to 15 percent. Surface ground displacement of several feet can result.

Human activities, such as irrigation, installation of utilities, impoundment of water and blockage of natural drainage-ways, as well as construction of buildings, result in the collapse and settlement of these soils. This can result in damage to private property and public improvements. Similar processes frequently affect old landfills or poorly placed earth fills.

(8) Potential Mine-Related Subsidence - Ground subsidence due to the collapse of coal mine voids has occurred in the Boulder Valley. Potentially devastating effects of collapse are graphically evident on the City-owned Richardson parcel northeast of the intersection of Cherryvale Road and Marshall Road. The surrounding lands are underlain by the old Eagle Mine, which closed in 1936. Linear trench-like depressions are evident where collapse has occurred subsequent to mine abandonment. A large percentage of the land in the Davidson Mesa area has been undermined.

- (4) Red Rocks - Spectacular slabs of once nearly flat-lying sedimentary Fountain Formation which have been tilted to a nearly vertical position by the uplift of the Rocky Mountains.
- (5) Echo Rocks - The resistant ridge forming Echo Rocks is made up of the Dakota sandstone - a rock laid down when a huge inland sea covered the area nearly 135 million years ago. Extensive beaches formed from an almost pure quartz sand, and later hardened into the Dakota Formation. Ripple marks can be seen in the sandstone near the water tank west of the National Center for Atmospheric Research. The Dakota Ridge can be traced from north of Colorado Springs to the Wyoming border.

## d. Wetlands

Wetlands are defined as those areas that are inundated or saturated by surface or groundwater enough to support vegetation typically adapted to wet soil conditions. A wetland has certain characteristics that distinguish it from other natural ecosystems. Wetland soils contain little or no oxygen and are saturated for varying periods of time during the growing season. Certain plants are adapted to living in wet, low-oxygen conditions and thrive in wetland areas. Cattails, rushes, willows, sedges, and cottonwoods are examples of wetland plants typically found in the Northern Great Plains and Rocky Mountains. When compared to other natural habitats in the region, wetlands support a greater number of bird, mammal, and amphibian species.

Wetlands are well known for the role they play in protecting water quality, but they also provide a broad range of other functions of value to the community. Wetlands are known to be critical in the functions of:

**• Ground water recharge/discharge**

*Wetlands allow for the gradual recharge of aquifers, regulate groundwater levels and moderate flows into and out of underground water supplies.*

**• Flood water retention/detention/storage**

*Wetlands in floodplains and along rivers form natural floodways that convey flood waters from upstream to downstream points.*

**• Shore-line anchoring**

*Wetlands along lake shores and creek banks reduce the impact of wave action and stabilize the shorelines.*

**• Sediment trapping**

*By reducing flood flows and the velocity of floods, wetlands reduce the amount of sediment carried by rivers and creeks.*

**• Nutrient retention**

*Wetlands act as natural filters, removing nutrients and some chemical contaminants from surface and ground water, thereby providing rich habitat for plant growth. This is an important water quality function; wetlands are places where plants can take up nutrients before they reach creeks, rivers, or lakes.*

**• Food chain support**

*Because of their high natural productivity, wetlands are the basis of far-reaching food chains and important areas for plant and animal diversity.*

**• Fish and wildlife habitat**

*Wetlands are essential habitat for waterfowl and other species of wildlife including amphibians, birds, mammals, and reptiles.*

Nearly 100 wetlands have been identified in Areas I and II of the Boulder Valley. These wetlands can be divided into three categories:

- natural;
- created by agricultural practices and gravel mining; or
- created by urban runoff.

Natural wetlands are 36 percent of the total wetland acreage identified while agricultural and gravel mining wetlands are 56 percent of the area. The remaining 8 percent of wetland area originated from urban runoff.

Most natural wetlands occur along the natural stream systems in oxbows, along shores, and in overflow areas. It may seem that because more than half of the Service Area's wetlands have been produced by human activity that more wetlands occur in the Boulder Valley now than prior to Euro-American settlement, but this is probably not true. Pre-settlement wetlands were probably found along streams and rivers and in landscape depressions. Many of these streams once had wide floodplains and meandered extensively. All streams and rivers in the study area, however, have been partially or totally channelized and their flows are now regulated. Abandoned or seldom used channels usually supported wetlands as well. However, most of these backwater areas, particularly along Boulder Creek, have been filled and the wetlands lost. Only one natural pond, Sombrero Marsh, occurs in the Service Area.

Agricultural and urban development have clustered along Boulder Creek throughout the Boulder Valley and most natural wetlands have been destroyed. Spring runoff which once inundated floodplains at the mountain front and on the Great Plains is retained in mountain reservoirs for municipal uses or diverted to ditches and canals for agricultural use. Seeps from ditches and reservoirs and extensive flood irrigation have created high water tables and wetlands in many parts of the Boulder Valley that were dry uplands in pre-settlement times. Municipal water ends up in sewer lines and is transported to wastewater treatment

plants or is street runoff. Urban wetlands are the result of the redistribution of street runoff, much of which has been diverted from streams.

The diversion of water for municipal and agricultural uses has created completely different patterns of water distribution, resulting in wetlands in different locations and of different types than occurred naturally in the Boulder Valley. Most likely, the area of wetlands supported now is similar to that in pre-settlement times, however, the types and functions of wetlands are probably very different. In pre-settlement times the majority of wetlands occurred in floodplains while the majority are now located away from floodplains. Some of the wetlands created by leaking reservoirs and ditches are among the largest and most biologically diverse wetlands in the Service Area.

Two functional values that have increased due to this change are increased habitat for waterfowl (created by open reservoirs) and active recreation.

In areas surrounded by development, wetlands provide a haven for a variety of wildlife. # e. Boulder Creek, Its Tributaries and Floodplains

## Background

Riparian areas are among the most diverse, dynamic, and complex terrestrial habitats on Earth. In semi-arid and arid regions of the country, such as Colorado, riparian ecosystems support over 70 percent of native wildlife species during some portion of their life-cycle. Yet, riparian and aquatic systems account for less than 3 percent of ecosystem types in the state. Preservation, restoration, and enhancement of these systems, therefore, are vitally important to maintaining biological diversity, water quality, and the overall ecological health of the environment.

Historically, creeks at the base of the foothills in the Boulder Valley were periodically flooded and scoured by snowmelt rushing out of the canyons and fanning out over the alluvial plain. As native plant and animal species of the foothills and high plains riparian areas evolved with this disturbance regime, many species became dependent upon it. Native cottonwoods and peach-leaf willows, for example, are dependent upon periodic flood scouring to create sand bars which are essential seed beds for germination and establishment of new populations.

Settlement of the foothills area in the Boulder Valley resulted in the dramatic alteration of riparian systems. Floodplains, which naturally spread out over much of the valley were greatly reduced by channelization of the streams and development of extensive ditch networks. No riparian system in the Boulder Valley has escaped impact by human activities. All streams have been partially or totally channelized and their flows have been considerably altered by upstream dams and ditch diversions.

Although urban creeks and wetlands have been greatly altered and degraded by human use, they are in many ways the lifeblood of the city's ecological processes. Creeks and their associated wetlands are primarily important for the following functions:

- Water quality protection (sediment trapping, nutrient retention and removal).
- Flood storage and groundwater recharge/discharge.
- Wildlife habitat and food chain support.
- Recreation and aesthetic value.

Boulder Creek and its tributaries are shown on Map 19. The initial settlement of the community along Boulder Creek established a pattern for similar residential, commercial, or industrial construction along other portions of Boulder Creek and within other flood areas in the Boulder Valley. As a result of private and public development over the floodplain, flood heights and velocities have increased and the potential for economic loss and concern for personal safety has increased. Boulder is particularly vulnerable to flood damage because of its unique geographic location. In addition, because of its proximity to the mouth of Boulder Canyon, the central area of the City is highly susceptible to the particular hazards of flash floods.

The conveyance zone is the area within the floodplain required for the passage or conveyance of the 100-year flood, where an increase in flood depths of one-half foot will result based on equal encroachment of the floodplain from the edges of the channel. The high hazard zone addresses life safety issues where the potential for washing people downstream in flood waters exists. In general, Boulder's floodplain regulations prohibit land uses for human occupancy in the high hazard zone.

Boulder Valley Natural Ecosystems are defined as places that support natural ecosystems of native plants and animals or possess important ecological, biological, or geological values. Boulder Valley Natural Ecosystems may also contain features

The Boulder Valley Natural Ecosystems Map provides a comprehensive, valley-wide view of natural systems and baseline information for long-range planning purposes. By providing a broad, qualitative overview of natural ecosystems in the Boulder Valley, the City and the County can continue to shape and further refine their efforts to protect the natural landscape. As more quantitative data are gathered and compiled about biological resources in the Boulder Valley, more specific habitat management, protection, and restoration guidelines will be outlined in natural resource management plans.

### 1. Great Plains Mixed Grass Prairie North Boulder Valley

This area is primarily characterized by native shortgrass and midgrass prairies on the mesas and outwash plains and tallgrass in the drainage swales and bottomlands. Significant wetlands occur in areas with seasonal moisture. The Boulder Reservoir supports numerous wetlands, including sedge meadows, cattail marshes, and lacustrine shorelines. The area supports extensive prairie dog colonies providing the prey base for more than ten species of raptors including nesting northern harriers and historical occurrences of burrowing owls. Wintering and migrating bald eagles use the perch and roost opportunities in the area as well. Rare plants occur on the Pierre shale outcrops along the mesas. Several woody draws support high densities of small mammals and birds.

### 2. Foothill Shrublands and Woodlands North Boulder Valley

This area north of Boulder represents the rich merger of Great Plains grasslands with the woodlands and shrublands of the Front Range foothills. Tallgrass and mixed-grass prairies blend into ponderosa pine savannahs and foothill shrublands of chokecherry, three-leaved sumac, and wild plum in woody draws. The steep gradient causes abundant biological diversity. The number of different kinds of habitats found in these areas support high numbers of native plants and animals. Rare plants occur on Pierre shale outcrops. Large predators (mountain lion and black bear) use the woody draws and rocky outcrops. Numerous mule deer are the principal prey species in this area. The grassy swales, woody draws, and streams and ponds are particularly important wildlife habitats. Rare reptiles and amphibians (e.g., prairie rattlesnake, tiger salamander) also occur in this area.

### 3. Montane Woodlands/Great Plains Mixed Grass Prairie

This area is one of the largest, relatively intact (i.e., few roads) areas in the County that preserves the transition from Great Plains grasslands to ponderosa pine savannahs, ponderosa pine forests, and ponderosa pine/douglas fir forests. The elevational gradient again supports abundant biological diversity. This area has one of the highest breeding bird densities in the state (98th percentile for breeding birds). Given the worldwide declines in numbers of neo-tropical migrant birds (birds which migrate to the tropics during the winter), this area has added importance for the birds which breed here. There is also a high density of nesting raptors (e.g., golden eagles, prairie falcons, and the federally-listed peregrine falcon) which are found nesting on craggy rock outcrops.

A high concentration of rare plants exists in this area. The federally listed Ute ladies'-tresses orchid is found unobtrusively flowering in wet riparian meadows. Several unique or representative geologic features such as the Flatirons, Red Rocks, Echo Rocks, Boulder Canyon, and Eldorado Canyon exhibit the geological history of the Boulder Valley. Large predator habitats supporting black bear and mountain lion link with habitats on County and State lands to the west.

### 4. Great Plains Shortgrass and Mixed Grass Prairie Complex - South Boulder

This expanse of primarily public land provides today's Boulder Valley resident with the feeling of the sweeping prairie expanses that existed prior to European settlement more than 150 years ago. Rolling topography supports complexes of short and mixed grass prairies. Several small streams carve the landscape, identified by their signature thin, narrow green ribbons of riparian forests. The numbers of plants and animals living in native prairie grasslands is truly astounding, and we're only just now finding out how important these areas are for maintaining healthy numbers of native plants and animals. The prairie dog is now recognized as a true "keystone" species for this area, as more than 160 different animals are known to depend upon thriving prairie dog colonies. Our chances of restoring and maintaining native prairies are probably best accomplished here due to the size of the area and the variety of ecological conditions.

### 5. South Boulder Creek Floodplain

Plains streams in the Boulder Valley received the greatest amount of disturbance and development during the past 150 years from damming, channelization, grazing, mining, and irrigation. Although this area has been similarly impacted, the South Boulder Creek floodplain is the best remaining example of a natural riparian corridor in the Boulder Valley. Historic meanders support extensive complexes of tallgrass prairie, sedge-dominated wetlands, and wet meadows. Floodplains of streams exiting the mountains are the real "wet environments" in this semi-arid climate. The South Boulder Creek floodplain provides habitats for many rare species — Ute ladies'-tresses orchid and Preble's meadow jumping mouse are among the rarest. The best examples of native tallgrass prairies in the Boulder Valley are located along South Boulder Creek.

Alteration of the hydrology of floodplains is the single most important impact of development on these areas. Historic wetlands which occurred throughout these extensive floodplains have largely disappeared due to grazing, development, irrigation, damming, and agriculture. Several floodplain areas of South Boulder Creek, however, have an inherent capacity for restoration, given the proximity to water and the relative lack of development impacts.

# B. Annexation Process

*It is anticipated in the Comprehensive Plan that Area IIIA shall be annexed to the City of Boulder within three years, consistent with the phased expansion of the City's capacity to provide adequate urban facilities and services. The following statements describe very briefly and very generally the City's present annexation policies and procedures, both of which may be modified from time to time without requiring an amendment of the Comprehensive Plan. ## State Law

Among other requirements, all annexations shall comply with requirements of Colorado State law which includes the following provisions:

- (1) Minimum Required Contiguity: At least one-sixth of the perimeter of the area to be annexed shall be contiguous to the city limits.
- (2) Annexation by Petition: A petition must be presented by more than half of the landowners owning more than 50 percent of the area to be annexed. For enclaves and municipally owned property, the City may take the initiative without petition.
- (3) Annexation by Election: Under certain conditions, an election may be held by the property owners and registered electors within the area to be annexed.

## Relationship to Existing City Boundaries and Consolidation of Petitions

The City may consolidate individual properties requesting annexation into larger aggregate parcels when the request areas are adjacent and such grouping is desirable because of contiguity, compactness, or logical extensions of then-existing city boundaries, or for the more efficient processing of numerous annexation requests.

## Zoning and Land Use

While zoning classifications in the County will be recognized, annexation into the City will need to conform to City zoning categories and the land use designations of the Comprehensive Plan.

## Environmental Features

Though detailed environmental impact statements generally will not be expected for lands within Area IIIA, commitments may be required at the time of annexation to protect parcels having special or unique natural features or hazards.

## Public Improvements

The following topics indicate the general terms and conditions that normally will be imposed for existing development at time of annexation. These conditions may vary to some degree depending upon the annexation of a particular area.

(1) Water and Ditch Rights: The City shall follow the procedures outlined in Ordinance No. 4351 concerning the acquisition of first right of refusal of water and ditch rights.

(2) Streets: The City will permit flexibility in the design and improvements of local streets when desired by local residents. As a condition of annexation, the City shall require the improvement of streets to levels which will ensure that the City will not incur extraordinary maintenance costs as a result of new annexations. The City will consider petitions from property owners to initiate a street improvement project at any time after annexation, so long as such project will not conflict with other scheduled capital improvements.

(3) Street Lights: Following annexation, street lights may be installed, upon petition of the residents of the area, subject to Public Service Company standards and approval.

(4) Water and Sewer: At the time of annexation, a property owner of property other than a large lot single-family residence shall connect to the City water and sewer if the lines abut the property. For a property owner of a large lot single-family residence that has an existing water and sewer system with no health-related problems, the property owner may continue use of the individual system until said system fails or needs upgrading.

(5) Payment of Water and Sewer Plant Investment Fees and Front Foot Charges: The City may consider financing and low-income deferrals for plant investment fees and front foot charges for previously developed property within the City and developed property annexing to the City. If the property is redeveloped or ownership changes, the outstanding charges will become due and payable at that time.

(6) Other Terms and Conditions: The items listed above will normally be imposed. Other conditions may be imposed by the City to meet the particular circumstances of any annexation and will be developed in a manner to allocate equitably the burdens, if any, of a particular annexation between the property owners and the City.

# D. Urban Service Criteria and Standards

## I. Purposes of Urban Service Standards

The urban service standards set the benchmark for providing a full range of urban services in the Boulder Valley. A basic premise of the Comprehensive Plan is that "adequate urban facilities and services" are a prerequisite for new urban development and that, within the Boulder Valley, the City of Boulder is the provider of choice for urban services since it can meet all the service provision requirements embodied in the urban service criteria and standards.

These standards are intended to be minimum requirements or thresholds for facilities and services that must be delivered to existing or new urban development to be considered adequate. These adequacy standards allow the County to determine if an urban level of services is met prior to approving new urban development in the unincorporated area, and they provide the City a basis for linking the phasing of growth to the planned provision of a full range of urban services in Area IIB/IIA, annexation, and capital improvement decisions.

The urban service standards for defining adequacy of urban services are included in this section of the Comprehensive Plan. They provide a tool for implementing Policy 3.02 in the Facilities and Services section of the Comprehensive Plan. Adequacy standards are included for those urban services that are required for urban expansion in Area II. These include: public water, public sewer, flood control and drainage, urban fire protection and emergency medical care, urban police protection, urban transportation, and developed urban parks.

## 2. Urban Service Criteria

Five criteria are to be used in the determination of the adequacy of proposed or existing urban facilities and services consistent with Policy 3.02. The urban service standards are written within the framework of these criteria. They include:

### a. Responsiveness to Public Objectives

It is desirable and necessary that all urban service systems be coordinated and integrated with other service systems; evaluated periodically for need, efficiency, and cost effectiveness; and studied for possible duplication of other service systems so as to be responsive to local public objectives and general public need as determined by the governing body.

As public funds and resources are limited, primary emphasis must be given to an effective allocation system which, to the greatest extent possible, effects a consistency of legislative

intent; public policy; urban service programs funding; a periodic assessment of the type, quantity and quality of various urban services; realistic estimates of revenue sources and future income; maximization of the availability of outside funding sources (state, federal, etc.); and consistency of the long-range program direction in accordance with a capital improvements plan. It has been determined that the municipal budget of the City of Boulder is such an allocation system.

### b. Sufficiency and Dependability of Financing

Financing for each urban service program must be based, to the greatest extent possible, on predictable annual revenues which are broad enough to support initial improvements, maintenance, and extensions of facilities and services in relation to, at least, minimum program requirements and unexpected contingencies. Where financing is tied solely to users' fees, serious problems may result if and when the demand for such programs may diminish. At the same time, programs relying heavily on real property revenues or state and federal supplemental funding could be severely restricted if such sources of financing fall short of initial predictions. While no guarantees can be given, financing from a variety of potential sources and spread over a broad base, including sufficient latitude so that funding can be adjusted in the future as changing conditions occur, should be preferred over single-source revenue programs.

### c. Operational Effectiveness

Each urban service program may have distinct operational needs and a wide array of operational activities might be considered. The end result, though, is whether or not a given public program is able to function in a direct, efficient manner. As an example, a volunteer fire department, depending upon personnel employed in other endeavors and scattered throughout the area of service may not respond as quickly and effectively as fire-fighting personnel located where the fire suppression equipment is stored. Also a small sewage treatment plant receiving only limited flows might not be adequately financed to permit proper and continuous monitoring and management.

Measures of operational effectiveness include current and long range project forecasting, coordination with other urban service programs, maximization of economies of scale in urban service provision, and the incorporation of operational processes and organizational methods that have proven effective in similar situations.

# III. Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map Designations

*The BVCP Map provides a sketch plan of the desired land use pattern in the Boulder Valley. Land use categories include residential, business, industrial, public/semi-public, open space, and park use. The BVCP Map also shows the location and functional classification of roads. The following descriptions are meant to be used in interpreting the BVCP Map.*

## Land Use

### Residential Land Use and Densities

Residential land use areas on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, for the most part, reflect the existing land use pattern or current zoning for an area. The highest density areas are generally located close to the University of Colorado. Medium density areas are generally situated near community shopping areas or along some of the major arterials of the City. Mixed density areas surround the downtown. Lower density areas in the older section of the City consist predominantly of single-family detached structures. Many of the areas developed in the City and the County over the last 20 years are characterized by a mixture of housing types ranging from single-family detached to cluster and patio homes, townhouses and apartments. The variety of housing types in the developing areas of the Boulder Valley shall continue to be encouraged during the planning period of the Comprehensive Plan.

Residential densities under the Comprehensive Plan range from very low density (two units or less per acre); low density (two to six units per acre); medium density (six to 14 units per acre); to high density (more than 14 units per acre). It is assumed that variations of the densities on a small area basis may occur within any particular classification, but an average density will be maintained for that classification.

Additionally, in older downtown neighborhoods that were developed with single family homes but for a time were zoned for higher densities, a variety of housing types and densities are found within a single block. The city's goal is to preserve current neighborhood character and not exacerbate traffic and parking problems. Few new housing units will be added. The average density in the mixed density designation is in the medium density range (six to 14 units per acre).

Within certain residential areas, there is also the potential for small neighborhood shopping facilities and related offices and government facilities through special review. Although the neighborhood market ceased to function in most areas of the city during the late 1960s as supermarkets took over the trade, neighborhood markets or other convenience facilities may become more popular in the future.

Mixed use-residential development may be deemed appropriate and will be encouraged in some residential areas through the

process of Major Site Review, subcommunity planning or area planning. After such review, these areas may be designated Mixed Use-Residential. In these areas, housing uses will predominate, although neighborhood scale retail and personal service uses will be allowed. Specific regulations will be adopted which define the desired mix, location and design characteristics of these uses.

### Business Land Use and Intensity

Within the Boulder Valley there are five categories of business land use, based on the intensity of development and the particular needs of the residents living in each subcommunity. The five categories are: Regional, Community, General, Arterial and Mixed Use Business. The Comprehensive Plan does not advocate strip commercial zoning of major arterials in the Valley, but it indicates particular areas where concentration of commercial activity has taken place or might best occur in the future.

The two major Regional Business areas of the Boulder Valley are the Downtown and the Crossroads Area. Within these areas are located the major shopping facilities, offices, financial institutions, and government and cultural facilities serving the entire Boulder Valley and abutting communities. These areas will continue to be refurbished and upgraded and shall remain the dominant focus for major business activities in the region.

A Community Business area is the focal point for commercial activity serving a subcommunity or a collection of neighborhoods. These are designated to serve the daily convenience shopping and service needs of the local populations, and are generally under 150,000 to 200,000 square feet in area. Offices within the Community Business areas should be offices designated specifically for residents of the subcommunity. Where feasible, multiple uses will be encouraged within these centers.

The General Business areas are located, for the most part, at junctions of major arterials of the City where intensive commercial uses have already been allowed. The Plan proposes that these areas continue to be used without expanding the strip character already established.

### Industrial Land Use and Intensity\*

The Comprehensive Plan projects five classifications of industrial use within the Boulder Valley: General, Community, Light, Performance, and Mixed Use Industrial.

The General Industrial classification is shown where the more intensive and heavy industries are located or planned within the Valley.

The Community Industrial classification is shown for those areas where the predominant uses provide a direct service to the planning area. These uses often have ancillary commercial activity and are essential to the life of the Boulder community. These uses include smaller scale auto-related uses, small printing operations, building contractors, building supply warehouses, small manufacturing operations and similar uses.

The industrial uses considered as "Light" and "Performance" Industrial on the Comprehensive Plan are primarily research and development, light manufacturing, large scale printing and publishing, electronics, or other intensive employment uses. These uses are concentrated primarily in "industrial parks" located within the Gunbarrel Hill area along the Longmont Diagonal, and along Arapahoe Avenue between 33rd and 55th Streets. Performance Industrial uses require high-quality site plans and must meet performance criteria for how on-site and off-site impacts are handled.

Mixed use-industrial development may be deemed appropriate and will be encouraged in some industrial areas through the process of Major Site Review, subcommunity planning, or area planning. Industrial uses will predominate. Housing compatible with and appropriate to the industrial character will be allowed.

Neighborhood retail and service uses may be allowed. Specific regulations will be adopted for these areas which define the desired mix, location and design characteristics of these uses.

### Public/Semi-Public Land Use Designations

Public/Semi-Public land use designations encompass a wide range of public and private non-profit uses that provide a community service. This category includes municipal and public utility services such as the municipal airport, water reservoirs, and water and wastewater treatment plants. Public/Semi-Public also includes: educational facilities, including public and private schools and the University; government offices such as City and County buildings, libraries, and the jail; government laboratories; and non-profit facilities such as cemeteries, churches, hospitals, retirement complexes and may include other uses as allowed by zoning.

### Agriculture Land Use Designation

An Agriculture land use designation identifies land in the Service Area that is planned to remain in agricultural use. Uses that are auxiliary to agriculture, such as a home, a barn and out-buildings and the incidental sales of farm or horticultural products are expected on land with this designation. Given the urban nature of Boulder, the designation will be used rarely.

### Natural Ecosystem Overlay

In order to encourage environmental preservation, a Natural Ecosystem overlay is applied over Comprehensive Plan Land Use Designations. The overlay denotes land with natural resource importance. A Natural Ecosystems overlay will not necessarily preclude development or human use of a particular area, but will serve to identify certain environmental issues in the area. The overlay will serve to guide the City and the County in decisions about annexations, rezonings, acquisitions, density transfers, development review and other planning processes. This overlay replaces and expands the land areas that were designated Environmental Preservation in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan from 1978 through 1995. An initial study was conducted which identified lands that contribute to the quality of natural ecosystems in the Boulder Valley. Private and public sites in the planning area which are included in this study have been identified on the Land Use Designation Map. As more information becomes available, the Natural Ecosystem Map and overlay may be revised and expanded.

# Open Space and Parks

## Open Space

Open Space designations on the BVCP Map included land already acquired by the City, or other governmental entities, and land planned for future acquisition. Open Space designations are not intended to limit acquisition, but to be indicative of the broad goals of the program. Other property which meets Open Space purposes and functions should be considered and may be acquired. Open Space designations indicate that the long-term use of the land is planned to serve one or more open space functions. However, Open Space designations may not reflect the current use of the land while in private ownership.

## Non-Urban Parks

Non-urban parks, generally identified as regional parks or wildlife habitat areas, are large, mostly natural areas used primarily for passive recreational use, such as hiking, picnicking, fishing, and bird watching, although more active uses including boating, swimming, beach activities, and large user groups are allowed in select areas. The three regional parks currently in the Boulder Valley include the Boulder Mountain Park, Boulder Reservoir, and Eldorado Canyon State Park. The wildlife habitat area designated as a Non-Urban Park is Sawhill Ponds (owned by the State of Colorado and managed by the City).

## Urban and Other Parks

Urban and Other Parks serve a variety of functions. Three types of parks are in this category: Community parks, Neighborhood Parks, and Other Parks. Boulder currently has three community Park sites that are developed or planned to be developed: Harlow Platts, East Boulder, and North Boulder Community Parks. The North Central Community Park site in Area III is land set aside for a new park if Area III land in North Boulder ever develops. Community parks provide a wide range of both active and passive park uses for large areas of the community.

Neighborhood Parks are ideally planned to be located in the center of the neighborhood or adjacent to an elementary school site. The Park should serve all age groups within the neighborhood and provide a wide variety of recreational activities. Typical elements of a Neighborhood Park include children's playgrounds, turf areas for informal open play space, picnic and passive areas, park shelter buildings, play courts, and landscaping. There are numerous Neighborhood Parks located throughout the City.

Other parks fill a variety of specialized needs and are affected by a variety of ownerships and management. For more information on different types of parks, see the City of Boulder Parks and Recreation Master Plan.

# Roads

Definitions for highway and street functional classifications are as follows:

## Freeway/Expressway

Highways serving the longest trip distances designed to provide convenience for higher speed traffic. Access is only at interchanges (freeways) or occasionally at intersections (expressways) with other streets. Street lineage is four or more through lanes with additional turning lanes at intersections.

## Parkway

Similar in function and design to an expressway. There is no land access to either the mainline or ramps. Differs from expressway in that more attention is paid to landscaping, berming, and physical attractiveness. Street lineage is four or more through lanes with additional turning lanes at intersections.

## Principal Arterial Street

Streets serving major activity centers, high traffic volume corridors, long trip distances, and a high proportion of total urban

area travel on a minimum of mileage. Service to abutting land is subordinate to the provision of travel service to major traffic movements. Street lineage is four to six through lanes as well as additional turning lanes.

## Minor Arterial Street

Streets interconnecting with and augmenting the principal arterial system and providing service to trips of moderate length at a somewhat lower level of travel mobility. The system places more emphasis on land access and distributes travel to geographic areas smaller than those identified with the principal arterial system. Street lineage is two to four through lanes, with additional turning lanes where appropriate.

## Collector Street

Streets serving neighborhoods, or commercial and industrial areas, collecting traffic from local streets in neighborhoods, and channeling it into the arterial systems. A minor amount of through traffic may be carried on collector streets, but the system primarily provides land access service and carries local traf-
