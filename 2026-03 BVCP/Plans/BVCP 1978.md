WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §31-23-202, as amended, the Boulder City Council and Boulder Planning Board have adopted the goals, policies, programs and supportive data of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §§30-28-106 and 30-28-108, as amended, the County Planning Commission and County Board of Commissioners have made and adopted a Master Plan for the physical development of the unincorporated area of the County; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-106(2)(a), as amended, the Master Plan shall not be effective within the boundaries of any incorporated municipality within the region unless such Plan is adopted by the governing body of the municipality; and

WHEREAS, C.R.S. (1973) §31-23-106, as amended, requires the approval of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan as it relates to unincorporated areas by the Boulder County Planning Commission and by the Boulder County Commissioners; and

WHEREAS, the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan (a master plan developed pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-106, as amended) has been made with the general purpose of guiding and accomplishing a coordinated, adjusted and harmonious development of the County which, in accordance with present and future needs and resources, will best promote the health, safety, morals, order, convenience, prosperity and general welfare of the inhabitants, as well as efficiency and economy in the process of development, including such distribution of population and of the uses of land for urbanization, trade, industry, habitation, recreation, agriculture, forestry, and other purposes which will tend to create conditions favorable to health, safety, transportation, prosperity, civic activities and recreational, educational and cultural opportunities; will tend to reduce the waste of physical, financial and human resources which result from either excessive congestion or excessive scattering of population; and will tend toward an efficient and economic utilization, conservation and production of the supply of food and water and of drainage, sanitary and other facilities and resources; and

WHEREAS, the State General Assembly has declared that the purpose of the annexation statutes is to encourage natural and well-ordered development of municipalities; to distribute fairly and equitably the cost of municipal services among those persons who profit therefrom; to extend municipal government services and facilities to eligible areas which form a part of the whole community; to simplify governmental structure in urban areas; to provide an orderly system for extending municipal regulations to newly annexed areas; to reduce friction among contiguous or neighboring municipalities; and to increase the likelihood of municipal corporations in urban areas being able to provide their citizens with the services they require; and

## SECTION I: APPLICATION

### 1.01

The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall be based on specific and nonconflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and *Colorado Revised Statutes, C.R.S.*, as amended (1973) §29-1-203, §29-20-101 *et seq.*, §30-28-106 through 31-23-211, as amended. The Comprehensive Plan, consisting of a series of maps together with explanatory statements, shall be derived to the maximum extent possible from the policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to City Charter, C.R.S. (1973) §29-1-203, §29-20-101 *et seq.*, §30-28-106 through 30-28-109, §31-12-101 *et seq.*, and §31-23-206 through 31-23-211, as amended.

### 1.02

The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall generally encompass the Boulder Valley which is defined as those areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa to the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north, and as specifically delineated on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map.

### 1.03

The length of the planning period is approximately fifteen years. This term has been determined to be reasonable and has been arrived at after weighing a combination of facts and policy considerations: land use and land use projections, service area projections, and capacity of the City to fund capital improvements, the growth rate experienced in the 1970's, and the desires of the community in regard to accommodating growth. It is anticipated that during this fifteen year period the Boulder Valley population will increase from 95,000 to between 126,000 and 140,000.

### 1.04

Projections of land use in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan are sufficiently detailed to be definitive and directional, although not limiting.

### 1.05

Joint planning and close cooperation among the University, the School Districts,

the City, the County, regional organizations, and other policy-making bodies (e.g., the City/County Health Department, utility and fire service entities) influencing the physical development of the Valley, shall be aggressively pursued so as to avoid conflicts and to provide a means by which each interest may more fully benefit from the presence of others.

### 1.06

Development referrals and responses of the City and County shall be coordinated through the respective planning departments according to a system which has been developed by the planning departments and approved by the legislative bodies of the City and County. The responses of each department shall specifically identify those requirements which a proposed development meets, or fails to meet, under the Comprehensive Plan.

### 1.07

The installation and extension of all utilities shall be consistent with the provisions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and with the responsibilities of the respective utility providers.

### 1.08

It is recognized that the acquisition of open space lands in the Boulder Valley is based upon the City's overall open space plan in which prioritization of need and available revenues are considered. From time to time, however, requests for development are made for parcels of land privately held which are designated as open space on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. In such cases it is the obligation of the City to make decisions with regard to the contemplated acquisition of such lands in a timely and responsible fashion. In the event that a negotiated agreement cannot be reached and the City does not wish to initiate condemnation action, then the property owner may proceed to request development consistent with other applicable policies of the Comprehensive Plan.

### 1.09

With regard to public facilities owned and operated in the other's jurisdiction, the City and County shall respect and abide by existing land use regulations insofar as reasonably practicable.

### 1.10

The City and County shall consider the impacts of open space management and utility installation on abutting property.

**SECTION II: COMMUNITY DESIGN**

An urban area is land intended to accommodate concentrations of people who conduct their many and varied living activities on that land. These people need and depend upon receiving various facilities and services. Governments, usually cities, provide these services.

In Boulder County, and not unlike most other areas throughout the country, land use regulations have traditionally permitted urban uses and development in areas where adequate urban facilities and services are not yet provided, coordinated, nor planned. It is uniformly and universally agreed that the resulting patterns of leapfrog remote urban development are inefficient, wasteful, and seriously contrary to the public interest, health, safety, and welfare. One of the most important objectives of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is the reduction, if not elimination, of this urban sprawl.

The basic outline and approach of the Plan can be concisely stated. The areas immediately surrounding the City can most efficiently and effectively be provided facilities and services by the City. These areas are the most logical areas for urban development. The City intends to provide, on a phased basis over the planning period, the facilities and services to accommodate this urban development. This context should be kept in mind when

considering the policies and other statements that follow.

2.01

For the purposes of Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan structure, the Boulder Valley is divided into three major areas. Area I is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development. Area II is the area now under County jurisdiction planned to accommodate urban development, and new urban development is to occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services and not otherwise. This area is projected to be provided required facilities and services by the City by being annexed to the City during the planning period; Area IIA being the area of immediate focus, the first three years, and Area IIB being accommodated within the balance of the planning period. Area III is the remaining area in the Valley, generally under County jurisdiction and which is not now planned to accommodate urban development for the following reasons: it is not projected that the City will there provide adequate facilities and services within the planning period; no other facilities and services agency comparable to the City is expected in the area; and it is primarily a rural and agricultural area and its character should be preserved and protected.

2.02

It is intended that throughout the Boulder Valley, new urban development, including all development within the City and those developments regulated in the unincorporated areas of the Valley by Boulder County pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-133, as amended, not occur until and unless adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development as set out in Policy Section III and in the Service Standards of the Comprehensive Plan.

New urban development is hereby defined to include:

a. All residential, commercial and industrial development within the City; or  
b. The subdivision of land pursuant to *Colorado Revised Statutes* (1973) §30-28-133, as amended, and the County's subdivision regulations; excepting those developments which could proceed under

the County's agricultural (non-urban) density transfer policies; or

c. Any proposed residential, commercial, business or industrial development subject to County special use hearings and process; provided the County determines that the proposed use(s):

- i. is (are) inconsistent with the Area II and land use projections contained within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map; or
- ii. adversely and materially affect(s) the adopted capital improvements program of the City of Boulder, and thereby affect(s) the projected adequacy or availability of urban services to the immediate area of the proposed use(s) and/or to Area II in general; or
- iii. would require a level of urban facilities and services equal to or greater than those defined in Policy 3.02; or
- iv. is (are) inconsistent with the Comprehensive Plan policies and intent.

### **SECTION III: FACILITIES AND SERVICES**

In the foregoing policies, the City and County find that new urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services and facilities is available to serve the development. It has been determined that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.

**3.01**

The City and County intend that new urban development not occur until adequate urban facilities and services are available

to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of urban facilities and services by a municipality is preferable to provision of urban facilities and services by special districts and private groups in part because municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of adequate urban facilities and services. Therefore, it is hereby presumed that adequate facilities and services can be provided only by the City of Boulder. The City shall extend, furnish or provide such services at such time as it can provide them all as provided under Paragraph 3.02 (c) below and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. However, it is not the intent to preclude the development and use of alternative facilities and service systems for new urban development so long as they are adequate as provided under Paragraph 3.02 (c) and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan.

**3.02**

a. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban residential development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, urban transportation, parks and schools based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

b. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban industrial and commercial development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, and urban transportation, based upon criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

c. The availability of adequate urban facilities and services as set forth in subparagraphs a. and b. above shall be determined based upon the following criteria as further defined in the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. Determinations of facilities and services adequacy shall be based upon the following criteria:

1. Responsiveness to public objectives;
2. Sufficiency and dependability of financing;
3. Operational effectiveness;
4. Adequacy of equipment and facilities; and
5. Proficiency of personnel.

**4.01**

The environmental impact of public and private projects shall, where legally necessary, be determined and the statement made available for public inspection and considered by governmental agencies before any discretionary decisions are made.

**4.02**

The Boulder Valley is to be planned in a manner which recognizes air quality as a critical concern. In light of this, logical land use relationships, expanded use of public transportation, use of pollutant control devices, stabilization of soils through use of vegetation, appropriate monitoring of urban development and mining operations, and such other means as may be feasible, shall be implemented.

**4.03**

The City and County shall seek to protect and improve the quality of the Boulder Valley waterways through identification and control of indirect, as well as direct, sources of pollution. Particular concern shall be given to improved land use configurations, standards to control degradation of streams and lakes caused by storm runoff in urban and rural areas, and control and monitoring of direct sources of discharge, including those of gravel extraction and wastewater treatment facilities.

**4.04**

Noise abatement shall be a continuing environmental concern which shall be implemented through improved land use relationships, as well as by ordinance, with particular attention being given to the impact of transportation and industrial facilities, and proper acoustical design.

**4.05**

Energy conservation shall be encouraged through such means as improved insulation of existing and new structures, clustering of buildings, and the use of alternative energy sources such as sun and wind, with concomitant consideration of air rights.

**4.06**

Hazardous areas which present danger to life and property from flood, forest fire, steep slopes, unstable soil, subsidence, or similar conditions, shall be delineated, and development in such areas carefully controlled or prohibited.

**4.07**

Hillside and ridge-line development shall be carried out in a manner which, to the extent possible, avoids both negative environmental consequences to the immediate and surrounding area, as well as the degrading of public views and vistas.

**4.08**

**4.09**

Critical habitat areas for wildlife and flora shall be maintained, preserved, and conserved.

**4.10**

Sand, gravel, oil, gas, coal, and similar finite resource areas shall be delineated and considered for special public protection, consistent with State and Federal laws.

**4.11**

Aquifer and groundwater recharge areas shall be identified and protected to the greatest extent possible in order to preserve these resources in the Boulder Valley.

**4.12**

The City and County support the City/County Health Department policy of discouraging the installation of sewage disposal systems where municipal collection systems are available, or where a potential pollution or health hazard would be created.

**4.13**

Measures to conserve water shall be utilized through recycling techniques, improved plumbing methods, efficient site design, and public education programs.

**4.14**

Efforts shall be made to save existing trees in the development of new areas and in the redevelopment and rehabilitation of older structures and neighborhoods. Preservation of mature trees is also to be an important consideration in the planning and design of public improvements.

**3.06**

All transportation systems and circulation throughout the Boulder Valley shall be safe, efficient, and reasonably compatible with abutting land uses and environment.

**3.07**

Park facilities and services, of the City and other service entities, shall provide an adequate range of recreational opportunities. Such facilities and services shall be designed in a manner which responds to the needs of the intended users.

**3.08**

Nothing within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall prohibit the City from denying the provision of utility services to any property within the Boulder Valley for utility-related reasons.

### **SECTION VI: INDIVIDUAL OPPORTUNITIES**

Planning of the Boulder Valley shall be directed toward achieving and maintaining a socially, culturally, economically and environmentally balanced community which offers a wide range of opportunities and choices for individual residents, and recognizes a variety of unique personal needs, services, activities, and interests.

**6.01**

The unique and powerful community identity and sense of place which characterizes the citizens of the Boulder Valley and its setting shall be recognized and respected by policy decision makers.

**6.02**

The City and County shall recognize the rights of citizens to play a role in governmental decisions which affect their lives and property, through continual efforts to maintain and improve open and public communication and conduct of business. In addition, the City and County shall continue to support adequate programs

and provide opportunities for citizen participation and neighborhood involvement.

**6.03**

The City and County shall seek the effective provision, through public and private organizational involvement, of maintenance, remedial and developmental programs and services to its citizens; such as special education, child care, nutrition, physical and mental health services, crime prevention, recreation, multimodal transportation, and manpower training.

**6.04**

The City and County shall take all reasonable steps to ensure that new development, public as well as private, be designed in a manner which is sensitive to meeting the social, physical, and emotional needs of the community. **6.05**

The City and County, through their land use regulations and incentive programs, shall encourage the private sector to provide a mixture of housing types with varied price ranges and densities, which attempt to meet the needs of all elements of the Boulder Valley population. Where such efforts are inadequate to meet the needs of low and moderate income families, the City and County shall utilize appropriate Federal, State and other programs and resources to supplement private activities.

**6.06**

The City and County shall encourage low and moderate income housing, whether publicly or privately financed, to be designed in such a manner as to be compatible with and dispersed throughout the individual project as well as the larger community.

## Subsidence of Undermined Areas

Ground subsidence due to the collapse of coal mine voids has occurred in the Boulder Valley. Potentially devastating effects of collapse are graphically evident on the City-owned Richardson parcel northeast of the intersection of Cherryvale and Marshall Roads. The surrounding lands are underlain by the old Eagle Mine, which closed in 1936. Linear trench-like depressions are evident where collapse has occurred subsequent to mine abandonment.

A large percentage of the land in the Davidson Mesa area has been undermined. In addition, large areas are still underlain by mineable coal, the rights to which are held by potential extractors. House Bill 1529 (enacted in 1973) stipulates that commercially extractable coal deposits must be conserved for future utilization. Eventual extraction of these reserves could result in subsequent subsidence. The majority of this area has been designated as open space. Development should not be allowed in areas designated as subsidence hazards unless extensive analysis and remedial engineering have been performed. Considering the existing state of the art of subsidence prediction, this would be an extremely formidable task.

Alluvial Fans  
Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, eroded along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to twenty feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating flood of the Big Thompson occurred, such as the August, 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan. Because of their hazardous character, alluvial fans are basically unsuitable for development.

Areas of active landslide should be off-limits to any development. Areas designated as "Potentially Unstable" require thorough slope stability analysis before decisions concerning development can be made.

Slopes Instability  
Land slides are evident throughout the Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure if modified by development. Open space buffers should be obtained on, above, and below steep slopes. These safe zones should prevent slope disturbance and remove citizens from areas of hazard.

**Material Movement**  
Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of material movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.

#### Earth Flows

Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Examination of the deposits on the sediment surface bordering the foothills has discovered boulders exceeding twenty-five feet in diameter.

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to twenty-nine feet in diameter in silt and clay-sized mud.

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation. Saturated accumulations of debris, along and within the foothills, liquefy and flow down stream channels and out across the mesa and valley surfaces. An earth flow would be extremely destructive in developed areas. Areas judged susceptible to earth flows are unsuitable for development.

#### Slope Debris

The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes. Anyone proposing development within the area shown as susceptible to debris damage should be required to provide analysis and mitigative engineering.

#### Expansive Soil and Rock

The Pierre Shale, Laramie Formation, and Fox Hills Formation each contain potentially disruptive expansive clays. The greater part of the Boulder Valley is underlain by one of these bedrock types. Proper engineering can mitigate the potential hazard. Anyone proposing development within an area designated as "Potentially Expansive" should be required to supply appropriate soil testing and mitigative engineering.

#### Hydrocompactible Loess

Hydrocompaction refers to sediment volume decrease by hydration. During Neo-Glacial times, the Denver-Boulder metro area was a glacial desert. Little organic, ground-stabilizing cover existed. Prevailing westerly winds winnowed silt-sized material from the valley floors and redeposited it atop and to the leeward side of mesas and other high land forms.

When exposed to development and resultant drainage changes, these materials tend to be compacted, decreasing their volume. This volume decrease can result in destructive surface subsidence.

The potential for hydrocompaction damage exists in numerous areas within the undeveloped southern and eastern portions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan area. Anyone proposing development within areas designated as "Potentially Hydrocompactible" should be required to perform appropriate tests and to supply mitigative engineering.

### **WASTEWATER**

Increasing concern for protection of the environment, with particular emphasis on water quality, has highlighted the importance of the City's wastewater collection and treatment system. The national goal of fishable, swimmable waters by 1983, and the expressed desire of City Council to develop Boulder Creek as a sport fishery, provide the framework for wastewater treatment decisions in the Boulder Valley. Planned treatment plant capacity is governed by service area population projections for 1985 and 1995.

The wastewater collection system consists of approximately 249 miles of pipe of varying sizes, separate from the storm sewer system. Major interceptors adequate to serve the existing service area are in place. Extension of sewer mains will be coordinated with other public facilities and constructed to conform with long-range plans to achieve the most economical means of providing sewer service. Sewer mains will need to be extended within the service area where individual systems fail, to prevent public health problems from polluted groundwater.

### **WATER SERVICE**

Historically, with few exceptions, the City of Boulder has been farsighted in its water utility planning, thus being prepared for the major population gains of the past two decades. In the future, improvements will be staged to serve the population increases as they are allowed to occur. The planning for water system improvements will be based on estimating the new facilities which will be needed to serve those areas designated for new growth. The staging of these facilities will be timed to correspond with the growth, so that service to existing customers will not be impaired.

In providing for present and future populations, Boulder's water supplies are derived from mountain regions tributary to the upper reaches of North Boulder and Middle Boulder Creeks and mountain lands in the upper reaches of the Colorado River on the Western Slope. Colorado River water is brought to the Eastern Slope by the Colorado-Big Thompson project which fills many reservoirs, including the Boulder Reservoir. Water in the Boulder Reservoir can then be exchanged for

additional Boulder Creek water or treated and directed into the Boulder water system. The water, after being treated, is held in four reservoirs along the westerly edge of the City. A fifth reservoir is located on Gunbarrel Hill to serve the Gunbarrel area. In the future, an additional reservoir may be required in the foothills near Four Mile Creek.

The distribution system is divided into three pressure zones serving ground elevations varying from 5,100 to 5,800 feet. Treated water from Boulder Creek can be delivered to all three zones by gravity through several pressure-regulating stations. Water from the Boulder Reservoir plant can also be delivered to all three zones through pump stations. In planning additional water service, the exact timing and location of extensions will need to be related carefully to future population increases, land use, and the resulting economies of service. As a basic tenet, maintaining adequate service for current customers and adding water taps to existing lines having an excess capacity will have priority over providing new facilities.

### **FIRE**

The Boulder Fire Department has been working on a comprehensive study of its functions to prevent, detect, and suppress fires efficiently and to provide emergency life support. For the past twelve years, Boulder has had only three major fire stations. As the land area of the City increased through annexation, areas distant from the stations have been provided with fire protection but with very poor response time. New stations are needed and new concepts have been developed to provide effective fire protection for the entire City.

One of the new concepts developed during the past year is the satellite fire station (a station smaller than the existing major stations). Its primary purpose would be the provision of fire protection and related services to residential areas. City Council has approved two satellite stations, one in north Boulder and one in south Boulder, to be located in converted dwellings. They will have light attack squad pumpers to provide fast application of fire suppression or emergency life support. The Gunbarrel area will require a full-sized fire station because of the industrial and commercial uses in this area.

The Fire Department is working on a system of measuring fire protection needs based on several factors, including land use, response times, and built-in fire protection, to determine the type of fire stations and equipment needed in the future.

In the areas that will not be annexed during the planning period, volunteer rural fire districts will continue to provide fire protection. Some of these fire districts could become taxing districts as the cost of providing fire protection increases.

## CENTRAL AREA POLICIES

The City of Boulder, through the merging of public and private interests, shall seek to retain the Central Area of the Boulder Valley, defined generally by Baseline Road, Iris Avenue, the west city limits, and 34th Street, as the primary governmental, cultural, office, financial, entertainment, shopping, medical facility, and high density residential district of the Boulder Valley.

Within the Central Area, several distinct, yet interrelated sub-areas exist: the Downtown Business District; the University; the Crossroads Area; transitional and older, stable residential neighborhoods, special business districts, the hospital facilities area, and newer multi-family neighborhoods.

### DOWNTOWN BOULDER

The Downtown shall retain its historic role as the governmental, entertainment, office, and financial center of the Boulder Valley. It shall continue to develop as a specialty retail center. This shall be accomplished by coordinating concerted and comprehensive public policies and investments with private redevelopment and rehabilitation efforts.

#### Pearl Street

Pearl Street is the historic downtown retail avenue of Boulder. The historic character of the buildings, in terms of their richness of materials, detail, texture, and intimate pedestrian scale, are assets which will be retained through the designation of histor-

ic landmarks, and by private rehabilitation efforts which restore, where appropriate, the original character and facade of each building. In addition, the City shall encourage development which will improve the compatibility of architecture and it shall encourage a variety of retail, service, restaurant, entertainment, banking, and upper-story residential uses to provide expanded economic and social opportunities. Pearl Street itself will be devoted to, and designed primarily to accommodate, pedestrian usage.

#### Walnut Street — Canyon Boulevard Corridor

This corridor remains in transition from its early role as an industrial area, to a major commercial, banking, and high density residential area. It is in this corridor, running from 6th Street on the west to 17th Street on the east, that the most dramatic redevelopment efforts will be encouraged to occur.

Efforts shall be made to concentrate major commercial uses between 9th and 15th Streets in order to maximize the pedestrian scale potential of the Downtown Area. The City shall promote multi-use rather than single-purpose projects. Each project shall respect and relate to other downtown projects in terms of orientation, pedestrian and vehicular circulation, and scale. No individual project shall become an enclave; pedestrian interfaces between projects and corridors are of paramount

The most appropriate uses in this area are office, retail, service, restaurant, entertainment, hotel and convention center, high-density residential, and transportation (parking, mass transit) facilities. Off-street parking shall, where feasible, be provided in well-designed and centrally located structures.

#### Boulder Creek

Boulder Creek is recognized as an environmentally sensitive area which offers dramatic potential as a scenic open space element to be preserved and enhanced through reasonable public investment. Priority will be devoted to developing recreational activities along the Creek such as hiking, bicycling, picnicking, tubing, etc. Flood control programs will be designed so as to protect and preserve the natural character of the Creek environment, and private development in the vicinity will be encouraged to complement rather than intrude upon the tranquility of the area. ### CROSSROADS

The Crossroads Area, described generally by Pearl Street, Folsom, Boulder Creek, and 30th Street, shall remain as a regional shopping center of the Boulder Valley. Other than the Downtown, no other regional shopping facility shall be permitted in the Boulder Valley.

Private redevelopment and upgrading of the entire area shall be a high priority of the City. ### TRANSITIONAL RESIDENTIAL

Transitional residential areas are those "close-in," predominantly residential neighborhoods situated generally between Arapahoe Avenue and Spruce Street, 3rd and Folsom Streets, which are expected to undergo considerable future in-fill and redevelopment pressures. The City recognizes the dual responsibility of permitting selective redevelopment to occur in these areas while, at the same time, utilizing sufficient control in order to protect the basic character and integrity of these neighborhoods from major undesirable disruption. City policies in these areas will be as follows:

1. Private high density redevelopment will be encouraged through an "in-fill" approach which is designed to minimize negative impacts upon the surrounding residential areas, and through the renovation of existing structures which are designed to retain and enhance their positive characteristics. Standards utilized to measure the impacts of such developments will be the relative height and bulk of structures, compatibility with nearby properties and structures, architectural treatment (i.e., use of compatible materials and colors), landscaping, and provision of off-street parking. Neighborhood input will be sought prior to discretionary actions taken by the City. Sensitive in-fill and private redevelopment which meet the above criteria shall be considered positive alternatives to urban sprawl, an asset to the Downtown and regional shopping corridor revitalization, as well as an important energy conservation approach.

2. The City shall encourage existing sound residential structures and mature landscaping to be retained and improved as an integral part of any new development.

3. No large scale urban renewal and residential relocation programs to implement residential redevelopment will be undertaken. Under certain circumstances, limited clearance of certain structures and non-conforming uses, which are themselves blighted or are deteriorating influences on the neighborhood, may be considered. In such cases, adequate compensation and relocation expenses would be provided to affected parties.

4. Rehabilitation assistance for residential structures may be provided to certain qualified residents within the area where long-term tenure and stability appears likely.

5. In transitional neighborhoods, upgraded public investments and services such as parks and recreation facilities, coordinated street furniture and street improvements, public transit, bikeways, etc., shall be given high priority in the Capital Improvements Program. Socio-economic programs such as day care will also be encouraged.

### OLDER, STABLE RESIDENTIAL NEIGHBORHOODS

The remaining, predominantly residential, neighborhoods situated generally between Iris Avenue and Baseline Road, the Front Range and Folsom Street, shall be considered "stable" residential environ-

ments. In these areas, the following policies will typically apply:

1. Private redevelopment through proper conversion of existing dwellings and in-fill of vacant or marginally developed lots shall be permitted. Compatibility of such activities with surrounding neighborhood character and underlying socio-economic structure of the community shall be encouraged.

2. Significant demolition activities, even as a part of private redevelopment activities, shall be discouraged through a variety of means including the consideration of possible historic preservation or rehabilitation districts.

3. Although limited clearance to remove structures and/or non-conforming uses which are blighted or blighting influences, or to provide necessary public improvements, such as parks and open space, may be considered, no large-scale public land assembly and/or demolition to implement redevelopment activities shall be permitted.

4. Residential rehabilitation assistance to qualified persons to enable them to meet codes and upgrade their properties shall be encouraged in these stable neighborhoods.

5. Deficiencies in public facilities and services, such as parks and recreation, coordinated street landscaping, furniture, and street improvements, etc., shall be given high priority in the City's Capital Improvements Program. In addition, social programs such as day care, crime prevention activities, library services, counseling, etc., will be encouraged as the need develops.

## I. CRITERIA

The following criteria are to be used in the determination of the adequacy of proposed or existing urban facilities and services:

**A. Responsiveness to Public Objectives.** It is desirable and necessary that all urban service systems be coordinated and integrated with other service systems; evaluated periodically for need, efficiency, and cost effectiveness; and studied for possible duplication of other service systems so as to be responsive to local public objectives and general public need as determined by the governing body.

As public funds and resources are limited, primary emphasis must be given to an effective allocation system which, to the greatest extent possible, effects a consistency of legislative intent; public policy; urban service programs funding; a periodic assessment of the type, quantity and quality of various urban services; realistic estimates of revenue sources and future income; maximization of the availability of outside funding sources (State, Federal, etc.); and consistency of the long-range program direction in accordance with a capital improvements plan. It has been determined that the municipal budget of the City of Boulder is such an allocation system.

**B. Sufficiency and Dependability of Financing.**

Financing for each urban service program must be based, to the greatest extent possible, on predictable annual revenues which are broad enough to support initial improvements, maintenance, and extensions of facilities and services in relation to, at least, minimum program requirements and unexpected contingencies. Where financing is tied solely to users' fees, serious problems may result if and when the demand for such programs may diminish. At the same time, programs relying heavily on real property revenues or State and Federal supplemental funding could be severely restricted if such sources of financing fall short of initial predictions. While no guarantees can be given, financing from a variety of potential sources and spread over a broad base, including sufficient latitude so that funding can be adjusted in the future as changing conditions occur, should be preferred over single-source revenue programs.

## C. Operational Effectiveness.

Each urban service program may have distinct operational needs and a wide array of operational activities might be considered. The end result, though, is whether or not a given public program is able to function in a direct, efficient manner. As an example, a volunteer fire department, depending upon personnel employed in other endeavors and scattered throughout the area of service may not respond as quickly and effectively as firefighting personnel located where the fire suppression equipment is stored. Also a small sewage treatment plant receiving only limited flows might not be adequately financed to permit proper and continuous monitoring and management. Measures of operational effectiveness include current and long-range project forecasting, coordination with other urban service programs, maximization of economies of scale in urban service provision, and the incorporation of operational processes and organizational methods that have proven effective in similar situations.

## D. Proficiency of Personnel.

Conditions and factors which may affect the competency of personnel include the following: 1) education and experience of personnel in meeting job demands; 2) interest and willingness of personnel to implement programs; 3) on-the-job training opportunities; 4) working conditions and fringe benefits related to employees' effectiveness; 5) the ability of the urban service agency to pay salaries commensurate with personnel requirements; and, 6) the effectiveness of recruitment programs.

As with other criteria, extremes may be involved, but in general, the measure of whether or not urban services are being provided must, in part, be determined by the skills of individuals carrying out such programs. For each job a comparison should be made of qualifications of the individual employed or to be employed, against personnel standards established by generally recognized public or technical agencies for similar positions.

## E. Adequacy of Equipment and Facilities.

This factor may be more easily measured than any of the others since "generally accepted standards" are involved. As an example, as residences are located in

closer proximity to each other, interest in developing neighborhood parks will normally increase. As evidenced by recreation programs desired by similar population groupings, certain standards for park area, spaces and facilities may be predicted. In a similar manner, most of the other public services and facilities described in this study have minimum locational space, equipment and building needs related to given population groupings.

## II. SERVICE STANDARDS

The remainder of this document contains standards for necessary urban services, according to the five primary criteria outlined above. The required urban services and facilities are as follows: public water, public sewer, urban fire protection, urban police protection, urban transportation, and urban parks. Each of the preceding six services (together with schools) are considered necessary for service to residential areas; the first five (excluding parks and schools) are required to serve industrial and commercial areas.

# WATER UTILITY MASTER PLAN

## INTRODUCTION

In 1975, Black & Veatch presented to the City of Boulder a comprehensive study of, and a long range plan of improvements for, its water works. The improvement plan developed in this 1975 report reflected the then-current City of Boulder plans for population growth and service area. Facilities were proposed to serve a 1980 population of 102,000, expandable to serve an ultimate population of 205,000. Since 1975, the City and County have adopted a new Comprehensive Plan of land use which includes more extensive greenbelt areas. The City and County, through their various powers, are controlling the direction, timing, and density of growth.

The changed development patterns wrought by City and County actions since 1975 have resulted in a smaller area to be served by the Water Utility. The projected total population within this service area has been significantly reduced, and the location of that population changed. These changed conditions occasioned the need for an updated plan of improvements.

The projected 1992 service area to be supplied water by the Boulder Water Utility is that area established by the City and County in the Comprehensive Plan. Planning for major facilities which would be difficult to enlarge or expand is based on projected ultimate population. Planning for all other facilities is based on projected 1992 population.

Scope of the study is limited to the following:

- Review of the new Comprehensive Plan and estimates of future populations and the probable location of those populations were provided by the City Planning Department.
- Review raw water supply facilities and water treatment requirements and develop a program of improvements to serve the projected ultimate population.
- Construct computerized models of the existing and proposed water distribution system; utilize the models to plan and analyze distribution system improvements; and maintain the models to allow analysis of specific problems as and when requested by the Water Utility.
- Present a general plan of improvements for expansion of the water works facilities scheduled to meet the water demands as the population within the service area increases, and present the probable capital costs of those improvements.

### HISTORY OF THE BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN

Increased public involvement in land use planning at the neighborhood level; acknowledgement that physical, social, environmental, and economic planning should be interrelated parts of a total system; and recognition of the interdependence of City and County actions all brought forth new directions for planning in the Boulder Valley.

The "Policies" of the Comprehensive Plan are designed to guide future official actions of the City and County as related to land use matters.

The procedure utilized in deriving the policies for the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan consisted first of extensive review of statements by City and County officials, supplemented by recommendations from various citizens' groups and concerned members of the public. More than thirty neighborhood and sub-community meetings were held throughout the Boulder Valley to discuss the proposed Plan, and several study sessions were scheduled with the City Council, City Planning Board, and County Long Range Planning Commission during formulation of the Plan. City and County staff members have also met on a periodic basis with various organizations and interest groups such as the League of Women Voters, Chamber of Commerce, Board of Realtors, and PLAN Boulder County, to provide balanced viewpoints and assistance in development of the recommendations. This final document incorporates many of the comments and concerns which were

expressed by these various groups and individuals.

The 1977 Comprehensive Plan follows several of the concepts introduced in the 1970 Plan. Foremost among these proposals are recognition of the joint responsibility of the City and County governments and staffs to promote rational land use planning throughout the entire Boulder Valley based upon a single land use plan; the need to maintain and strengthen the role of the Central Area as the dominant regional focus of the Boulder Valley; the use of open space lands as a means of preserving unique natural features and land forms and to provide for urban shaping; and a commitment to combat urban sprawl and leapfrog development.

## BUSINESS LAND USE AND INTENSITY

Within the Boulder Valley there are four categories of business land use, based on the intensity of development and the particular needs of the residents living in each subcommunity. The four categories are: regional, community, general, and arterial business. The Comprehensive Plan does not advocate strip commercial zoning of major arterials in the Valley, but it indicates particular areas where concentration of commercial activity has taken place or might best occur in the future.

The two major regional business areas of the Boulder Valley are the Central Business District and the Crossroads Area. Within these areas are located the major shopping facilities, financial institutions, entertainment and office sites for the entire Valley and abutting communities.

These areas will continue to be refurbished and upgraded, and shall remain the dominant focus for major business activities in the region.

A community business area is the focal point for commercial activity within each of the five subcommunities. These are designed to serve the daily convenience shopping needs of the local populations, and are generally under 150,000 to 200,000 square feet in area. Offices within the community business areas should be professional offices designed specifically for residents of the sub-community. Where feasible, multiple uses will be encouraged within these centers.

The general business areas are located, for the most part, at junctions of major arterials of the city where intensive commercial uses have already been allowed. The Plan proposes that these areas continue to be used without expanding the strip character already established.

The arterial business designation is shown along certain major streets of the Valley. These are areas usually zoned for less intensive business uses than in the general business areas.

#### ENERGY AND RESOURCE CONSERVATION

The past few years have provided definitive evidence of the need for the public to conserve a dwindling world-wide supply of energy as well as to become less wasteful of water and other fixed resources. Consumptive behavior and habits of our population will have to change as costs of gas and oil escalate and as cyclic fluctuations in weather continue to cause economic and resource dislocation.

While recent emphasis has been placed upon modifications in building codes to require improved insulation, and to en-

courage the use of non-fossil energy sources such as the sun or the wind, research has also shown that patterns and mixtures of land use, densities, and housing types have a profound effect on the above factors. It has been documented (*The Costs of Sprawl*, by Real Estate Research Corporation, 1974, prepared for the Council on Environmental Quality and other agencies) that higher-density, planned communities consume about 50% less land area; save about 55% in costs for roads and utilities; save up to 44% in reduced energy consumption; utilize 35% less water; and generate lower levels of pollution when compared to low-density, sprawl communities characterized by leapfrog, scattered development.

The Comprehensive Plan, with its policies of encouraging Central Area revitalization, directing new growth into areas already served by an existing infrastructure of services and facilities, and providing for contiguous growth through annexation rather than permitting leapfrog development on the periphery, becomes a vital component in the community's efforts to conserve energy and protect our natural resources.

Steps should be taken now to reduce our energy consumption. There are several measures, ranging from changes in the Building Code, to use of alternative sources of energy, to more mixed-use and higher-density development that can conserve energy. Development of a more efficient land use pattern for the community can save additional energy by encouraging people to walk, ride the bus, or bike. Changes in present residential street width standards and provision of street trees can save asphalt, reduce surface heat absorption during the summer, mitigate the effects of carbon monoxide emissions, and provide a human scale for residents who live on these streets. Greater concern must be shown in dealing with orientation of buildings to gain the maximum heating and cooling from the sun and summer breezes.

Boulder must consider several measures to insure energy savings today so that residents will have necessary energy resources in the future.

•The population of the City and the surrounding Boulder Valley area has grown from approximately 21,000 in 1950 to approximately 95,000 at present;

•A study commission reviewed growth and related questions in the mid-1970's and the people of Boulder adopted a growth limitation proposal in November, 1976;

•A comprehensive revision of the plan for the Boulder Valley has been under study and review for well over a year and during that time much data and information has been accumulated and analyzed concerning present conditions and numerous plans and projections for the future have been made and developed, and

WHEREAS, the City of Boulder Planning Board and the City Council have determined that the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN meets statutory requirements, to-wit: has a general purpose of guiding and accomplishing a coordinated, adjusted and harmonious development of the City and its environs which will, in accordance with present and future needs, best promote health, safety, morals, order, convenience, prosperity, and general welfare, as well as efficiency and economy in the process of development, including, among other things, adequate provision for traffic, the promotion of safety from fire, flood waters, and other dangers, adequate provision for light and air, the promotion of helpful and convenient distribution of population, the promotion of good civic design and arrangement, wise and efficient expenditure of public funds, and adequate provision of public utilities and other public requirements, and

WHEREAS, the approval of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, as it relates to unincorporated areas, is required by the Boulder County Planning Commission and Board of County Commissioners, and

WHEREAS, C.R.S. 1973, 30-28-108, allows the adoption of the County Comprehensive Plan, in whole or in part, and

WHEREAS, Boulder County has not yet completed the County Comprehensive Plan, and

WHEREAS, after over fifteen years of cooperative planning for the Boulder Valley, the County concurs with the City in the statements that the pressure for growth and development is anticipated to continue and planning should respond in a positive way; reasonable steps must be taken to accommodate the growth while at the same time retaining the character of the community; that such plan should consider, among other factors, those which follow:

•That land within the Boulder Valley is a finite resource and must be put to use in an efficient and environmentally sound manner;

•That people demand facilities and services and these should be phased in accordance with a reasonable and realistic financial program;

•That efforts should be made to provide low and moderate income housing;

•That the City should continue to be an attractive place to work and live so as to prevent and discourage increased pressures for suburban communities;

•That protection of the physical setting and environment is of importance and concern to the public, and

WHEREAS, Boulder County land use decisions within the Boulder Valley have been guided by the previously jointly adopted City/County master plan since 1970, and the County has acquired considerable land use knowledge and experience therefrom, and

WHEREAS, the County has, over the period of the last two years, committed considerable staff planning efforts in the master plan study and review; conducted numerous and unprecedented neighborhood meetings throughout the unincorporated areas of the Boulder Valley, notifying virtually every unincorporated property owner of record of said meetings; conducted numerous public meetings before the Boulder County Long Range Planning Commission and Boulder County Board of County Commissioners so that public input might be facilitated and encouraged at every point in the development of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN; and referred the proposed 77 revision to the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN on March 31, 1977 to all other municipalities in Boulder County and to affected local, State, and regional agencies, and

77

WHEREAS, the County of Boulder, through its involvement in the review and development of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, has found and determined as follows:

1. That a single and jointly adopted City/County master plan is desirable and necessary at this time to assure the harmonious development of the City of Boulder in relationship to its environs; being particularly necessary for the proper and efficient coordination of transportation facilities, the management of flood plains and other hazard areas, the review of proposals under City and County jurisdiction for new urban development, and the proper and accurate assessment of the external effects of City's growth policies on the other municipalities and unincorporated areas of Boulder County.

2. That the area encompassed within the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, as generally described within Policy 1.02 of Exhibit 2 and as more particularly delineated on Exhibit 1, is properly designated as the planning area of the City of Boulder by virtue of

- regional water and wastewater Boulder service area designations

- topographic and historic relationships of land forms and uses within the Boulder Valley

- the established commitment of the City of Boulder to peripheral Boulder Valley open space acquisition

3. That the reasonableness of the "Area II/III" designations of the master plan is substantiated and supported by the City of Boulder's service commitments and Capital Improvements Plan, and that said designations are necessary in order particularly to

- encourage future urban development to locate within or adjacent to existing urban areas in order to eliminate sprawl and strip development, to assure the provision of adequate urban services and to preserve agriculture, forestry, and other similar land uses

- to avoid the duplication and overlapping costs in the provision of urban services and to establish a satisfactory level of quality, quantity, and dependability of those services

- maintain a balanced and diversified economy, assuring an adequate level of local employment opportunities and strengthening and stabilizing the local tax base

- maintain a functional balance between community facilities and potential land capacities (population)

- promote an efficient, coordinated, and multi-mode transportation system

- promote the use of open space as an urban-shaping tool

- encourage through land uses the provision of adequate water for all the County's diverse land uses, and to discourage the further conversion of agricultural water within the County to urban uses

4. That the planning period of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, as described in Policy 1.03 of Exhibit 2, is a desirable and reasonable time frame in which to accurately project land uses and the availability of services.

5. That the Plan concept of an "Area II/Area III service line" (which "service line" may be changed, with the resultant conversion of additional Area III lands into Area II designation, but only through formal Plan amendment procedures) is necessary for full and effective implementation of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN.

6. That the master plan is consistent with the County's goal statement 1.2:

"Existing communities should grow, if at all, at whatever rate they consider desirable, within the limits of what is acceptable to the citizens of areas potentially affected by that growth, and to the citizens of the County, while preserving and improving the quality of life and the aesthetic and functional fitness of land uses within the County."

7. That the inclusion of and strict adherence of the City and County to Policy 3.07 of Exhibit 2 is one of the most significant aspects of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN; that strict compliance with the intent and provisions of the "Municipal Annexation Act of 1965" C.R.S. 1973, as amended, and the resultant unification of the growth decision-making process within the Valley, is so vital to the County's Plan implementation efforts as to warrant additional and unilateral County actions to require compliance with Policy 3.07 should the City be unable and/or unwilling to comply.

8. That hardships and inequities resulting from the policy transition should be minimal because (a) services can be provided to existing development where necessary to serve the public interest (e.g., alleviate a major health or safety problem); (b) the term "new urban development" refers basically to new proposed subdivisions and major rezonings; (c) there is an existing inventory of more than two thousand (2000) vacant platted units/lots in the County at this time; and (d) the amount of vacant land shown for urban development on the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is substantially more than that needed to accommodate the projected population within the planning period.

9. That specific methods of Plan implementation to be used by the City and County shall include, but not be limited to, the following:

- land use regulations
- City Capital Improvements Plan
- service standards
- City annexation and utility extension procedures and decisions
- open space preservation

That it is the expressed understanding of the County that (a) strict adherence on the part of the City to a formally adopted Capital Improvements Plan, and (b) the ability of the City to cause substantial annexation of Area II within this planning period, that both are crucial to the ultimate success of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, and WHEREAS, the addition of Exhibit 1.E.1 Specified Density/North Boulder and Exhibit 1.E.2 Specified Density/Cherryvale-Hoover Hill to the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is warranted to insure compatibility of future development with the existing neighborhoods in those areas.

BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that the County Attorney be and hereby is directed to draft an intergovernmental agreement between the County and City of Boulder to implement said Plan.
