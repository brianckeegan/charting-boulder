RECITALS

WHEREAS, the County is the ultimate governmental authority with regard to land use control and development in the unincorporated areas of the county, which areas include the Boulder Valley as defined in the attachment hereto; and

WHEREAS, the City is the only significant potential source of adequate urban facilities and services required for the orderly urban development of the Boulder Valley; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §31-23-202, as amended, the Boulder City Council and Boulder Planning Board have adopted the goals, policies, programs and supportive data of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §§30-28-106 and 30-28-108, as amended, the County Planning Commission and County Board of Commissioners have made and adopted a Master Plan for the physical development of the unincorporated area of the County; and

WHEREAS, pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-106(2)(a), as amended, the Master Plan shall not be effective within the boundaries of any incorporated municipality within the region unless such Plan is adopted by the governing body of the municipality; and

WHEREAS, C.R.S. (1973) §31-23-106, as amended, requires the approval of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan as it relates to unincorporated areas by the Boulder County Planning Commission and

by the Boulder County Commissioners; and

WHEREAS, the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan (a master plan developed pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) §30-28-106, as amended) has been made with the general purpose of guiding and accomplishing a coordinated, adjusted and harmonious development of the County which, in accordance with present and future needs and resources, will best promote the health, safety, morals, order, convenience, prosperity and general welfare of the inhabitants, as well as efficiency and economy in the process of development, including such distribution of population and of the uses of land for urbanization, trade, industry, habitation, recreation, agriculture, forestry, and other purposes which will tend to create conditions favorable to health, safety, transportation, prosperity, civic activities and recreational, educational and cultural opportunities; will tend to reduce the waste of physical, financial and human resources which result from either excessive congestion or excessive scattering of population; and will tend toward an efficient and economic utilization, conservation and production of the supply of food and water and of drainage, sanitary and other facilities and resources; and

WHEREAS, the State General Assembly has declared that the purpose of the annexation statutes is to encourage natural and well-ordered development of municipalities; to distribute fairly and equitably the cost of municipal services among those persons who profit therefrom; to extend municipal government services and facilities to eligible areas which form a part of the whole community;

Section 1: Application.

1.01 The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall be based on specific and nonconflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and Colorado Revised Statutes, C.R.S., as amended (1973)S29-1-203, S29-20-101 et seq., S30-28-106 through 31-23-211, as amended. The Comprehensive Plan, consisting of a series of maps together with explanatory statements, shall be derived to the maximum extent possible from the policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to City Charter, Colorado Revised Statutes C.R.S. (1973)S29-1-203, S29-20-101 et seq., S30-28-106 through 30-28-109, S31-12-101 et seq., and S31-23-206 through 31-23-211, as amended.

1.02 The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall generally encompass the Boulder Valley which is defined as those areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa to the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north, and as specifically delineated on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map.

1.03 The length of the planning period is approximately fifteen years. This term has been determined to be reasonable and has been arrived at after weighing a combination of facts and policy considerations: land use and land use projections, service area projections, the capacity of the City to fund capital improvements, the growth rate experienced in recent years, and the desires of the community in regard to accommodating growth. Each 5-year review of the plan shall extend the planning period approximately five years. It is anticipated that during the fifteen year period between 1982 and 1997 the Boulder Valley population will increase to between 121,000 and 136,000.

1.04 Projections of land use in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan are sufficiently detailed to be definitive and directional, although not limiting.

1.05 Joint planning and close cooperation among the University, the School Districts, the City, the County, regional organizations, and other policy-making bodies (e.g. the City/County Health Department, utility and fire service entities) influencing the physical development of the Valley, shall be aggressively pursued so as to avoid conflicts and to provide a means by which each interest may more fully benefit from the presence of others.

1.06 Development referrals and responses of the City and County shall be coordinated through the respective planning departments

11

according to a system which has been developed by the planning departments and approved by the legislative bodies of the City and County. The responses of each department shall specifically identify those requirements which a proposed development meets, or fails to meet, under the Comprehensive Plan.

1.07 The installation and extension of all utilities shall be consistent with the provisions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and with the responsibilities of the respective utility providers.

1.08 It is recognized that the acquisition of open space lands in the Boulder Valley is based upon the City's overall open space plan in which prioritization of need and available revenues are considered. From time to time, however, requests for development are made for parcels of land privately held which are designated as open space on the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. In such cases it is the obligation of the City to make decisions with regard to the contemplated acquisition of such lands in a timely and responsible fashion. In the event that a negotiated agreement cannot be reached and the City does not wish to initiate condemnation action, then the property owner may proceed to request development consistent with other applicable policies of the Comprehensive Plan.

1.09 With regard to public facilities owned and operated in the other's jurisdiction, the City and County shall respect and abide by existing land use regulations insofar as reasonably practicable.

1.10 The City and County shall consider the impacts of open space management and utility installation on abutting property.

1.11 For the purpose of policy construction:

Where the "County" alone is referred to within the policy text, said policy may be amended by the County, after referral to the City.

Where the "City" alone is referred to within the policy text, said policy may be amended by the City, after referral to the County.

All other policies shall be construed to be joint City and County statements of policy, and are to be amended by joint action.

Where a particular "area" is not specified in the policy text, the policy shall apply to all areas.

## Section II: Community Design.

An urban area is land intended to accommodate concentrations of people who conduct their many and varied living activities on that land. These people need and depend upon receiving various facilities and services. Governments, usually cities, provide these services.

In Boulder County, and not unlike most other areas throughout the country, land use regulations have traditionally permitted urban uses and development in areas where adequate urban facilities and services are not yet

12

provided, coordinated, nor planned. It is uniformly and universally agreed that the resulting patterns of leapfrog remote urban development are inefficient, wasteful, and seriously contrary to the public interest, health, safety, and welfare. One of the most important objectives of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is the reduction, if not elimination, of this urban sprawl.

The basic outlines and approach of the Plan can be concisely stated. The areas immediately surrounding the City can most efficiently and effectively be provided facilities and services by the City. These areas are the most logical areas for urban development. The City intends to provide, on a phased basis over the planning period, the facilities and services to accommodate this urban development. This context should be kept in mind when considering the policies and other statements that follow.

2.01 For the purposes of Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan structure, the Boulder Valley is divided into three major areas. Area I is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development. Area II is the area now under County jurisdiction planned to accommodate urban development, and new urban development is to occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services and not otherwise. This area is projected to be provided required facilities and services by the City by being annexed to the City during the planning period; Area IIA being the area of immediate focus, the first three years, and Area IIB being accommodated within the balance of the planning period. Area III is the remaining area in the Valley, generally under County jurisdiction and which is not now planned to accommodate urban development for the following reasons: it is not projected that the City will there provide adequate facilities and services within the planning period; no other facilities and services agency comparable to the City is expected in the area; and it is primarily a rural and agricultural area and its character should be preserved and protected.

2.02 It is intended that throughout the Boulder Valley, new urban development, including all development within the City and those developments regulated in the unincorporated areas of the Valley by Boulder County pursuant to C.R.S. (1973) S30-28-133, as amended, not occur until and unless adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development as set out in Policy Section III and in the Service Standards of the Comprehensive Plan.

New urban development is hereby defined to include:

- a. All residential, commercial and industrial development within the City; or
- b. The subdivision of land pursuant to Colorado Revised Statutes (1973) S30-28-133, as amended, and the County's subdivision regulations; excepting those developments which could proceed under the County's agricultural (non-urban) density transfer policies; or

13

c. Any proposed residential, commercial, business or industrial development subject to County special use hearings and process; provided the County determines that the proposed use(s):

i. is (are) inconsistent with the Area II and land use projections contained within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan Map; or

ii. adversely and materially affect(s) the adopted capital improvements program of the City of Boulder, and thereby affect(s) the projected adequacy or availability of urban services to the immediate area of the proposed use(s) and/or to Area II in general; or

iii. would require a level of urban facilities and services equal to or greater than those defined in Policy 8.3.02; or

iv. is (are) inconsistent with the Comprehensive Plan policies and intent.

2.03 The City is an adequate provider of facilities and services. These facilities and services will continue to be supplied to Area I and the City will make them available to Area II within the planning period pursuant to the City's annexation policies and Capital Improvements Program.

2.04 The policies in regard to annexation to be pursued by the City are:

a. Annexation will be required before adequate facilities and services are furnished;

b. Annexation of existing substantially developed areas will be offered in a manner and on terms and conditions which respect existing life styles and densities, and the City will expect these areas to be brought to City standards only where necessary to protect the health and safety of the residents of the subject area or of the City.

c. For undeveloped areas, the City will impose terms and conditions to protect the public interest and attempt not to impose terms such that annexation is unrealistic or impracticable.

d. There will be no annexations of areas outside the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan boundaries.

e. Area II is anticipated to become part of the City within the planning period. Area III is not anticipated to become part of the City within the planning period. However, publicly owned property located in Area III and intended to remain in Area III may be annexed to the City if the property requires less than

14

a full range of urban services or requires inclusion under City jurisdiction for health, welfare and safety reasons.

2.05 Progress by the City on both annexation and capital improvements shall be reviewed annually by the City and County, as any substantial failure in performance shall be deemed cause for substantial amendment of the Plan by the City and County. The Plan shall also be reviewed at least every five years for possible amendments to reflect changes in circumstances and community desires.

2.06 The City and County will each analyze and evaluate existing land use regulations and make changes, if any, required to bring said regulations into compliance with the Comprehensive Plan and at the same time to consider the rights of affected property owners.

2.07 Where the provider(s) of the facilities and services is(are) other than the City, the County and City will take all reasonable and legal steps to the end of facilitating assimilation by the City upon annexation.

2.08 The City and County commit to aid the assessor and property owners by furnishing Comprehensive Plan projections of service and timing of development to the assessor for ad valorem tax purposes.

2.09 The City and County will preserve lands with unique natural features and characteristics as permanent open space by purchase of development rights, fee simple gifts and/or purchases, and other measures as appropriate and financially feasible.

2.10 The City and County will, by implementing the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, ensure that development will take place in an orderly fashion which will take advantage of existing urban services and shall avoid, insofar as possible, patterns of leapfrog, noncontiguous, scattered development within the Valley.

2.11 Major entryways into the Boulder Valley shall be identified, protected and enhanced in order to emphasize and preserve the natural setting and appearance of the community.

2.12 Agricultural areas shall be identified and evaluated to insure, to the greatest extent possible, their long-term existence and protection from development.

2.13 The central area shall continue as the regional service center of the Valley for office, retail, financial, governmental, cultural, and University activities. As such, it shall remain the primary activity center and focal point of the Boulder Valley.

2.14 Within the Boulder Valley, identifiable subcommunities, containing open space and support facilities which contribute to their livability and enjoyment, shall be planned so that, in time, they can develop into areas of recognizable physical and social identity.

15

2.15 Each of the subcommunities shall be served as needed with shopping facilities and provided with additional programs which cater to the particular needs of that subcommunity, such as child care and library facilities.

2.16 Buildings, districts, and sites of historic, architectural, or archaeological significance shall be identified and protected. Preservation and/or rehabilitation of such buildings, districts, and sites by the private sector shall be encouraged by the City and County through the creation of incentive programs and public improvements.

2.17 New residential, commercial, and industrial development and redevelopment shall be encouraged to follow sound and innovative land use planning; to provide a harmonious relationship between nature and people; and, through judicious use of landscaping, materials and scale, to respect the character of the surrounding area.

2.18 Incentives shall be provided to encourage development of all types of housing, lessen monotony in site design, provide for more functional open space, achieve greater variety in housing choice, and accommodate more attractive and economical landscaping.

2.19 The City shall encourage, consistent with other land use policies, a variety of land uses, provided that the predominant land use designation is consistent with the Comprehensive Plan land use designation, including but not limited to neighborhood shopping facilities or services.

### Section III: Facilities and Services.

In foregoing policies, The City and County find that urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services is available to serve the development. It has been determined that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.

3.01 The City and County intend that new urban development not occur until adequate urban facilities and services are available to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of urban facilities and services by a municipality is preferable to provision of urban facilities and services by special districts and private groups in part because municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of adequate urban facilities and services. Therefore it is hereby presumed that adequate facilities and services can be provided only the City of Boulder. The City shall extend, furnish or provide such services at such time as it can provide them all as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) below and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. However, it is not the intent to preclude the development and use

16

of alternative facilities and service systems for new urban development so long as they are adequate as provided under Paragraph 3.02(c) and the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan.

3.02 a. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban residential development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, urban transportation, parks and schools based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

b. "adequate facilities and services" for new urban industrial and commercial development means the availability of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, and urban transportation, based upon the criteria set forth in subparagraph c. below.

c. The availability of adequate urban facilities and services as set forth in subparagraphs a. and b. above shall be determined based upon the following criteria as further defined in the urban service criteria and standards section of this plan. Determinations of facilities and services adequacy shall be based upon the following criteria:

1. Responsiveness to public objectives;
2. Sufficiency and dependability of financing;
3. Operational effectiveness;
4. Adequacy of equipment and facilities; and
5. Proficiency of personnel.

3.03 a. The City and County agree that extensions, furnishing, or provision of less than adequate facilities and services for new urban development is contrary to the objectives and intent of the Comprehensive Plan and would be injurious to the public health, safety and welfare because it would seriously impair the efforts of the County, which has governmental authority and jurisdiction regarding land use control and development in the unincorporated areas of the county, to implement the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan through reasonable land use regulations.

b. The County requests that the City accompany any extension of adequate facilities and services to new urban development outside the boundaries of the City with concurrent annexation to the City of the land served. The City agrees not to extend or furnish facilities and services to new urban development outside the boundaries of the City without annexing to the City the land to be served.

17

c. The City and County recognize that certain properties within the Boulder Valley have filed for subdivision approval with Boulder County prior to June 13, 1977, the date on which the County approved amendments to its subdivision regulations which require compatibility of applicants for subdivision approval with the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, and have previously been granted water and sewer by the City. The development of these particular properties may be permitted to occur without full compliance with the desires expressed above provided that such development is otherwise in accordance with the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and existing Boulder County land use regulations at the time of submission of the application.

d. The City's capital improvements program is a major program for funding an adequate range of urban services and facilities to Area II as annexation occurs during the planning period. The capital improvements program, within the framework of a responsible budget of balanced revenues and expenditures, schedules the necessary capital projects to insure maintenance of an adequate range of urban services within Area I and to provide urban facilities and services to Area II through annexation, on a phased and orderly basis over the planning period.

3.04 Multi-purpose use of public lands, facilities, and personnel services shall be emphasized. However, in consideration of potential use of parks and open space lands, only activities consistent with the original intent of purchase shall be considered.

3.05 The establishment of new incorporated communities within the Boulder Valley shall be opposed.

3.06 All transportation systems and circulation throughout the Boulder Valley shall be safe, efficient, and reasonably compatible with abutting land uses and environment.

3.07 Park facilities and services, of the City or other service entities, shall provide an adequate range of recreational opportunities. Such facilities and services shall be designed in a manner which responds to the needs of the intended users.

3.08 Nothing within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall prohibit the City from denying the provision of utility services to any property within the Boulder Valley for utility-related reasons.

3.09 Library facilities and services of the Boulder Valley shall provide for an adequate range of informational and intellectual opportunities for all residents.

## Section IV: Environmental Features.

The fragile environment which characterizes the Boulder Valley is a critical asset that must be preserved and protected. It is the framework within which growth and development may be permitted to take place.

- 4.01 The environment impact of public and private projects shall, where legally necessary, be determined and the statement made available for public inspection and considered by governmental agencies before any discretionary decisions are made.
- 4.02 The Boulder Valley is to be planned in a manner which recognizes air quality as a critical concern. In light of this, logical land use relationships, expanded use of public transportation, use of pollutant control devices, stabilization of soils through use of vegetation, appropriate monitoring of urban development and mining operations, and such other means as may be feasible, shall be implemented.
- 4.03 The City and County shall seek to protect and improve the quality of the Boulder Valley waterways through identification and control of indirect, as well as direct, sources of pollution. Particular concern shall be given to improved land use configurations, standards to control degradation of streams and lakes caused by storm runoff in urban and rural areas, and control and monitoring of direct sources of discharge, including those of gravel extraction and wastewater treatment facilities.
- 4.04 Noise abatement shall be a continuing environmental concern which shall be implemented through improved land use relationships, as well as by ordinance, with particular attention being given to the impact of transportation and industrial facilities, and proper acoustical design.
- 4.05 Hazardous areas which present danger to life and property from flood, forest fire, steep slopes, unstable soil, subsidence, or similar conditions, shall be delineated, and development in such areas carefully controlled or prohibited.
- 4.06 Hillside and ridge-line development shall be carried out in a manner which, to the extent possible, avoids both negative environmental consequences to the immediate and surrounding area, as well as the degrading of public views and vistas.
- 4.07 The functional and aesthetic qualities of drainage courses and waterways shall be preserved and enhanced in a manner compatible with a basically nonstructural approach to flood control; and in particular, a noncontainment approach to flood management on Boulder Creek.
- 4.08 Critical habitat areas for wildlife and flora shall be maintained, preserved, and conserved.

19

4.09 Sand, gravel, oil, gas, coal, and similar finite resource areas shall be delineated and considered for special public protection, consistent with State and Federal laws.

4.10 Aquifer and groundwater recharge areas shall be identified and protected to the greatest extent possible in order to preserve these resources in the Boulder Valley.

4.11 The City and County support the City/County Health Department policy of discouraging the installation of sewage disposal systems where municipal collection systems are available, or where a potential pollution or health hazard would be created.

4.12 Measures to conserve water shall be utilized through recycling techniques, improved plumbing methods, efficient site design, and public education programs.

4.13 Efforts shall be made to save existing trees in the development of new areas and in the redevelopment and rehabilitation of older structures and neighborhoods. Preservation of mature trees is also to be an important consideration in the planning and design of public improvements.

### Section V: Economic Considerations.

The City and County shall encourage a viable and balanced economic structure and employment base within the parameters of established land use, environmental and growth policies; with emphasis on providing employment opportunities for Boulder Valley residents and a tax base which provides sufficient governmental revenue to meet community goals.

5.02 Since the public costs of annexing and developing several areas concurrently could prove excessive, the City shall limit said costs to those which can reasonably be accommodated within the Capital Improvements Program, and are compatible with anticipated revenues.

5.03 The City and County will take steps to have furnished or obtain cost-benefit studies for all new urban development proposals of more than 40 acres in size as an essential reference for decision making. Decisions will be made, to the extent possible, to require such new development to bear the expense of facilities and services which it may reasonably require.

5.04 The City and County shall support maintenance and expansion of a reasonably diversified employment base within the Boulder Valley, reflecting manpower and labor force capabilities and recognizing

amenities for emphasizing scientific, technological, and related industries. In addition, where consistent with the various policies of the Comprehensive Plan, the internal growth of industries currently located within the Valley shall be facilitated to the extent possible.

5.05 All reasonable legal steps will be taken toward the end of keeping new employment opportunities within the Boulder Valley consistent with the employment needs, stated growth policy, and other objectives of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. ## BACKGROUND STATEMENT ON URBAN GROWTH AND SERVICES

The Boulder Valley (including the City of Boulder, and generally encompassing those land areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa on the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north--as more specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map) has been experiencing unprecedented and rapid growth with respect to population, housing, land development, and the utilization of natural and community resources since the early 50's growing from 21,500 people in 1950 to 95,000 people at present. The citizens of Boulder have become seriously concerned about the effects of uncontrolled growth on their city and the quality of life. Voters of the City of Boulder in November, 1971 approved a resolution directing local government to "take all steps necessary to hold the rate of growth in the Boulder Valley to a level substantially below that of the 1960's." In 1976, the Growth Limitation Ordinance, which was designed to control the rate of new population increases and to increase the density of the Central Area, was approved by the citizens of Boulder. Figures derived from the 1970 and 1980 censuses indicate that in 1970 the Boulder Valley population was approximately 80,000 and that in 1980, there were an estimated 94,000 people living within the Boulder Valley. The growth limitation ordinance was replaced in 1981 by the residential growth management ordinance, also designed to control the growth rate, allowing a long term population growth rate of no greater than 2% per year in the City.

Transportation, water, sewerage, schools, parks and recreation, police and fire protection, and other necessary public facilities and services are being planned to meet the needs of the Boulder Valley's growing population, but the City and County are experiencing an increasing inability to fund and provide the demanded facilities and services at a pace which will keep abreast of the ever-growing public need.

It is the experience of Boulder County that such adequate urban facilities and services are not available to new urban developments outside the city limits of Boulder, inasmuch as the County of Boulder is not funded nor equipped to provide such urban facilities and services, and those urban facilities and services are not being provided or made available by developers, special districts, or private groups. Residents of the Boulder Valley, and a growing number of fellow Americans, are increasingly challenging the ethic that all growth is good growth. No longer is unconditional growth being accepted unquestioningly as a necessary premise of progress and an enhanced standard of living. Growing environmental concerns, a recent and renewed awareness of the energy crisis confronting us at the national and local levels, together with a growing sense of loss of sense of community, have combined with increased significance to make us realize that we do not have unlimited room or resources. We are only beginning to see that our energy, environmental, and community ills stem, essentially, from the same source: from patterns of uncontrolled and illogical urban growth and development that waste our energy resources just as liberally as they lay waste our natural environment. We no longer live in a time when we were few and the land was wide and waiting, but have reached the point where the natural resources and amenities once regarded as endlessly available and expendable are becoming increasingly scarce.

Perhaps one of the most difficult changes which must be made is in the patterns of urban growth. No longer can we accept or tolerate leapfrog or urban sprawl development which scatters jobs, homes, shopping centers and recreation areas across the length and breadth of the landscape. The spreading patterns of settlement and development that characterize our urban areas are the legacy of our old illusion that we had endless acres of land to build on and unlimited energy to burn. The Citizen's Advisory Committee on Environmental Quality and the Task Force on Land Use and Urban Quality issued a report, "The Use of Land: A Citizen's Policy Guide to Urban Growth", which begins with the finding that Americans must begin to view land as a resource, as limited as minerals, water and clean air, and indicates that there is a new mood in America. Increasingly, people are asking what urban growth will add to the quality of their lives; they are questioning the way relatively unconstrained, piecemeal urbanization is changing their communities, and are rebelling against the traditional processes of government and the marketplace which, they believe, have inadequately guided development in the past.

This new attitude toward growth, practically unheard of a few years ago, is rapidly spreading through communities across the country. In previous years, any reasonably good developer could enter a zoning, subdividing, or permit hearing confident that, if his project was well-designed, he would get anything he wanted in the way of zoning modifications, sewer permits, and other special requirements. Local governments were more than happy to remove any potential roadblocks in the name of encouraging growth - or progress. Today, the picture is quite different. Local governments are responding to public pressure to control growth, to stop the inefficient use of land, and to safeguard their public monies from waste on urban sprawl. Studies have indicated that growth does not pay for itself and urban sprawl or leapfrog development is very costly for local governments. In 1972, the Urban Institute, Washington, D.C., studied residential units and 30 acres of commercial development in a new area

**TRANSPORTATION.** The Transportation Plan has evolved directly from the land use considerations of the revised Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. A two and one-half year multi-agency study assessed Boulder Valley transportation needs, using urban design and travel demand modeling techniques. Solutions which satisfied those needs were identified, developed, and extensively analyzed. Travel modes studied in detail included highways, public transportation, bikeways and pedestrian facilities.

Every attempt was made to insure that substantial and reliable citizen input was incorporated into the study. Random sample interviews, twenty citizens' workshops, and forty hearings were conducted to aid in the establishment of goals and objectives and to address the various alternatives. Videotapes of many technical addresses were made and placed in the public library for community use. Thirteen technical volumes were published.

**Roadway and Transit Elements - Planning Methods** A simulation model of existing travel relationships was used to forecast future trip characteristics. Projection of future increases in the study area's population and employment levels was combined with observed trip-making behavior to estimate travel demand and distribution, and to identify the prominent transportation corridors.

A modal split model was developed to indicate how people might choose between transit and automobiles. This distribution is reflected in transportation networks for evaluation of alternatives.

Mobility deficient groups, the elderly, families without cars, etc., have traditionally been transit dependent. Special care was taken to analyze the accessibility needs of this group and to insure provision of a high level of transit service to the areas where this population was concentrated.

**Projected Travel Demands.** The study found that future major travel demands on the present highway system would mandate construction of new facilities to accommodate the projected number of automobiles, even if significant use of mass transportation were to occur. These additional major facilities are the urbanized fringe areas.

35

The analysis of highways, transit facilities, bikeways, and pedestrian ways revealed serious safety, capacity, and service problems. It was therefore necessary to proceed with an analysis of alternative systems.

**Transit Element.** The transit planning effort for Boulder had two principal objectives. The first was to identify a public transit system responsive to the needs of the study area. The second was to develop recommendations for interim improvements to the existing transit system which would lead incrementally to the recommended level of service.

The more promising alternatives were evaluated against the following criteria:

- 1) patronage demand
- 2) environmental impact
- 3) assessment of technological options
- 4) structure of the urban setting which reinforces and capitalizes on transit.

Identification of the corridors resulted in the conclusion that the level of service needed is approximately double the existing amount. A second conclusion was that provision of service in excess of six times the current level would result in only fifteen percent transit usage.

A diversified bus system (Advanced Bus Concept) was determined to be the most favorable, cost-effective transit opportunity. The quantitative and qualitative comparison of alternative systems indicated it to be the best short-and medium-range solution, by providing Boulder with a substantially expanded transit service at an acceptable cost, with minimal adverse impacts, and in a manner acceptable to the majority of citizens. The longer-term situation (i.e., 1985-2000) is less well defined and must be re-evaluated at a later time.

The patronage model predicted almost 84,000 transit riders in the year 2000 which would represent almost 12 percent of the total trips made in the area. This compares with a modal split of just over two percent today.

**ENVIRONMENTAL FEATURES.** Geological factors and their attendant constraints have been important components in the development of the 1977 Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. The 1970 Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan considered limited geological data. Accumulated earth science information has been compiled in the "Urban Geology Map Series", which has been utilized in the preparation of the updated Plan.

Environmental factors can be separated into four categories: geological hazards, engineering constraints, mineral resources, and flood hazard management. Each factor discussed hereafter has definite implications for development of the updated Plan.

**Geological Hazards.**

**Mine Fire.** An area of approximately 150 acres south and east of Marshall evidences active combustion of in-place coal, surface subsidence and cracking, and surface denudation. It is the opinion of the United States Bureau of Mines' engineering staff that fire and its combustion products constitute nuisances and possible hazards to nearby residents.

**Subsidence of Undermine Areas.** Ground subsidence due to the collapse of coal mine voids has occurred in the Boulder Valley. Potentially devastating effects of collapse are graphically evident on the City-owned Richardson parcel northeast of the intersection of Cherryvale and Marshall Roads. The surrounding lands are underlain by the old Eagle Mine, which closed in 1936. Linear trench-like depressions are evident where collapse has occurred subsequent to mine abandonment.

A large percentage of the land in the Davidson Mesa area has been undermined. In addition, large areas are still underlain by mineable coal, the rights to which are held by potential extractors. House Bill 1529 (enacted in 1973) stipulates that commercially extractable coal deposits must be conserved for future utilization. Eventual extraction of these reserves could result in subsequent subsidence. The majority of this area has been designated as

41

open space. Development should not be allowed in areas designated as subsidence hazards unless extensive analysis and remedial engineering have been performed. Considering the existing state of the art of subsidence prediction, this would be an extremely formidable task.

### Engineering Constraints.

**Material Movement.** Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The Foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of material movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.

**Slope Instability.** Land slides are evident throughout Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure if modified by development. Open space buffers should be obtained on, above and below steep slopes. These safe zones should prevent slope disturbance and remove citizens from areas of hazard.

Areas of active landsliding should be off-limits to any development. Areas designated as "Potentially Unstable" require thorough slope stability analysis before decisions concerning development can be made.

**Alluvial Fans.** Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, evident along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to twenty feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating event, such as the Big Thompson flood of August 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan. Because of their hazardous character, alluvial fans are basically unsuitable for development.

**Earth Flows.** Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Examination of the deposits on the sediment surface bordering the foothills has discovered boulders exceeding twenty-five feet in diameter.

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to twenty-nine feet in diameter in silt and clay-sized mud.

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation. Saturated accumulations of debris, along and within the foothills, liquify and flow down stream channels and out across the mesa and valley surfaces. An earth flow would be extremely destructive in developed areas. Areas judged susceptible to earth flows are unsuitable for development.

**Slope Debris.** The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes. Anyone proposing development within the area shown as susceptible to debris damage should be required to provide analysis and mitigative engineering.

**Expansive Soil and Rock.** The Pierre Shale, Laramie Formation, and Fox Hills Formation each contain potentially disruptive expansive clays. The greater part of the Boulder Valley is underlain by one of these bedrock types. Proper engineering can mitigate the potential hazard. Anyone proposing development within an area designated as "Potentially Expansive"

42

should be required to supply appropriate soil testing and mitigative engineering.

**Hydrocompactible Loess.** Hydrocompaction refers to sediment volume decrease by hydration. During Neo-GLacial times, the Denver-Boulder metro area was a glacial desert. Little organic, ground-stabilizing cover existed. Prevailing westerly winds winnowed silt-sized material from the valley floors and redeposited it atop and to the leeward side of mesas and other high land forms.

When exposed to development and resultant drainage changes, these materials tend to be compacted, decreasing their volume. This volume decrease can result in destructive surface subsidence.

The potential for hydrocompaction damage exists in numerous areas within the undeveloped southern and eastern portions of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan areas. Anyone proposing development within areas designated as "Potentially Hydrocompactible" should be required to perform appropriate tests and to supply mitigative engineering.

**Mineral Resources.** Boulder County has an abundance of mineral resources. House Bill 1529 (enacted in 1973) requires the conservation and preservation of limestone, sand, gravel, quarry aggregate, and coal. The intent of the bill is to involve the subject of mineral conservation for extraction in the comprehensive planning process. To comply with the Bill, the Boulder Urban Area Master Plan for Mineral Extraction was prepared and has been included in the updated Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

**Sand and Gravel.** An extensive analysis involving consideration of project size, deposit quality, future commerciality, resource inventory, and future demand has been completed. As of 1977, approximately 38.5 million tons of high quality sand and gravel remain for potential extraction within the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan area. The proposed Boulder Urban Area Mineral Extraction Plan provides for the regulation of lands designated as "Potentially Commercial" high quality sand and gravel reserves.

**Coal.** A comprehensive analysis involving consideration of resource inventory, quality, and future commerciality has been completed for coal deposits. The proposed Land Use Regulations for Special Use Review of mineral extraction also provide for the regulation of lands designated as containing "Potentially Extractable" coal reserves.

**Oil and Gas.** Boulder has a history of oil production. **Flood Hazard Management.** The streams and creeks within the City of Boulder were formed by nature to provide channels for flood flows resulting from heavy snow melt and rainfall. Boulder's flood problems began with the initial development of the community adjacent to Boulder Creek. This development established a pattern for similar residential, commercial, or industrial construction along other portions of Boulder Creek and within other flood areas in the Boulder Valley. As a result of private and public development over the floodplain, flood heights and velocities have increased and the potential for economic loss and concern for personal safety has increased.

Boulder is particularly vulnerable to flood damage because of its unique geographic location. In addition, because of its proximity to the mouth of Boulder Canyon, the Central Area of the city is highly susceptible to the particular hazards of flash floods. In an effort to provide an overall, coordinated program to control flood situations, and to protect life and property, the City of Boulder has adopted a comprehensive Flood Hazard Management Program.

There are four main elements in Boulder's Flood Hazard Management Program. The primary effort has been the adoption of a City Flood Plain Management Ordinance which regulates construction within the area subject to 100-year flooding. Each flood plain within the Boulder Valley will be divided into two parts: first, a storage area for flood waters where development is allowed to take place if adequate floodproofing measures are taken; and, second, the floodway or flood hazard district, which is the primary channel of the 100-year flood, including the areas where waters will flow at significant depths and velocities.

A second important element of the City's Flood Hazard Management Program is the recognition of flood insurance as a key factor in reducing damages resulting from flood events. Individuals owning businesses or residences within the floodplain are informed of the availability of flood insurance and are encouraged to purchase it. It is the City's policy to work in close cooperation with the Federal Insurance Administration (FIA) in order to assure Boulder's standing in the National Flood Insurance Program, and to request FIA input on proposed projects that could significantly affect floodplain boundaries.

The third element of Boulder's Program is the flood hazard early warning system. In conjunction with Boulder County and the Urban Drainage and Flood Control District, a system of early warning for flooding has been developed and installed. The system includes weather radar, automated rain and stream gauges, and communications to activate an evacuation plan. The system is being expanded to include the South Boulder Creek Basin, as well.

The final element of the Flood Hazard Management Program includes maintenance of existing drainageway capacities, modification of man-made obstructions, and restoration of historic drainage courses. Such construction activities will be reflected in the updated master drainage plan and are intended to improve local site drainage.

The Flood Hazard Management Program provides a sound regulatory basis for guiding private and public development in flood areas, taking into account both the most desirable uses of land from the viewpoint of the community as a whole and the needs and rights of private landowners.

The County of Boulder has floodplain regulations which are generally compatible with those of the City of Boulder; therefore, transition from County to City jurisdiction as it relates to the Flood Hazard Management Program will not cause great difficulties.

The desire of Boulder citizens for education and career information, and their need for increased leisure-time activities, provided the impetus for the Library to acquire a variety of materials and to initiate many different services and programs.

The Library's policy of trying to meet the need of segments of the population not usually reached by traditional programs resulted in the establishment of the Municipal Government Reference Center and a Media Center, the development of a Homebound Services Program, and the creation of several special collections.

The George Reynolds Branch Library is located on Table Mesa Drive and serves the South subcommunity. It was designed to provide a "homelike environment" for recreational and simple reference purposes. As the population increases in other subcommunities, additional branch libraries will be considered and other outreach services will be developed.

#### HOUSING.

The Provision of Low Income Housing. The City has continually emphasized and supported programs for low income families and elderly and handicapped households. The City Council has established a Housing Authority, which, through the assistance of local banks, the Colorado State Division of Housing, and various federal agencies, has built and maintains 411 units of low income housing as of June 1982. Another 110 units are scheduled for construction or assumption by January 1983. Additionally, the Housing Authority provides 195 units through the Federal Section 8 Subsidy Program. The following are ways in which the City government meets its commitment to the provision of low income housing:

- \* The provision of monies to the Housing Authority from the General Fund and Community Development Funds for the purchase of land and the front end costs associated with the development of housing for low income persons.
- \* The exemption of all low income housing projects from the City's residential growth management system.
- \* The utilization of all available federal, state and other financial sources to provide low income housing.
- \* The construction by the Housing Authority of low income housing units built to a quality consistent with community standards and dispersed throughout the community.
- \* The provision of monies for the administrative staff of the Housing Authority.

The Provision of Affordable Housing. High housing prices are a national problem and not simply a local phenomenon: they are and have been a reflection of the national economic scene. It is appropriate that in the past, cities have taken advantage of state and federal programs through which they have aggressively provided housing for their low income population. Local governments have very few options; however, in trying to provide moderately priced housing within their communities. The City has used incentives and regulations to encourage the availability of affordable housing.

The City requires that all new development of ten or more units provide at least 15% of the units for moderate income sales or rental housing if the

45

land was annexed after December 1973, and at least 10% of the units for moderate income housing if annexed before December 1973. The price/rent of the moderate income units is controlled by the Housing Authority and the units must stay moderately priced (sales or rents) for at least ten years.

As part of the City's Growth Management Ordinance, the City awards up to 50 merit points, out of a possible 150 merit points, for moderate income units during times when the residential growth management system requires a comparative review for building permits. Additionally, reasonable density increases are granted to developers for the provision of more moderate income housing than required.

The City encourages public/private cooperation where possible in the provision of low and moderate income housing. Currently public/private partnerships are being explored to provide affordable housing through various methods. Additionally, a dialogue has been initiated between the City Council and the regents of the University of Colorado to explore ways to address common housing issues.

**Retention of Existing Housing Stock.** In an effort to save the existing housing stock of the community, the City in conjunction with local banks, has established a comprehensive housing rehabilitation program, described below:

- \* Rehabilitation grants are awarded to qualified low income homeowners using Community Development Block Grant Funds.
- \* Rehabilitation special loans are awarded to qualified lower income households who do not qualify for a grant but do not have enough income to qualify for a regular loan. Funds are provided by the City of Boulder General Fund.
- \* Rehabilitation loans are awarded to qualified households at below market interest rates. The funds for the low interest loans are made available by a consortium of local lending institutions.

The City of Boulder also has established a comprehensive rental licensing program that requires all rental properties to be inspected before they can be rented to the public. All housing code violations must be corrected before a rental license is issued.

**Energy Efficiency in Housing.** Energy conserving improvements have been made with public monies to almost the entire 1,200 low income units in the City of Boulder. These improvements have ranged from solar panels and window treatments to caulking and storm doors.

All new low income housing projects built by the Housing Authority use and will continue to use life-cycle, cost-effective technology to achieve energy efficiency. The Housing Authority is committed to energy conservation through building design and encourages a community-wide residential energy conservation program.

The rehabilitation program requires energy conservation improvements for all units that make use of the loan and grant programs. Programs are being designed which will make available materials and installation training to qualified low income renters.

**WATER SERVICE.** Historically, with few exceptions, the City of Boulder has been far-sighted in its water utility planning, thus being prepared for the major population gains of the past two decades. In the future, improvements will be staged to serve the population increases as they are allowed to occur. The planning for water system improvements will be based on estimating the new facilities which will be needed to serve those areas designated for new

46

growth. The staging of these facilities will be timed to correspond with the growth, so that service to existing customers will not be impaired.

In providing for present and future populations, Boulder's water supplies are derived from mountain regions tributary to the upper reaches of North Boulder and Middle Boulder Creeks and mountain lands in the upper reaches of the Colorado River on the Western Slope. The Colorado River waters are brought to the Eastern Slope by the Colorado-Big Thompson project which fills many reservoirs, including the Boulder Reservoir. Water in the Boulder Reservoir can then be exchanged for additional Boulder Creek water or treated and directed into the Boulder water system. The water, after being treated, is held in four reservoirs along the westerly edge of the City. A fifth reservoir is located on Cunbarrel Hill to serve the Cunbarrel area. In the future, additional reservoirs may be required at sites at the top of Hoover Hill, on Arapahoe Road, and in the foothills near Four-Mile Creek.

The distribution system is divided into three pressure zones serving ground elevations varying from 5,100 to 5,800 feet. Treated water from Boulder Creek can be delivered to all three zones by gravity through several pressure-regulating stations. Water from the Boulder Reservoir plant can also be delivered to all three zones through pump stations.

In planning additional water service, the exact timing and location of extensions will need to be related carefully to future population increases, land use, and the resulting economies of service. As a basic tenet, maintaining adequate service for current customers and adding water taps to existing lines having an excess capacity will have priority over providing new facilities.

**WASTEWATER.** Increasing concern for protection of the environment, with particular emphasis on water quality, has highlighted the importance of the City's wastewater collection and treatment system. The national goal of fishable, swimmable water by 1983, and the expressed desire of City Council to develop Boulder Creek as a sport fishery, provide the framework for wastewater treatment decisions in the Boulder Valley. Planned treatment plant capacity is governed by service area population projections for 1985 and 1995.

The wastewater collection system consists of approximately 249 miles of pipe of varying sizes, separate from the storm sewer system. Major interceptors adequate to serve the existing service area are in place. Extension of sewer mains will be coordinated with other public facilities and constructed to conform with long-range plans to achieve the most economical means of providing sewer service. Sewer mains will need to be extended within the service area where individual systems fail, to prevent public health problems from polluted groundwater.

## CENTRAL AREA POLICIES

Exhibit 2.C

The City of Boulder, through the merging of public and private interests, shall seek to retain the Central Area of the Boulder Valley, defined generally by Baseline Road, Iris Avenue, the west city limits, and 34th Street, as the primary governmental, cultural, office, financial, entertainment, shopping, medical facility, and high density residential district of the Boulder Valley.

Within the Central Area, several distinct, yet interrelated sub-areas exist: the Downtown Business District; the University; the Crossroads Area; transitional and older stable residential neighborhoods, special business districts, and hospital facilities area, and newer multi-family neighborhoods.

**DOWNTOWN BOULDER.** The Downtown shall retain its historic role as the governmental, entertainment, office, and financial center of the Boulder Valley. It shall continue to develop as a specialty retail center. This shall be accomplished by coordinating concerted and comprehensive public policies and investments with private redevelopment and rehabilitation efforts.

Pearl Street. Pearl Street is the historic downtown retail avenue of Boulder. The Pearl Street Mall, included with the surrounding business district as an historic district on the National Register of Historic Places, forms a unique focal point for downtown business and social activity. The historic character of the buildings located on the mall, in terms of their richness of materials, detail, texture, and intimate pedestrian scale, are assets which combine to create a viable pedestrian mall and city activity center. In many cases, private rehabilitation efforts have restored the original character and facade of these buildings. In order to enhance and preserve this historic character, the City shall encourage continuing redevelopment efforts which will improve the compatibility of architecture and new development which will complement and enhance the historic character of the downtown area. In addition, the City shall encourage a variety of retail, service, restaurant, entertainment, banking, and upper-story residential uses to provide expanded economic and social opportunities.

Walnut Street - Canyon Boulevard Corridor. This corridor remains in transition from its early role as an industrial area, to a major commercial, banking, and high density residential area. It is in this corridor, running from 6th Street on the west to 17th Street on the east, that the most dramatic redevelopment efforts will be encouraged to occur.

Efforts shall be made to concentrate major commercial uses between 9th and 15th Street in order to maximize the pedestrian scale potential of the Downtown Area. The City shall promote multi-use rather than single purpose projects. Each project shall respect and relate to other downtown projects in terms of orientation, pedestrian and vehicular circulation, and scale. No individual project shall become an enclave; pedestrian interfaces between projects and corridors are of paramount importance. The most appropriate uses in this area are office, retail, service, restaurant, entertainment, hotel and convention center, high-density residential, and transportation (parking, mass transit) facilities. Off-street parking shall, where feasible, be provided in well-designed and centrally located structures.

Boulder Creek. Boulder Creek is recognized as an environmentally sensitive area which offers dramatic potential as a scenic open space element to be preserved and enhanced through reasonable public investment. Priority will be devoted to developing recreational activities along the Creek such as

50

hiking, bicycling, picnicking, tubing, etc. Flood control programs will be designed so as to protect and preserve the natural character of the Creek environment, and private development in the vicinity will be encouraged to complement rather than intrude upon the tranquility of the area. Both Central Park and the mall between the Library and the Municipal Building may be utilized for more active functions such as small concerts, fairs, performing arts programs, bazaars, etc., serving as a regional support facility to the Downtown and surrounding high-density residential areas. Any new permanent structure in this area shall be designed and located to minimize the disruptive impact on the tranquil nature of this sector.

**CROSSROADS.** The Crossroads Area, described generally by Pearl Street, Folsom, Boulder Creek, and 30th Street, shall remain as a regional shopping center of the Boulder Valley. Other than the Downtown, no other regional shopping facility shall be permitted in the Boulder Valley.

Private redevelopment and upgrading of the entire area shall be a high priority of the City. The replacement of existing non-conforming buildings and marginal uses, as well as the rehabilitation and "recycling" of existing unattractive and/or obsolete buildings, shall be encouraged. Appropriate uses shall be major retail outlets and their necessary support facilities. Motels and convention facilities, restaurants, theatres, and other entertainment facilities may be permitted; however, such uses shall generally be encouraged to locate in the Downtown sector.

Innovative, non-auto "people movers" shall be promoted as essential links between activity nodes.

**TRANSITIONAL RESIDENTIAL.** Transitional residential areas are those "close-in", predominantly residential neighborhoods situated generally between Arapahoe Avenue and Spruce Street, Third and Folsom Streets, which are expected to undergo considerable future in-fill and redevelopment pressures. The City recognizes the dual responsibility of permitting selective redevelopment to occur in these areas while, at the same time, utilizing sufficient control in order to protect the basic character and integrity of these neighborhoods from major undesirable disruption. City policies in these areas will be as follows:

1. Private high density redevelopment will be encouraged through an "in-fill" approach which is designed to minimize negative impacts upon the surrounding residential areas, and through the renovation of existing structures which are designed to retain and enhance their positive characteristics. Standards utilized to measure the impacts of such developments will be the relative height and bulk of structures, compatibility with nearby properties and structures, architectural treatment (i.e., use of compatible materials and colors), landscaping, and provision of off-street parking. Neighborhood input will be sought prior to discretionary actions taken by the City. Sensitive in-fill and private redevelopment which meet the above criteria shall be considered positive alternatives to urban sprawl, an asset to the Downtown and regional shopping corridor revitalization, as well as an important energy conservation approach.
2. The City shall encourage existing sound residential structures, the rehabilitation and recycling of these structures, and the retention of mature landscaping as an integral part of any new development.
3. No large scale urban renewal and residential relocation programs to implement residential redevelopment will be undertaken. Under certain

51

circumstances, limited clearance of certain structures and nonconforming uses, which are themselves blighted or are deteriorating influences on the neighborhood, may be considered. In such cases, adequate compensation and relocation expenses would be provided to affected parties.

4. Rehabilitation assistance for residential structures may be provided to certain qualified residents within the area where long-term tenure and stability appears likely.
5. In transitional neighborhoods, upgraded public investments and services such as parks and recreation facilities, coordinated street furniture and street improvements, public transit, bikeways, etc., shall be given high priority in the Capital Improvements Program. Socio-economic programs such as day care will also be encouraged.

**OLDER, STABLE RESIDENTIAL NEIGHBORHOODS.** The remaining, predominantly residential, neighborhoods situated generally between Iris Avenue and Baseline Road, the Front Range and Folsom Road; shall be considered "stable" residential environments. In these areas, the following policies will typically apply:

1. Private redevelopment through proper conversion of existing dwellings and in-fill of vacant or marginally developed lots shall be permitted. Compatibility of such activities with surrounding neighborhood character and underlying socio-economic structure of the community shall be encouraged.
2. Significant demolition activities, even as a part of private redevelopment activities, shall be discouraged through a variety of means including the consideration of possible historic preservation or rehabilitation districts.
3. Although limited clearance to remove structures and/or non-conforming uses which are blighted or blighting influences, or to provide necessary public improvements, such as parks and open space, may be considered, no large-scale public land assemblage and/or demolition to implement redevelopment activities shall be permitted.
4. Residential rehabilitation assistance to qualified persons to enable them to meet codes and upgrade their properties shall be encouraged in these stable neighborhoods.
5. Deficiencies in public facilities and services, such as parks and recreation, coordinated street landscaping, furniture, and street improvements, etc., shall be given high priority in the City's Capital Improvements Program. In addition, social programs such as day care, crime prevention activities, library services, counseling, etc., will be encouraged as the need develops.

**NEWER MULTI-FAMILY AREAS.** Multi-family units located between Iris Avenue and Baseline Road, Folsom and 34th Streets, tend to be occupied mainly by young adults and retirement age persons. Both groups include a high percentage of single-person households. These newer multi-family developments appear to be more transient in nature than older multi-family portions of the Central Area. The unique characteristics of this area are recognized in the following policies:

1. The mix of pedestrian, bike, and vehicular traffic within and adjacent to these multi-family areas is greater than in any other portion of the Boulder Valley. Recognizing this, the City will strive to alleviate these vehicle and "people" conflicts.

52

2. Because of the number of young adults living within this area, public and private recreational activities and facilities will be encouraged to serve these residential units. In addition, accessory uses for medical services, child care, personal services, and social meeting and activity centers will also be supported by the City.

**UNIVERSITY.** The City shall support the University of Colorado's policy of limiting its enrollment to the present level of full-time students.

The University shall retain its major identity and activities in the Central Area, remaining an integral part of the revitalized core area.

The University shall be encouraged to expand its "continuing education" functions so that its role as an integral, rather than distinctive, part of the Boulder community is more effective.

The University shall be encouraged to coordinate its cultural events and activities with broad community needs to the maximum extent feasible.

The City of Boulder will continue to play a major role in the housing of full-time University students.

**HOSPITAL AREAS.** Both Community Hospital, at Balsam and Broadway, and Memorial Hospital, at Mapleton and Fourth Street, and the nearby related facilities, provide specialized and significant Boulder Valley medical facilities and services. As a result of the unusual impact of each hospital on adjacent residential areas, the following special policies are adopted:

1. Adequate public transportation to each hospital shall be provided.
2. Added pressures for adjacent multi-family development, particularly near Community Hospital, may be anticipated. Recognizing this, redevelopment of certain portions of the area between Broadway and 9th Street, Alpine Avenue and Maxwell Street, consistent with neighborhood scale, will be encouraged.
3. Joint planning of medical facilities and services by both hospitals is important in order to avoid needless duplication of effort.

**SPECIAL BUSINESS AREAS.** In addition to the Downtown and Crossroads business areas, three other smaller but distinct business areas within the Central Area require policy direction. These are University Hill area at 13th Street and College Avenue, the North Broadway retail center along Broadway south of Balsam Avenue, and the strip commercial zone on either side of 28th and 30th Streets. The following policies are appropriate to address the impacts of these areas:

1. Improved access to each center, particularly for pedestrians, bicyclists, and those using mass transportation, shall be implemented. In addition, the problems of vehicular circulation and off-street parking deficiencies shall be resolved.
2. Integration of architectural elements for each area would provide an improved community image and, at the same time, a stronger market potential. Coordinated renovation and rehabilitation of existing structures shall, therefore, be encouraged.
3. The "street-scape" in each area should be improved through coordinated landscaping, the use of berms, removal of overhead utility lines, reduction in the number of curb cuts, improved sign control, and similar methods.
4. Efforts shall be undertaken to ensure that these commercial centers are contained and, as appropriate, intensified within their present

53

boundaries rather than being permitted to intrude into adjacent residential neighborhoods. In certain situations, multi-use developments which incorporate both residential and retail facilities in a planned and complementary fashion may be permitted.

**URBAN DESIGN ELEMENT.** Each of the sub-areas of the Central Area have interrelationships which must be recognized and maintained for the entire area to remain a healthy and viable entity. The critical issues involved in determining the appropriate range of land uses for each area, the relative intensity of such uses, and the degree and rapidity with which any particular area might undergo redevelopment or change in the future have been described in the preceding policies. These policies are important determinants of Boulder's Central Area revitalization programs, be they public or private, and be they physical, social, and/or economic in their effects. Inherent in these policies and programs are recognition of, and sensitivity to, the underlying characteristics of the particular area or neighborhood under consideration.

Of additional importance is the development of adequate linkages between the subcommunities. Elements which define these linkages include the following:

1. Enhance Boulder Creek as a greenway, serving as common open space, bikeway and pedestrian thread which has the potential of unifying each subcommunity, as well as providing an attractive and functional buffer between the Downtown Area and the residential neighborhoods to the south.
2. Promote development of an improved mass transit system which directly links the major activity nodes of the Central Area, the Downtown, Crossroads, and University neighborhoods. With certain reasonable cost-benefit constraints and other impacts, such a system should represent the technological state of the art.
3. Promote development of an inviting, safe, and convenient bikeway and pedestrian system to serve and interconnect each section of the Central Area.
4. Improve the capacity of major arterial collectors in the Central Area to handle as much through traffic demand as practical in order to preserve, to the greatest extent feasible, the quiet residential character of local neighborhood streets.
5. Future strip commercial development shall be discouraged along major arterials.

**IMPLEMENTATION OF CENTRAL AREA GOALS.** A partnership of the public and private sectors must be retained throughout the planning process. Several techniques to implement the Central Area goals currently exist, such as zoning, capital improvements programming, State and Federal assistance. The City shall strive to utilize these tools in an effective, innovative, and sensitive manner. The City will explore, as well, the use of other potential techniques not presently available. Possible new tools include: impact or incentive zoning in redeveloping areas; development rights transfers; tax increment financing or creation of development authorities; economic development bonds; urban design plan for Central Area; creation of historically-or architecturally-significant landmarks and districts.

The relative size, intensity, and scale of the Central Area to the total community is vital to the entire concept of enhancing community identity and focus and the combatting of urban sprawl.

It is further recognized that, in time, the boundaries and size of the Central Area, particularly its transitional and stable older neighborhoods, may change. As these neighborhoods change, the programs most relevant to their status will be modified accordingly.

## AREA III ANNEXATION POLICIES

Exhibit 3.A

It is anticipated in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan that Area IIIA shall be annexed to the City of Boulder within three years, consistent with the phased expansion of the City's capacity to provide adequate urban facilities and services. The following statements describe very briefly and very generally the City's present annexation policies and procedures, both of which may be modified from time to time without requiring an amendment of the Comprehensive Plan. The City's annexation process may vary somewhat to fit the circumstances of any particular annexation, but shall be consistent with Policy 8.2.04.

A. State law: Among other requirements, all annexations shall comply with requirements of Colorado State Law which includes the following provisions:

1. At least one-sixth of the perimeter of the area to be annexed shall be contiguous to the city limits.
2. A petition must be presented by more than half of the landowners owning more than fifty percent of the area to be annexed. For enclaves and municipally owned property, the City may take the initiative without petition.
3. Under certain conditions, an election may be held by the property owners and registered electors within the area to be annexed.

B. Relationship to existing City boundaries and consolidation of Petitions: The City may consolidate individual properties requesting annexation into larger aggregate parcels when the request areas are adjacent and such grouping is desirable because of contiguity, compactness, or logical extensions of then-existing city boundaries, or for the more efficient processing of numerous annexation requests.

C. Zoning and land use: While zoning classifications in the County will be recognized, annexation into the City will need to conform to City zoning categories and the land use designations of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

D. Environmental features: Though detailed environmental impact statements generally will not be expected for lands within Area IIIA, commitments may be required at the time of annexation to protect parcels having special or unique natural features or hazards.

E. Public improvements: The following topics indicate the general terms and conditions that normally will be imposed for existing development at time of annexation. These conditions may vary to some degree depending upon the annexation of a particular area.

1. Water and Ditch Rights: The City shall follow the procedures outlined in Ordinance No. 4351 concerning the acquisition of first right of refusal of water and ditch rights.
2. Streets: The City will permit flexibility in the design and improvements of local streets when desired by local residents. As a condition of annexation, the City shall require the improvement of streets to levels which will insure that the City will not incur extraordinary maintenance costs as a result of new annexations. The City will consider petitions from property owners to initiate a street improvement project at any

57

time after annexation, so long as such project will not conflict with other scheduled capital improvements.

- 3. Street Lights: Following annexation, street lights may be installed, upon petition of the residents of the area, subject to Public Service Company standards and approval.
- 4. Water and Sewer: At the time of annexation, a property owner of property other than a single family residence shall connect to the City water and sewer if the lines abut the property. For a property owner of a single family residence that has an existing water and sewer system with no health-related problems, the property owner may continue use of the individual system until said system fails or needs upgrading.
- 5. Payment of Water and Sewer Plant Investment Fees and Front Foot Charges: The City will consider financing and low-income deferrals for plant investment fees and front foot charges for previously developed property within the City and developed property annexing to the City. If the property is redeveloped or changes ownership, the outstanding charges will become due and payable at that time.
- 6. Other Terms and Conditions: The items listed above will normally be imposed. Other conditions may be imposed by the City to meet the particular circumstances of any annexation and will be developed in a manner to allocate equitably the burdens, if any, of a particular annexation between the property owners and the City.
- F. Special action by the City: The City will assist property owners in Area 11A in taking positive steps toward annexation which should be in their best interests as well as those of a majority of the population of the Boulder Valley by providing factual information for these landowners. This material is expected to include capital improvements information, petitions for annexation, legal references, land use forecasts, and recent building and subdivision records. The City will allocate administrative staff time so that such persons will be available to attend public meetings and discussions with landowners in Area 11A so that such property owners may be better informed regarding annexation policies, procedures and potentials.

61

with the projected expansion of the City's urban facilities and services systems and is consistent with the City's ability to annex within three years from the date of change.

3. Other Maps. Minor adjustments to all other maps may be made in the following manner: Adjustments within the City (Area I) may be made unilaterally by the City after a referral request to the County and after the City has determined that the proposed adjustment makes no significant impact on the Comprehensive Plan; adjustment within Area II may be made by joint agreement of the City and County Planning Departments after those departments, subject to call up by the Planning Board or Commission, as the case may be, determine that the proposed adjustment makes no significant impact on the Comprehensive Plan; and adjustments to Area III may be made unilaterally by the County after a referral request to the City, and after the County has determined that the proposed adjustment makes no significant impact on the Comprehensive Plan. All other map changes shall be accomplished pursuant to formal joint amendment by all signatories of the Plan.

All changes to documents contained within the implementation section of the comprehensive plan may be accomplished by joint written and dated agreement of the signatories of the Plan. Changes shall be made where necessary to accommodate changes in the policy section of the plan. Changes will generally be made upon annual review of the plan in order to improve the efficiency or effectiveness of the implementation of the plan.

## REFERRAL PROCESS

Exhibit 3.C

As part of the cooperative planning process, the City of Boulder and County of Boulder have established the following referral process for certain types of land use and public improvements activity within the Boulder Valley. For proposed new urban development as defined in Policy 2.02, replats, rezoning, special use review, and major public improvements in the unincorporated areas of the Boulder Valley, the County Planning Department shall submit a referral to the City of Boulder Planning Department for review and comment. For proposed rezonings, annexations, capital improvements and open space acquisitions by the City, the City Planning Department shall submit a referral to the Boulder County Planning Department for review and comment. A referral is a written communication from the Planning Department of either the City or County to the Planning Department of either the County or City, respectively, in which there is contained either a request for or a response to a request for review and comment on the above-described matters relevant to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

The following guidelines shall apply to the referral process:

1. The referral process shall be a communication procedure whereby referral requests are mandatory and referral responses are optional. The receipt of all referrals shall be formally acknowledged.
2. Responses to all referrals shall be based upon the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, including all applicable policies, maps, and

62

implementation documents, and applicable codes, ordinances and resolutions of the respective jurisdictions.

3. All referral requests and responses of departments of the City and County shall be received, reviewed, and communicated through the respective planning departments, with the understanding that referral responses may be reviewed by the Planning Board or Planning Commission and/or the City Council or Board of County Commissioners of the referee as may be determined by the referee in its discretion or as required by law.

4. Complete referral responses shall be made promptly and in no case later than fifteen working days from date of referral receipt unless the referee notifies the referor in writing that it intends to extend the referral response time to a date certain and the referee states the reasons for the extension. In no case shall a referral extend beyond 90 days. Failure to respond within 90 days in the case of an extension may be regarded by the referor as a response with no conflict. The referor shall consider all referral responses or the fact that there has been no referral responses before proceeding with the activity proposed in the referral request.

5. For all new urban development proposed outside the City, the City shall not accept any applications for water and sewer permits until the sketch plan or special use review has been approved by the County. The County shall not hold public hearings concerning new urban development proposed in the Boulder Valley unless it has first received a referral response from the City, or unless the City has failed to make a referral response as required by paragraph (4) above.

6. The City shall not grant or deny applications for out-of-City water and sewer permits for development activities in Area II unless it has first received a referral response from the County, or unless the County has failed to make a referral response as required by paragraph (4) above.

7. All open space designated land, and land designated for acquisition as a City park, proposed for development, shall be reviewed by the Open Space Board of Trustees or Park and Recreation Advisory Board, as appropriate, for action. After negotiation with the property owner and time for an appraisal, if necessary, the Board, as appropriate, shall recommend whether to acquire at that time or to defer any action to some time in the future.

## URBAN SERVICE AND CRITERIA STANDARDS. Exhibit 3.E

### 1. Criteria.

The following criteria are to be used in the determination of the adequacy of proposed or existing urban facilities and services :

**A. Responsiveness to Public Objectives.** It is desirable and necessary that all urban service systems be coordinated and integrated with other service systems; evaluated periodically for need, efficiency, and cost effectiveness; and studied for possible duplication of other service systems so as to be responsive to local public objectives and general public need as determined by the governing body.

As public funds and resources are limited, primary emphasis must be given to an effective allocation system which, to the greatest extent possible, effects a consistency of legislative intent; public policy; urban service programs funding; a periodic assessment of the type, quantity and quality of various urban services; realistic estimates of revenue sources and future income; maximization of the availability of outside funding sources (state, federal, etc.); and consistency of the long-range program direction in accordance with a capital improvements plan. It has been determined that the municipal budget of the City of Boulder is such an allocation system.

**B. Sufficiency and Dependability of Financing.** Financing for each urban service program must be based, to the greatest extent possible, on predictable annual revenues which are broad enough to support initial improvements, maintenance, and extensions of facilities and services in relation to, at least, minimum program requirements and unexpected contingencies. Where financing is tied solely to users' fees, serious problems may result if and when the demand for such programs may diminish. At the same time, programs relying heavily on real property revenues or state and federal supplemental funding could be severely restricted if such sources of financing fall short of initial predictions. While no guarantees can be given, financing from a variety of potential sources and spread over a broad base, including sufficient latitude so that funding can be adjusted in the future as changing conditions occur, should be preferred over single-source revenue programs.

**C. Operational Effectiveness.** Each urban service program may have distinct operational needs and a wide array of operational activities might be considered. The end result, though, is whether or not a given public program is able to function in a direct, efficient manner. As an example, a volunteer fire department, depending upon personnel employed in other endeavors and scattered throughout the area of service may not respond as quickly and effectively as firefighting personnel located where the fire suppression equipment is stored. Also a small sewage treatment plant receiving only limited flows might not be adequately financed to permit proper and continuous monitoring and management.

Measures of operational effectiveness include current and long-range project forecasting, coordination with other urban service programs, maximization of economies of scale in urban service provision, and the incorporation of operational processes and organizational methods that have proven effective in similar situations.

77

**D. Proficiency of Personnel.** Conditions and factors which may affect the competency of personnel include the following: 1) education and experience of personnel in meeting job demands; 2) interest and willingness of personnel to implement programs; 3) on-the-job training opportunities; 4) working conditions and fringe benefits related to employees' effectiveness; 5) the ability of the urban service agency to pay salaries commensurate with personnel requirements; and, 6) the effectiveness of recruitment programs.

As with other criteria, extremes may be involved, but, in general, the measure of whether or not urban services are being provided must, in part, be determined by the skills of individuals carrying out such programs. For each job a comparison should be made of qualifications of the individual employed or to be employed, against personnel standards established by generally recognized public or technical agencies for similar positions.

**E. Adequacy of Equipment and Facilities.** This factor may be more easily measured than any of the other since "generally accepted standards" are involved. As an example, as residences are located in closer proximity to each other, interest in developing neighborhood parks will normally increase. As evidenced by recreation programs desired by similar population groupings, certain standards for park areas, spaces and facilities may be predicted. In a similar manner, most of the other public services and facilities described in this study have minimum locational space, equipment and building needs related to given population groupings.

**II. Service Standards.** The remainder of this document contains standards for necessary urban services, according to the five primary criteria outlined above. The required urban services and facilities are as follows: public water, public sewer, urban fire protection, urban police protection, urban transportation, and urban parks. Each of the preceding six services (together with schools) are considered necessary for service to residential areas; the first five (excluding parks and schools) are required to serve industrial and commercial areas.

## **WATER UTILITY MASTER PLAN.** Exhibit 3.F

**Introduction.** In 1975, Black & Veatch presented to the City of Boulder a comprehensive study of, and a long range plan of improvements for, its water works. The improvement plan developed in this 1975 report reflected the then current City of Boulder plans for population growth and service area. Facilities were proposed to serve a 1980 population of 102,000, expandable to serve an ultimate population of 205,000. Since 1975, the City and County have adopted a new Comprehensive Plan of land use which includes more extensive greenbelt areas. The City and County, through their various powers, are controlling the direction, timing, and density of growth.

The changed development patterns wrought by the City and County actions since 1975 resulted in a smaller area to be served by the Water Utility. The projected total population within this service area has been significantly reduced, and the location of that population changed. These changed conditions occasioned the need for an updated plan of improvements.

The projected 1992 service area to be supplied water by the Boulder Water Utility is that area established by the City and County in the Comprehensive Plan. Planning for major facilities which would be difficult to enlarge or expand is based on projected ultimate population. Planning for all other facilities is based on projected 1992 population.

Scope of the study is limited to the following:

- \* Review of the new Comprehensive Plan and estimates of future populations and the probable location of those populations was provided by the City Planning Department.
- \* Review raw water supply facilities and water treatment requirements and develop a program of improvements to serve the projected ultimate population.
- \* Construct computerized models of the existing and proposed water distribution system; utilize the models to plan and analyze distribution system improvements; and maintain the models to allow analysis of specific problems as and when requested by the Water Utility.
- \* Present a general plan of improvements for expansion of the water works facilities scheduled to meet the water demands as the population within the service area increases, and present the probably capital costs of those improvements.

**Treatment.** The City currently operates two wastewater treatment plants, the 75th Street Plant and the East Pearl Plant. The main plant is the 75th Street Plant which has a design capacity of 15.6 mgd and is a secondary treatment facility utilizing the trickling filter process with sludge handling by lime stabilization, vacuum filtration, and landfill disposal. A project is currently under construction which includes the addition of anaerobic digesters, an administration building, a maintenance building, odor control facilities, and a new headworks to the 75th Street Plant. This project is scheduled for completion in December, 1979.

The East Pearl Plant is currently operated as a base-loaded primary treatment facility with effluent returned to the interceptor sewer and subsequently treated at the 75th Street Plant. Sludge is treated by anaerobic digestion and is injected into the soil on a site adjacent to the plant. It is anticipated that treatment at the East Pearl Plant will be discontinued when the anaerobic digesters at the 75th Street Plant are complete.

Future anticipated improvements to the 75th Street Plant are the installation of a sludge injection system similar to the system now being used at the East Pearl Plant and liquid stream improvements to improve the quality of the plant effluent. Both of these improvements are being held up pending evaluation by the Environmental Protection Agency.

The major purpose of the liquid stream improvements will be to provide nitrification. Two processes currently under consideration include infiltration-percolation basins and additional on-site biological treatment. The City, in conjunction with the University of Colorado, is currently running pilot studies on infiltration-percolation basins and three methods of additional biological treatment, namely, activated sludge, rotating biological contactors, and a biological fluidized-bed.

### I. INTRODUCTION

The development of park land in Boulder began with the purchase of Chatuauqua in 1898. The voters approval of a bond proposal to fund the acquisition of this 80 acre site which was leased to the Texas-Chautauqua Association reflected the enthusiastic desire of the citizens of Boulder to develop land for recreational and cultural activities. The following year the City received a grant from the United States Congress for acquiring 1,800 acres of mountain land to be used and preserved for parks and recreation.

Over the next few years, major emphasis was placed on the acquisition of land for park use which eventually lead to the development of the Boulder City Improvement Association in 1903. The purpose of the Association was to develop a plan for city improvements including the additional development of park lands.

An official Park Board was later developed to complement the efforts of the Association by recommending park land acquisitions and administering a small budget for park maintenance.

103

In 1908 the improvement Association invited Frederick Law Olmsted, Jr. to study Boulder to determine the physical improvements needed to make the city an agreeable and pleasant place to live and work. Olmsted made many recommendations to improve Boulder; one of his suggestions was the provision of adequate parks and open spaces. This report by Olmsted essentially became the first master plan involving proposals for city parks.

In the following years the efforts of the Park Board were recognized, resulting in the creation of the Planning and Parks Commission. This Commission was to provide a comprehensive approach to guide the development of the City of Boulder. The expanding growth of Boulder in the subsequent years led to a division of the Planning and Parks Commission into separate boards for Planning and for Parks and Recreation, and the Boulder Parks and Recreation Department was formed.

Today, the Parks and Recreation Department serves a vital function to the recreational needs of the community. The Department's responsibility includes the development and maintenance of parks, open space and recreational facilities and programs in an effort to furnish the citizens of Boulder with a broad spectrum of recreational and leisure-time activities.

104

The development and utilization of the Parks and Recreation Master Plan serves as an important guideline for the projection and subsequent implementation of the goals of the City related to providing parks and recreation services. The main objective of the Master Plan is to provide parks and recreation programs to serve the needs of the citizens and thereby enhance the quality of life in the City of Boulder.

While this objective will be accomplished primarily through traditional means, innovative ideas and new technologies should be closely scrutinized to assess their ability to provide better and less expensive service to the citizenry. Mobile facilities to take programs into areas that have limited programmable space, especially in the core area, computerized services through program registration, the use of cable TV to take programs to the public, and working with private and commercial developers to include public recreational services in their developments are but a few examples of methods for achieving the overall objectives of the Plan.

In addition, a comprehensive effort should be made to enhance the overall quality of the urban environment of Boulder. Preservation of the vistas to the mountain backdrop, street and right-of-way beautification programs,

105

and sensitive landscape developments within the urban setting are three examples of elements which contribute to the quality of the urban environment in Boulder.

Although these issues will not be addressed further in the Parks and Recreation Master Plan, they are recognized as factors which impact the demand for parks and recreation facilities.

The Master Plan is designed to function as a flexible guide in providing park and recreation facilities and programs responsive to the desires of the citizens of Boulder. The development projected in the Master Plan will be implemented in coordination with the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, and serve as a supplement to the overall plan.

106

## II. PARKS AND RECREATION STANDARDS

Park and Recreation Standards were conceptually endorsed by the Boulder City Council during its study session July 31, 1979, and it is these Standards which are the foundation upon which this Master Plan is established. The intent of this Plan is to cover the planning period 1980 through 1992 by providing specific direction for the future development of parks and recreation facilities and programs and serving as a supplement to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. The establishment of the Standards for Neighborhood and Community Parks takes into consideration the recreational opportunities provided by the Open Space and Mountain Park lands as well as the adequacy of existing Neighborhood and Community Parks to meet current demands.

Considerable emphasis is placed on the Standards for Neighborhood and Community Parks since these are the basic elements within the park system. The size and distribution Standards for Neighborhood and Community Parks are expressed as ranges rather than specific inflexible figures (i.e. Neighborhood Parks should be 5 to 10 acres with a service radius of 1/2 to 3/4 mile). This size range

107

reflects the fact that available sites often have some areas that are undevelopable thus requiring more than the minimum size in order to provide adequate space for a full range of recreational amenities. Also, in cases where the location of barriers makes it logical to expand the service radius to the upper limit (3/4 mile) additional developable land may be needed because of the larger population being served. These same factors also apply in determining the actual size of Community Parks within the size range provided in the Standard for Community Parks.

Currently, the ratio of Neighborhood and Community Parks to the population of the city as a whole is 2.5 acres per 1000 population. This ratio reflects the fact that older portions of the city fall significantly below newer areas which come much closer to the park Standard of 3.5 acres per 1000 population established by the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan (1.5 acres for Neighborhood Parks and 2 acres for Community Parks). By illustration a survey of the existing parks in the area south of Baseline Road found the existing park ratio (4.03/1000) closely approximated the adopted Standard, and will

108

park size is able to serve many people participating in a variety of activities at the same time.

Substandard or supplemental Neighborhood Park areas do not allow adequate space to be able to provide a complete range of park services for the entire neighborhood, and thus these substandard parks usually serve only a limited number of age or activity groups. There are, however, situations where supplemental Neighborhood Parks (or Neighborhood Playgrounds) provide the level of service necessary to address a specific need within the community.

One such example is by providing a children's activity area in a residential community which is isolated from and inaccessible to other Neighborhood Park areas by the younger children.

A site such as this should be 1 to 2 acres in size and designed to meet the most important concern of the sub-neighborhood. Development in such a Neighborhood Playground would include a children's play area, turfed open play space, and landscaping.

The concept of Mini-Parks, 1 to 2 residential lots in size, provides limited recreational opportunity. Mini-Parks may however be selected as an alternative in meeting some of the Neighborhood Park needs in special and severe situations where no other suitable

111

alternative exists. It should be recognized, however, that both Mini-Parks and Neighborhood Playgrounds require higher operating and maintenance costs because of their scattered locations and the lack of the economy of scale afforded by the larger park unit.

### COMMUNITY PARKS

The Community Park should be 50 to 100 acres located within 1-½ to 3 miles of the population served, and it should be located in an area that is readily accessible by the major modes of transportation within the community. It should be designed to accommodate the recreational needs of all age groups within the community to be served, and it should provide a wide variety of both active and passive activities. It should also be located and designed into the community in such a way as to minimize any adverse effects between the more active spaces and the residential community. In much the same manner as the Neighborhood Park is compatible with the elementary school site, the Community Park might be compatible with both the junior and senior high school sites. Applying the size and distribution Standards outlined above, and assuming that Community Parks will average 75 acres,

112

the Standard of 2 acres per 1000 population established by the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan will be achieved.

Typical elements of a Community Park design might include turfed and landscaped areas for picnicking, walking, sitting, and other passive enjoyment activities as well as the more active play fields, play courts, playgrounds, racquet facilities, swimming pool, and recreation center building. Unique and one of a kind facilities may also be included such as water or unique natural features, community gardens, or a special recreational activity. Community Parks should also serve to meet the Neighborhood Park needs for the residents within a 3/4 mile radius provided reasonable pedestrian access is available.

### REGIONAL PARKS

Regional Parks should serve the entire city and, to some degree, the population within one hour driving time of such parks. Regional Parks are normally located to protect or preserve some valuable recreational resource, such as lake or mountain environments, and the recreational use normally centers around these resources. Other

113

recreational facilities such as picnic areas, trails, nature centers, golf courses, botanical gardens, and playfields may also be accommodated if the site is suitable for such development.

### PARK CORRIDORS

These areas are typically linear in character, generally following sensitive environmental corridors such as streams and ridgelines. Park Corridors provide linkages between other park areas and serve as pedestrian ways with trails to link various service areas within the community. In addition, they provide some recreational opportunities for picnicking and spontaneous recreational activities. These corridors may be highly manicured areas in the urban setting while preserved in a natural state in the rural areas. There is no applicable standard for Park Corridors as they are responsive to the location and amount of environmentally sensitive areas within the community. Since many of these sensitive areas have already been designated as open space under the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, these two programs should be closely coordinated.

114

### PRESERVES

Selected park areas or portions of a park may be designated as Natural Preserves. These areas receive only minimal maintenance to eliminate hazards, remove trash and debris, and accommodate minimal public access for hiking and nature study. There are no set standards for the provision of Preserve areas as they are subject to the availability of significant natural areas.

### ATHLETIC COMPLEXES

Outdoor athletic playfields (i.e. softball, soccer, baseball, rugby, and football fields) because of their intense use, are not acceptable activities to be located in Neighborhood and most Community Parks. These playfields should be brought together into Athletic Complexes isolated from interference with the residential community and separate from most park areas. Athletic Complexes may be compatible with both Community Parks (provided that additional land is acquired specifically for this purpose) and Regional Parks. Due to the potential impacts such athletic facilities may have upon a residential area, it is also very appropriate and usually desirable to locate Athletic Complexes away from residential areas and close to arterial transportation linkages to

115

facilitate ease of access to and from the complex. Multi-purpose facilities with maximum flexibility should be provided whenever possible to accommodate full year use and to provide for possible fluctuations in demands for athletic programs. Due to this fluctuating demand for athletic activities which is exemplified by the rapid increases in participation rates during recent years, establishing a specific standard to be applied in this Master Plan is not practical. Therefore, the Standard for Athletic Complexes shall be to provide as a minimum the same relative level of service in the future that is demanded by the community at the present time. This is determined by applying the current demand for facilities per population and to the anticipated growth in population during the planning period. The annual review of the Parks and Recreation Capital Improvements program provides an appropriate vehicle to revise the projections for Athletic Complexes as demands increase or decrease.

Many of the future needs for Athletic Complexes may be accommodated by joint development of junior high and high school sites with the Boulder Valley School District and with the University of Colorado; however, adequate provision should be made to assure the availability of future sites to respond to changing needs for athletic facilities.

116

### RECREATION CENTERS

Recreation Centers should be located in Community Parks because of the complementary recreational opportunities provided by the Community Park and the ease of access which should be present. Based on current usage of the existing centers, a Standard of one Recreation Center per 40,000 population should be the minimum desirable Standard since the existing Recreation Centers have reached and possibly exceeded reasonable capacity. This Standard is based upon future Centers being comparable to the existing Centers which provides gymnasium, indoor pool, handball/racquetball courts class activity rooms, and multi-purpose activity spaces. However, indoor recreational facilities for city-wide special programs (i.e. gymnastics center, 50 meter pool for competitive swimming, etc.) should be combined with a Recreation Center for economy of operation.

### SPECIAL FACILITIES

These include, but are not limited to, golf courses, racquetball/handball centers, ice arenas, and tennis centers. Because of the specialized function of these facilities they should be located in easily accessible locations which are particularly suitable for the intended use. Neighborhood and Community Parks are not normally suitable locations nor do they provide

117

sufficient space. Regional Parks and Athletic Complexes might, however, be able to accommodate some of these facilities. Due to the fluctuating nature of demand for these activities, it is difficult to establish specific standards. For purposes of this Master Plan the projected need for Special Facilities is based only upon current demand per population, projected directly upon population growth.

#### CORE AREA PARKS

The older portions of the city are addressed within the framework of the concepts outlined above, but it is recognized that existing conditions dictate some modifications to the Standards for new park development.

#### IV. THE PLAN

In applying the Standards outlined in a previous section to the City of Boulder as it presently exists and as it is projected in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, a system of complete Park and Recreation services evolves.

In order to effectively discuss the situations affecting the Neighborhood Parks and to be able to more readily address the influential factors surrounding them, the city has been divided into 5 Neighborhood Park planning areas. The boundary lines for these planning areas in most cases are clearly defined major divisions within the community. The exception to this is the common boundary between the Core and the North Areas. The Core Area delineated on this plan is the same Core Area as identified by the Community Development Program, and since this area is presently receiving special planning emphasis it is desirable that this plan also reflect this special emphasis.

Discussions of other components of the Master Plan will be directed in a city-wide context since they address a much broader section of the community, and the area they serve is not restricted in most instances by major transportation arterials or geographical dividing lines.

### NEIGHBORHOOD PARKS:

#### Core Area

The Core Area is bordered by Balsam Avenue on the north, 28th Street on the east, Baseline Road on the south, and the mountains on the west. The Area is characterized as being the older portion of the city and contains a high concentration of residential units, shopping facilities, offices and entertainment centers. The Area is highly influenced by three major activity nodes: the Central Business District, the University of Colorado, and the Crossroads Shopping Center immediately across 28th Street. The Central Business District is the center of regional activities containing the Downtown Mall, and both the Municipal and County government complexes.

The Core Area contains 4 Neighborhood Parks (Eben G. Fine, North Boulder, Canyon and Beach), a Neighborhood Playground (Barker), various mini-parks (Fitzpatrick, 5th and Mountain View, 5th and Pearl, Canyon Pointe, and Goss/Grove), and is served by 5 Neighborhood Parks (Olmssted, Salberg, Columbine, Scott Carpenter, and Chautauqua) immediately outside the Area boundary. It should be noted that this Plan recognizes that Scott Carpenter Park contains some facilities of community-wide use (i.e. a large swimming pool and a lighted ballfield) but it is more appropriately classified as a

126

Neighborhood Park since it does not have the potential to be developed as a Community Park facility.

Due to the complete buildout within the Area, vacant property does not exist for any significant park land purchases, and the expense of property in this Area further inhibits such acquisitions.

The emphasis in the Core Area is to provide Neighborhood Park services within 3/4 mile radius of the residences in the Area but not necessarily within a single Neighborhood Park site. By projecting these 3/4 mile radii from the 9 Neighborhood Parks, the entire Core Area is currently within the service area of a Neighborhood Park. However, due to the limited size of most of these parks, they are unable to provide the full services to the public that Neighborhood Parks should provide. The most apparent shortcoming of Neighborhood services appears to be the lack of open playfields.

The recent Whittier School/Park improvements in conjunction with the complete re-development of Canyon Park should meet the Neighborhood Park needs of that section of the planning area. Similar joint use and improvement agreements with the Boulder Valley School District for other sites such as Lincoln, Casey Bluff, Mapleton, Boulder High, Flatirons and University Hill Schools should be pursued in order to provide the full range of services needed to meet the demands for

127

Neighborhood Park amenities in the Core Area. It should not be necessary to enter into joint development agreements on all of these sites, but each one should be evaluated in depth to determine the desirability of joint development. The Saint Gertrudes site owned by the University of Colorado should also be recognized as providing Neighborhood Park services even though it is not under the ownership of the City.

Redevelopment of the existing older parks will also provide a broader range of Neighborhood Park services to the community. The continuation of the redevelopment of Canyon Park presently underway as well as the proposed redevelopment of Eben G. Fine Park are two such programs and this effort should be continued at the other park sites as the need for improvement and rehabilitation dictates.

Special use areas such as the Downtown Mall, Boulder Creek/ Municipal Complex, and Columbia Lodge Cemetery (currently used for jogging and various passive recreational activities) present unique recreational opportunities to the area residents and sensitive attention should be directed to these facilities to enhance their potentials to provide Neighborhood Park services.

Simply providing the Neighborhood Park services to the people of the Core Area is not enough, however. Some special problems are evident, such as pedestrian accessibility

128

to park areas. The residents of the Goss/Grove area are without good access to Canyon Park, a very short distance away, due to the vehicular traffic volumes along Canyon Boulevard. The residents in the southern portion of the planning area are similarly separated from Chautauqua Park by Baseline Road. By working in close cooperation with other City departments, problems such as these can be solved and additional services provided to the public.

By employing all of the alternatives outlined above in the Core Area, the City will be able to provide the full Neighborhood Park services to the people of this area. All Neighborhood Park services may not be available at a single site, but they will be available within a 3/4 mile service radius.

The implementation of Mini-Parks within the Core Area should continue to be carefully considered as an alternative in meeting the Neighborhood Park needs, and initiated in special and severe situations when no other suitable alternative exists.

### COMMUNITY PARKS:

The City of Boulder presently contains one Community Park facility, Harlow Platts Park in the South Area of the city. Harlow Platts supplies a complete range of Community Park services and no substantial improvements or additional facilities are projected.

To supply the Community Park needs for the north community of Boulder, a substantial expansion of Frederick Law Olmsted, Jr. Park is needed. This park site is best suited to service the North Area needs because of its accessibility to the public and the available vacant property to the north of the park. The acquisition of as much of this vacant land as possible is recommended in order to adequately meet the needs for such a Community Park facility. Even though it will not be possible to acquire enough land for a full scale Community Park, a substantial improvement in Community Park services to this area of the city can be achieved.

A third Community Park is needed to meet the needs of East Boulder. The park site should be as central as possible to the residential community in this section of town and should be easily accessible. A site adjacent to either existing or proposed open space would be an ideal location in order to allow complementary adjacent land uses.

153

A fourth Community Park site is projected as being needed within the planning period for the extreme northeast residential community of Boulder, assuming this area annexes within this period. This site should also be as centrally located as possible to the community it serves with direct access to at least one major arterial. As discussed with the East Boulder site, adjacent open space property would be an asset.

These four Community Parks, each located in separate identifiable residential communities should adequately meet the needs of Community Park services for the Boulder Valley during this planning period.

#### REGIONAL PARKS:

The City of Boulder presently operates and maintains two Regional Park facilities in the Boulder Valley. The first is the Mountain Parks west of Boulder which contain approximately 4,200 acres of property. The other is Boulder Reservoir northeast of the city which is 1,400 acres in size. Both of these sites contain some limited development and improvements, most of which are very old and in immediate need of repair and refurbishment.

154

The water based recreational activities of Boulder Reservoir, particularly the boating activities, are in greater demand than the reservoir can adequately handle. Due to this situation it is desirable to pursue during the planning period an additional water based regional recreational activity area to help meet the needs of the public.

Beyond this, however, the Regional Park emphasis should be to upgrade, improve, and develop existing Regional Parks. In-depth restoration and development plans should be prepared for the Mountain Parks and Boulder Reservoir, each under separate cover from this Master Plan. By developing and improving our present resources and providing for an additional water based activity area, the Regional Park needs for Boulder should be adequately met through the planning period.

#### PARK CORRIDORS:

The primary emphasis for Park Corridors during the planning period will be directed at Boulder Creek along its course through the urban planning area. This emphasis will be on preserving and/or improving the recreational and aesthetic amenities of the Park Corridor through the urban area while at the same time securing and perpetuating the natural creek area in the rural portions of the community. The primary element of the development plans for this Corridor will be the provision

155

of a hike and bike trail along its entire length. Special attention will be given to this area to insure that future urban development along Boulder Creek recognizes the need to protect this very important Park Corridor. It is important that other City plans, such as Transportation, Flood Control and the Crossroads redevelopment proposal reflect this planning objective as well.

Other corridors have been designated as open space, both in the urban and rural sections of the planning area. It is essential that both the Parks and Recreation and Open Space Departments coordinate their efforts very closely in order to achieve a unified system of future corridor spaces which will serve as vital linkages within the Boulder Valley. The emphasis for these other corridors during the planning period should be directed at preserving these environmentally sensitive areas.

**PRESERVES:**

The Department of Parks and Recreation presently controls lands in the Mountain Parks, Boulder Reservoir (prairie dog habitat), and Sawhill Ponds which are environmentally sensitive and are representative of natural and wildlife Preserves. The complete inventory and identification of these areas in the Mountain Parks requires in-depth evaluation and special emphasis should be given to

156

this concern in the future Mountain Parks Master Plan. The Sawhill Ponds Preserve is owned by the State of Colorado and leased and controlled by the City of Boulder. The wildlife resources here are significant and special consideration should be given to this Preserve to insure the perpetuation of the habitat.

The Open Space Departments of the City and Boulder County both control land areas that contain or have the potential to contain environmentally sensitive ecosystems and this same type of preservation and perpetuation of the resources contained should be continued.

It is felt that environmentally sensitive lands within the Boulder Valley Area have been previously identified and either have been purchased or proposed for purchase by either the City or the County. However, if it should become apparent during this planning period that additional significant resources exist within the planning area, special attention should be directed at obtaining or insuring the perpetuation of these resources in the public interest.

#### OUTDOOR ATHLETIC COMPLEXES:

Due to the fluctuating nature of the demands for programs which would be most properly served through Athletic Complexes, the projected need for additional facilities is solely based upon the population increases

157

placing extra demands upon the presently needed facilities and not based upon expansion of the athletic program. Therefore it must be recognized that if demands for program expansion are placed upon these facilities, these projections must be revised. As previously mentioned it is necessary to remove these types of intensively used athletic facilities from Neighborhood and inadequate Community Parks and relocate them in appropriate Athletic Complexes easily accessible by the public and removed from residential development. These Complexes should be multi-purpose in design to accommodate maximum flexibility in use and allow for the changing and fluctuating nature of the demand for these facilities. It is important that the Department purchase or obtain agreements for the use of lands for these necessary Complexes as soon as possible in order to phase the construction process as the demand for fields dictates. Future joint use agreements with the Boulder Valley School District should also provide for adequate practice space for these activities.

### RECREATION CENTERS:

Recreation Center services are currently provided by the North and South Boulder Recreation Centers and the recreation center facilities of the University of Colorado which meets the needs of the large number of students and staff associated with the University. However before the end of the planning period an additional Center will be needed to

### Tennis

The projected population growth during the planning period requires that a minimum of 16 additional tennis courts be developed in order to meet the current ratio of 1 court/2,500 population within the City. It has been demonstrated at the 2 existing recreation centers that tennis courts can be more effectively used and revenues can be generated to help offset operating expenses if the tennis courts are placed in minimum groups of 4 and controlled through the center.

In order to also meet the present needs for adequate tournament and instructional activities, an 8 court complex should be located at the Stazio Recreation Complex on the University of Colorado's East Campus as the initial phase of the 16 court complex provided for under the current contract with the University of Colorado. The second priority for tennis court development is at the two proposed Community Park sites in east and northeast Boulder where facilities of 4 courts each should be provided. If additional courts are needed to serve a particular area of the community they may be located in Neighborhood Parks provided that the need is from within that neighborhood,<sup>7</sup> the park is of sufficient size to handle the activity, and the facility is not lighted.

162

### Racquetball/Handball

There are presently 40 reservation requests turned away each day for the existing 2 handball/racquetball courts. This translates to an immediate need of 5 additional courts to meet our current demand.

Population growth is expected to increase this need to an additional 3 courts during the planning period.

In order to meet this demand, the tennis facility to be located at the Stazio Recreation Complex should contain 4-6 racquetball/handball courts, all of which should be controlled through one Special Facility Center.

Provisions should also be made within the new recreation center for development of at least 2 courts, and any additional courts which may be needed due to growth in demand should be incorporated into the 2 existing recreation centers if feasible.

### Golf

The current needs for a public golf facility are presently the responsibility of the city-owned Flatirons Golf Course which is operated through a lease agreement with Flatirons Country Club. Assuming that Flatirons is presently serving the public needs, it is reasonable to project a need for an expansion of 9 additional holes for this facility during the planning period. The basic options available to the City at this time are to expand the present course and retain the City's interest in the

163

facility, or to sell the golf course and build a new facility. In order to arrive at a suitable alternative of the above options, a detailed evaluation must be completed well in advance of the expiration of the current lease in 1986.

### Ice Arena

Currently the need for an ice arena in the City of Boulder is being met by the privately owned Flatirons Ice Arena and the University of Colorado facility, and the current demand for indoor ice skating is marginally capable of supporting these facilities. Assuming there continues to be a sufficient demand to support these ice arenas, this need can best be met by the present facilities. It is recommended that the City should not provide an indoor ice arena unless a financial feasibility study were prepared which showed that there is adequate demand to support an arena.

### Swimming

Existing aquatic facilities provided by the City are the 2 indoor pools at the Recreation Centers, Spruce Pool, Scott Carpenter Pool and the swimming beach at Boulder Reservoir. Boulder Reservoir is a special facility of regional significance and is therefore not included in projecting the future needs of swimming facilities for the City of Boulder. It should be considered, however, for

164

swimming beach expansion under the development of Boulder Reservoir. Based upon population growth, the city will need at least one additional swimming pool within the planning period. It is anticipated that this additional pool will be provided at the proposed new Recreation Center. If possible, this pool should be a 50 meter indoor pool in order to provide a more complete aquatics program to the city as a whole.

### Gymnastics

The City's gymnastics program is currently housed in leased space at the Flatirons Ice Arena. This space should be replaced within the development of the proposed new Recreation Center by providing a special activity area for gymnastics. This activity area should be designed to allow for reasonable program expansion during the planning period.

### Special Interest Facilities

There are many special interest groups such as the Boulder Aeromodelers Society and the Boulder Rifle Club that provide needed recreational services to the public. Recognizing this, the City should make every effort to work with special interest groups and allow them temporary use and development of vacant parkland where practical. Such use and development would be conditional on the approval of the Parks and Recreation Advisory Board with

site development and maintenance subject to Departmental standards and commensurate with the particular site and its location.

## V. IMPLEMENTATION

The acquisition and development of parks and recreation facilities proposed within this Master Plan are anticipated to be implemented by 1992, which is the end of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan planning period. The principal controlling factors to the implementation schedule are the timing and location of actual growth and the ability of the City to finance the necessary operating and maintenance costs of the proposed acquisition and development.

Potential funding sources for implementing the proposed park and recreation improvements include the following:

Permanent Park Fund - currently this funding source provides approximately \$300,000 per year, which is generated primarily from the .9 mill assessment on all real property within the city. In addition to the .9 mill levy, park fees derived from subdivision contributions are deposited in this fund and total approximately \$30,000 per year at the current park fee schedule.

166

General Fund - due to the limited amount of funding available from the Permanent Park Fund, the General Fund has been used for some parks and recreation capital improvement projects; however, it is not realistic to expect that any significant portion of this Master Plan could be implemented from the General Fund.

Parks and Recreation Bond Programs - this source of funding offers a distinct advantage of allowing construction at current costs with actual payment over a period of years. A bond program would, however, require authorization of additional taxes or new revenues in order to retire the debt.

Conservation Trust Funds - in previous years this fund has provided very limited funding (approximately \$25,000/year) from the State of Colorado to support local parks and recreation projects. In 1979 the State Legislature eliminated funding for this program; however, there will be an issue before the voters in the State of Colorado in November of 1980 to authorize a State administered sweepstakes, with proceeds to go into the Conservation Trust Fund. If passed, this could provide a significant amount of funds to support the implementation of the Master Plan as well as subsidize the operating and maintenance costs of parks and recreation facilities constructed with funds from this source. Based on current projections it is estimated Boulder might receive \$400,000 per year from

167

the proceeds of the sweepstakes.

Federal Grants - this source of funding includes categorical grants on a matching basis for specific projects, and block grants such as those provided under the Revenue Sharing Program and the Community Development Program. The principal source of categorical grants for parks and recreation facilities is the Land and Water Conservation Trust Fund administered by the Heritage Conservation and Recreation Service.

Categorical grants have provided to the City \$366,500 from 1974 through 1979. Examples of park projects made possible by these grants include Chautauqua Auditorium restoration, the purchase of park property at 3rd and Canyon Boulevard, and the development of Parkside, Tantra and Heuston Parks. The block grant funding sources probably offer the greatest potential to address some of the needs for park improvements in the Core Area, assuming that these grant programs continue to be funded. Block grants have provided \$283,900 to park and recreation projects during the period 1974-1979, and these funds contributed substantially to projects for Chautauqua restoration, Whittier School/Park Improvements, Tree Planting Program, Canyon Park redevelopment, and Core Area Mini-Parks. Also, any available government surplus property should be investigated as possible park and recreation areas for development of the needed facilities identified in this Master Plan.

168

In summary, the successful implementation of the Parks and Recreation Master Plan will require the use of all of the above funding sources. No one source can be expected to provide sufficient funds to implement the Parks and Recreation Master Plan, but collectively they should provide an adequate resource base to make many positive gains in meeting Boulder's parks and recreation needs.

### Implementation Schedule

A major consideration that must be weighed in scheduling the implementation of the Parks and Recreation Master Plan is the ability of the City to finance the ongoing operating and maintenance costs for new parks and recreation facilities. As the Six Year Capital Improvement Program for the City is reviewed and updated each year an operating and maintenance cost projection should be prepared for each project in order to insure adequate operating and maintenance funds will be available to support the project when it is completed.

The development proposed by this Master Plan is in direct response to the city's projected growth as outlined in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

In order to meet the standards outlined in the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, maintain the current level of recreation services, and address current deficiencies in parks and recreation services, the proposals outlined

169

in this plan should be implemented within the planning period of 1980 to 1992. Priorities for implementation of the Master Plan are as follows:

1. Acquisition of land in advance of subdivision development.

2. Restoration and redevelopment of older deteriorating parks and recreation facilities, and improved access to existing park areas to include accommodations for the handicapped.

3. Development of joint school/park sites to address deficiencies in older developed portions of the city.

4. Development of new parks and recreation facilities in response to growth of the city.

These priorities are needed as a general guide for implementing this Master Plan and are not intended to dictate that all items under category number 1 be accomplished prior to addressing the projects under category number 2, but rather that projects of equal need and significance will be prioritized by this implementation system. As an example, a land acquisition project which is not threatened by immediate private development of the land would not have a higher priority than a project which would restore to use an older deteriorated park facility because the relative significance of one project may be of a more immediate concern than another project.

170

The Parks and Recreation Master Plan has been developed to provide maximum flexibility to respond to changing economic conditions and fluctuations in the growth of the city. There currently exists appropriate review processes for the Capital Improvement Program to allow a dynamic planning approach to implementing the concepts and standards established by this plan. It is also anticipated that the Parks and Recreation Master Plan will be subject to the same review and amendment process provided by the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan and can thereby be maintained as a current planning document throughout the planning period.

191

but only through formal Plan amendment procedures) is necessary for full and effective implementation of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN.

6. That the master plan is consistent with the County's goal statement 1.2;

"Existing communities should grow, if at all, at whatever rate they consider desirable, within the limits of what is acceptable to the citizens of areas potentially affected by that growth, and to the citizens of the County, while preserving and improving the quality of life and the aesthetic and functional fitness of land uses within the County."

7. That the inclusion of and strict adherence of the City and County to Policy 3.07 of Exhibit 2 is one of the most significant aspects of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN; that strict compliance with the intent and provisions of the "Municipal Annexation Act of 1965" CRS 1973, as amended, and the resultant unification of the growth decision-making process within the Valley, is so vital to the County's Plan implementation efforts as to warrant additional and unilateral County actions to require compliance with Policy 3.07 should the City be unable and/or unwilling to comply.

8. That hardships and inequities resulting from the policy transition should be minimal because (a) services can be provided to existing development where necessary to serve the public interest (e.g., alleviate a major health or safety problem); (b) the term "new urban development" refers basically to new proposed subdivisions and major rezonings; (c) there is an existing inventory of more than two thousand (2000) vacant platted units/lots in the County at this time; and (d) the amount of vacant land shown for urban development on the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is substantially more than that needed to accommodate the projected population within the planning period.
