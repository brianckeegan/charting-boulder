RESOLUTION NO. 243

A RESOLUTION APPROVING THE BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN AND SUPPORTING AND RELATED DOCUMENTS; AND, SETTING FORTH DETAILS IN RELATION THERETO.

WHEREAS, the City Planning Board and Council, pursuant to C.R.S. §31-23-106 (1973) and other laws, have authority to adopt a master plan for the City of Boulder and those areas outside of the City which are related to the planning for the community;

WHEREAS, it is found and determined as follows:

1. The population of the City and the surrounding Boulder Valley area has grown from approximately 21,000 in 1950 to approximately 95,000 at present;

2. A study commission reviewed growth and related questions in the mid-1970's and the people of Boulder adopted a growth limitation proposal in November, 1976;

3. A comprehensive revision of the plan for the Boulder Valley has been under study and review for well over a year and during that time much data and information have been accumulated and analyzed concerning present conditions and numerous plans and projections for the future have been made and developed;

The plan and related documents have the approval and support of the professional planners who have been instrumental in its development, many groups interested in public issues, and numerous individuals throughout the community;

WHEREAS, pressure for growth and development is anticipated to continue and planning should respond in a positive and affirmative way; reasonable steps must be taken to accommodate the growth while at the same time retain the character of the community; such planning should consider, among other factors, those which follow:

a. That land both inside and outside the City is a finite resource and must be put to use in an efficient manner;

b. That people demand facilities and services and these should be phased in accordance with a reasonable and realistic financial program;

c. That efforts should be made to provide low and moderate income housing;

d. That the City must be an attractive place to work and live so as to prevent and discourage increased pressures for sub-urban communities;

e. That protection of the physical setting and environment is of importance and concern to the public;

WHEREAS, the Planning Board and the City Council are of the opinion that the plan now proposed appropriately considers the factors outlined above and that it meets statutory requirements, to-wit: has a general purpose of guiding and accomplishing a coordinated, adjusted and harmonious development of the City and its environs which will, in accordance with present and future needs, best promote health, safety, morals, order, convenience, prosperity, and general welfare, as well as efficiency and economy in the process of development, including, among other things, adequate provision for traffic, the promotion of safety from fire, flood waters, and other dangers, adequate provision for light and air, the promotion of helpful and convenient distribution of population, the promotion of good civic design and arrangement, wise and efficient expenditure of public funds; and adequate provision of public utilities and other public requirements.

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE PLANNING BOARD AND THE CITY COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF BOULDER, COLORADO:

- That land within the Boulder Valley is a finite resource and must be put to use in an efficient manner;
- That people demand facilities and services and these should be phased in accordance with a reasonable and realistic financial program;
- That efforts should be made to provide low and moderate income housing;
- That the City should continue to be an attractive place to work and live so as to prevent and discourage increased pressures for suburban communities;
- That protection of the physical setting and environment is of importance and concern to the public, and

WHEREAS, Boulder County land use decisions within the Boulder Valley have been guided by the previous jointly adopted City/County master plan since 1970, and the County has acquired considerable land use knowledge and experience therefrom, and

WHEREAS, the County has, over the period of the last two years, committed considerable Staff planning efforts in the master plan study and review; conducted numerous and unprecedented neighborhood meetings throughout the unincorporated areas of the

- 6 -

Boulder Valley, notifying virtually every unincorporated property owner of record of said meetings; conducted numerous public meetings before the Boulder County Long Range Planning Commission and Boulder County Board of County Commissioners so that public input might be facilitated and encouraged at every point in the development of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN; and referred the proposed '77 revision to the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN on March 31, 1977 to all other municipalities in Boulder County and to affected local, State, and regional agencies, and

WHEREAS, the County of Boulder, through its involvement in the review and development of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, has found and determined as follows:

1. That a single and jointly adopted City/County master plan is desirable and necessary at this time to assure the harmonious development of the City of Boulder in relationship to its environs; being particularly necessary for the proper and efficient coordination of transportation facilities, the management of flood plains and other hazard areas, the review of proposals under City and County jurisdiction for new urban development, and for the proper and accurate assessment of the external effects of City's growth policies on the other municipalities and unincorporated areas of Boulder County.

2. That the area encompassed within the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, as generally described within Policy 1.02 of Exhibit 2 and as more particularly delineated on Exhibit 1, is properly designated as the planning area of the City of Boulder by virtue of

- regional water and wastewater Boulder service area designations

- 7 -

- topographic and historic relationships of land forms and uses within the Boulder Valley

- the established commitment of the City of Boulder to peripheral Boulder Valley open space acquisition

3. That the reasonableness of the "Area II/III" designations of the master plan is substantiated and supported by the City of Boulder's service commitments and Capital Improvements Plan, and that said designations are necessary in order particularly to

- encourage future urban development to locate within or adjacent to existing urban areas in order to eliminate sprawl and strip development, to assure the provision of adequate urban services and to preserve agriculture, forestry, and other similar land uses

- to avoid the duplication and overlapping costs in the provision of urban services and to establish a satisfactory level of quality, quantity, and dependability of those services

- maintain a balanced and diversified economy, assuring an adequate level of local employment opportunities and strengthening and stabilizing the local tax base

- maintain a functional balance between community facilities and potential land capacities (population)

- promote an efficient, coordinated, and multi-mode transportation system

- promote the use of open space as an urban-shaping tool

- encourage through land uses the provision of adequate water for all the County's diverse land uses, and to discourage the further conversion of agricultural water within the County to urban uses

4. That the planning period of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN, as described in Policy 1.03 of Exhibit 2, is a desirable and reasonable time frame in which to accurately project land uses and the availability of services.

5. That the Plan concept of an "Area II/Area III service line" (which "service line" may be changed, with the resultant conversion of additional Area III lands into Area II designation,

- 8 -

but only through formal Plan amendment procedures) is necessary for full and effective implementation of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN.

6. That the master plan is consistent with the County's goal statement 1.2:

"Existing communities should grow, if at all, at whatever rate they consider desirable, within the limits of what is acceptable to the citizens of areas potentially affected by that growth, and to the citizens of the County, while preserving and improving the quality of life and the aesthetic and functional fitness of land uses within the County."

7. That the inclusion of and strict adherence of the City and County to Policy 3.07 of Exhibit 2 is one of the most significant aspects of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN; that strict compliance with the intent and provisions of the "Municipal Annexation Act of 1965" CRS 1973, as amended, and the resultant unification of the growth decision-making process within the Valley, is so vital to the County's Plan implementation efforts as to warrant additional and unilateral County actions to require compliance with Policy 3.07 should the City be unable and/or unwilling to comply.

8. That with the provision of services to existing development where necessary to serve the public interest (e.g., alleviate a major health or safety problem) specifically exempted from the provisions of Policy 3.07; and with the term "new urban development" referring basically to new proposed subdivisions and major rezonings; and finally with an existing inventory of more than two thousand (2000) vacant platted units/lots in the County at this time; that hardships and inequities resulting from the policy transition should be minimal.

- 9 -

9. That it is the expressed understanding of the County that the effect of the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is not to, nor intended to, reduce in any way the existing rights of unincorporated property owners under 31-8-101 through 31-8-211 CRS 1973, as amended.

10. That specific methods of Plan implementation to be used by the City and County shall include, but not be limited to, the following:

- land use regulations
- City Capital Improvements Plan
- service standards
- City annexation and utility extension procedures and decisions
- open space preservation, and

WHEREAS, the addition of Exhibit 1.E.1 Specified Density/ North Boulder and Exhibit 1.E.2 Specified Density/Cherryvale- Hoover Hill to the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is warranted to insure compatibility of future development with the existing neighborhoods in those areas.

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN (including Exhibits 1.E.1 and 1.E.2, but excluding, pending further review and study, the Exhibits designated 2.C.3 as they relate to the unincorporated areas in the Boulder Valley) is hereby adopted by the Planning Commission as part of the Boulder County Comprehensive Plan; and

BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that the BOULDER VALLEY COMPREHENSIVE PLAN is hereby recommended for adoption by the Boulder County Board of County Commissioners.

## SECTION I: APPLICATION

1.01 The structure, administration, and implementation of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan shall be based on specific and non-conflicting policies jointly adopted by Boulder County and the City of Boulder pursuant to the City Charter and Colorado Revised Statutes 30-28-106 through 30-28-109, and 31-23-106 through 31-28-110, C.R.S 1973, as amended. ## SECTION II: COMMUNITY DESIGN

An urban area is land intended to accommodate concentrations of people who conduct their many and varied living activities on that land. These people need and depend upon receiving various facilities and services. Governments, usually cities, provide these services.

In Boulder County, and not unlike most other areas throughout the country, land use regulations have traditionally permitted urban uses and development in areas where adequate urban facilities and services are not yet provided, coordinated, nor planned. It is uniformly and universally agreed that the resulting patterns of leapfrog remote urban development are inefficient, wasteful, and seriously contrary to the public interest, health, safety, and welfare. One of the most important objectives of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan is the reduction, if not elimination, of this urban sprawl.

The basic outline and approach of the Plan can be concisely stated. The areas immediately surrounding the City can most efficiently and effectively be provided facilities and services by the City. These areas are the most logical areas for urban development. The City intends to provide, on a phased basis over the planning period, the facilities and services to accommodate this urban development. This context should be kept in mind when considering the policies and other statements that follow.

2.01 For purposes of Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan structure, the Boulder Valley is divided into three major areas. Area I is that area within the City of Boulder which has adequate facilities and services and is expected to continue to accommodate urban development. Area II is the area now under County jurisdiction planned to accommodate urban development, and new urban development is to occur coincident with the availability of adequate facilities and services. This area is projected to be provided required facilities and services by the City by being annexed to the City during the planning period; Area IIA being the area of immediate focus, the first three years, and Area IIB being accommodated within the balance of the planning period. Area III is the remaining area in the Valley, generally under County jurisdiction and which is not now planned to accommodate urban development for the following reasons: it is not projected that the City will there provide adequate facilities and services within the planning period; no other facilities and services agency comparable to the City is expected in the area; and it is primarily a rural and agricultural area and its character should be preserved and protected.

### SECTION III: FACILITIES AND SERVICES

In the foregoing policies, the City and County find that new urban development should not occur within the Boulder Valley until an adequate range of urban services is available to serve the development. It has been determined that the deferment of or variance from the requirement(s) of adequate urban services for urban development is not in the best interests of public health, safety, and welfare and should not be continued.

3.01

The City and County intend that new urban development not occur until an adequate range of facilities and services is available to serve the development. The County experience indicates that provision of services by a municipality is preferable to provision of services by special districts and private groups, in that municipalities have politically accountable leadership, general police power and the ability to coordinate provision of all necessary urban services. Therefore, there is a rebuttable presumption that adequate urban services can only be provided by the City of Boulder. However, it is not the intent of the City or County to preclude the proposal of alternate service systems so long as these services meet the criteria for services set forth in the Comprehensive Plan and the standards for adequacy developed by the City of Boulder and County of Boulder.

3.02

Adequate facilities and services for residential development shall minimally consist of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, urban transportation, parks, and schools; for industrial and commercial development shall minimally consist of public water, public sewer, urban fire and police protection, and urban transportation. The determination of when new urban development will be allowed shall be based on the availability of the foregoing services, as more particularly defined herein and in Service Standards Appendix to the Comprehensive Plan Policies. Determinations of facilities and services adequacy shall include each of the following factors:

## SECTION VI: ENVIRONMENTAL FEATURES

The fragile environment which characterizes the Boulder Valley is a critical asset that must be preserved and protected. It is the framework within which growth and development may be permitted to take place.

4.01 The environmental impact of public and private projects shall, where legally necessary, be determined and the statement made available for public inspection and considered by governmental agencies before any discretionary decisions are made.

4.02 The Boulder Valley is to be planned in a manner which recognizes air quality as a critical concern. In light of this, logical land use relationships, expanded use of public transportation, use of pollutant control devices, stabilization of soils through use of vegetation, appropriate monitoring of urban development and mining operations, and such other means as may be feasible, shall be implemented.

4.03 The City and County shall seek to protect and improve the quality of the Boulder Valley waterways through identification and control of indirect, as well as direct, sources of pollution. Particular concern shall be given to improved land use configurations, standards to control degradation of streams and lakes caused by storm runoff in urban and rural areas, and control and monitoring of direct sources of discharge, including those of gravel extraction and wastewater treatment facilities.

4.04 Noise abatement shall be a continuing environmental concern which shall be implemented through improved land use relationships, as well as by ordinance, with particular attention being given to the impact of transportation and industrial facilities, and proper acoustical design.

4.05 Energy conservation shall be encouraged through means such as improved insulation of existing and new structures, clustering of buildings, and the use of alternative energy sources such as sun and wind, with concomitant consideration of air rights.

4.06 Hazardous areas which present danger to life and property from flood, forest fire, steep slopes, unstable soil, subsidence, or similar conditions, shall be delineated, and development in such areas carefully controlled or prohibited.

4.07 Hillside and ridge-line development shall be carried out in a manner which, to the extent possible, avoids both negative environmental consequences to the immediate and surrounding area, as well as the degrading of public views and vistas.

4.08 The functional and aesthetic qualities of drainage courses and waterways shall be preserved and enhanced in a manner compatible with a basically non-structural approach to flood control.

4.09 Critical habitat areas for wildlife and flora shall be maintained, preserved, and conserved.

## SECTION V: ECONOMIC CONSIDERATIONS

The City and County shall encourage a viable and balanced economic structure and employment base within the parameters of established land use, environmental and growth policies; with emphasis on providing employment opportunities for Boulder Valley residents and a tax base which provides sufficient governmental revenue to meet community goals.

5.01 In order to maximize the return on previous capital improvements, new development and redevelopment shall locate in areas where adequate public services and facilities presently exist or are planned to be provided under the City's Capital Improvements Program.

5.02 Since the public costs of annexing and developing several areas concurrently could prove excessive, the City shall limit said costs to those which can reasonably be accommodated within the Capital Improvements Program, and are compatible with anticipated revenues.

5.03 The City and County will take steps to have furnished or obtain cost-benefit studies for all proposed subdivisions of more than 40 acres in size as an essential reference for decision making. Decisions will be made, and to the extent possible, will require new development to bear the expense of facilities and services reasonably required to serve such projects.

5.04 The City and County shall support maintenance and expansion of a reasonably diversified employment base within the Boulder Valley, reflecting manpower and labor force capabilities and recognizing amenities for emphasizing scientific, technical and related industries. In addition, where consistent with the various policies of the Comprehensive Plan, the internal growth of industries currently located within the Valley shall be preferred over the establishment of new industries.

5.05 All reasonable legal steps will be taken toward the end of keeping new employment opportunities within the Boulder Valley consistent with the employment needs, stated growth policy, and other objectives of the City and County.

5.06 The City and County shall encourage local employers, to the maximum extent feasible, to provide employment opportunities for the local unemployed and underemployed work force, and to implement affirmative action programs in cooperation with various agencies providing employment assistance programs.

5.07 The City and County shall encourage new industries to locate within industrial parks in order to provide for efficient utilization of land, improved site design, accessibility by public transportation and other essential services, and economies of scale.

5.08 Industrial zoning under the Comprehensive Plan shall provide the opportunity for the location of industries of various types and uses, including those uses considered essential to the Boulder Valley population from a service standpoint.

## SECTION VI: INDIVIDUAL OPPORTUNITIES

Planning of the Boulder Valley shall be directed toward achieving and maintaining a socially, culturally, economically and environmentally balanced community which offers a wide range of opportunities and choices for individual residents, and recognizes a variety of unique personal needs, services, activities, and interests.

6.01 The unique and powerful community identity and sense of place which characterizes the citizens of the Boulder Valley and its setting shall be recognized and respected by policy decision makers. ✓

6.02 The City and County shall recognize the rights of citizens to play a role in governmental decisions which affect their lives and property, through continual efforts to maintain and improve open and public communication and conduct of business. In addition, the City and County shall continue to support adequate programs and provide opportunities for citizen participation and neighborhood involvement. ✓

6.03 The City and County shall seek the effective provision through public and private organizational involvement, of maintenance, remedial and developmental programs and services to its citizens; such as special education, child care, nutrition, physical and mental health services, crime prevention, recreation, multi-modal transportation, and manpower training.

6.04 The City and County shall take all reasonable steps to ensure that new development, public as well as private, be designed in a manner which is sensitive to meeting the social, physical, and emotional needs of the community. Broadly defined, this shall include factors such as accessibility to the elderly, handicapped, and other less mobile individuals; provision of coordinated facilities for pedestrians, bicyclists, and bus-riders; provision of functional landscaping and open space; and the appropriate scale and massing of buildings. ✓

6.05 The City and County, through their land use regulations and incentive programs, shall encourage the private sector to provide a mixture of housing types with varied price ranges and densities, which attempt to meet the needs of all elements of the Boulder Valley population. Where such efforts are inadequate to meet the needs of low and moderate income families, the City and County shall utilize appropriate Federal, State and other programs and resources to supplement private activities.

6.06 The City and County shall encourage low and moderate income housing, whether publicly or privately financed, to be designed, in such a manner as to be compatible with and dispersed throughout the individual project as well as the larger community.

6.07 Expansion of the Boulder Valley housing supply should reflect, to the extent possible, job availability in the private and public sectors, and should relate to the income levels of those employment opportunities.

The Boulder Valley (including the City of Boulder, and generally encompassing those land areas bounded by the mountain backdrop on the west, Marshall Mesa on the south, Davidson Mesa on the southeast, Gunbarrel Hill on the northeast, and the ridge between Mesa Reservoir and Boulder Reservoir on the north - as more specifically delineated on the Comprehensive Plan Map) has been experiencing unprecedented and rapid growth with respect to population, housing, land development, and the utilization of natural and community resources since the early 50's; growing from 21,500 people in 1950 to 95,000 people at present. The citizens of Boulder have become seriously concerned about the effects of uncontrolled growth on their city and the quality of life. Voters of the City of Boulder in November, 1971 approved a resolution directing local government to "take all steps necessary to hold the rate of growth in the Boulder Valley to a level substantially below that of the 1960's." In 1976, the Growth Limitation Ordinance, which was designed to control the rate of new population increases and to increase the density of the Central Area, was approved by the citizens of Boulder.

Transportation, water sewerage, schools, parks and recreation, police and fire protection, and other necessary public facilities and services are being planned to meet the needs of the Boulder Valley's growing population, but the City and County are experiencing an increasing inability to fund and provide the demanded facilities and services at a pace which will keep abreast of the ever-growing public need.

It is the experience of Boulder County that such adequate urban facilities and services are not available to new urban developments outside the City limits of Boulder, inasmuch as the County of Boulder is not funded nor equipped to provide such urban facilities and services, and those urban facilities and services are not being provided or made available by developers, special districts, or private groups. Examples of inadequate services provided to urban development under County jurisdiction include:

**WATER** - Many subdivisions and special districts have experienced supply, dependability, contamination, administration, and/or distribution problems with individual or central water sources and distribution systems, relying in some cases on cisterns and excess Boulder water in backup for domestic supply. In most cases, limitations, or the non-existence, of distribution systems, lack of water pressure, and/or insufficiency of adequate maximum flows result in inadequate on-site supplies for fire fighting purposes and dry hydrants, resulting in rural fire associations and districts having to bring limited water supplies to the fire by vehicle.

Residents of the Boulder Valley, and a growing number of fellow Americans, are increasingly challenging the ethic that all growth is good growth. No longer is unconditional growth being accepted unquestioningly as a necessary premise of progress and an enhanced standard of living. Growing environmental concerns, a recent and renewed awareness of the energy crisis confronting us at the national and local levels, together with a growing sense of loss of sense of community, have combined with increased significance to make us realize that we do not have unlimited room or resources. We are only beginning to see that our energy, environmental, and community ills stem, essentially, from the same source: from patterns of uncontrolled and illogical urban growth and development that waste our energy resources just as liberally as they lay waste our natural environment. We no longer live in a time when we were few and the land was wide and waiting, but have reached the point where the natural resources and amenities once regarded as endlessly available and expendable are becoming increasingly scarce.

Perhaps one of the most difficult changes which must be made is in the patterns of urban growth. No longer can we accept or tolerate leapfrog or urban sprawl development which scatters jobs, homes, shopping centers and recreation areas across the length and breadth of the landscape. The spreading patterns of settlement and development that characterize our urban areas are the legacy of our old illusion that we had endless acres of land to build on and unlimited energy to burn. The Citizen's Advisory Committee on Environmental Quality and the Task Force on Land Use and Urban Quality issued a report; The Use of Land: A Citizens' Policy Guide to Urban Growth, which begins with the finding that Americans must begin to view land as a resource, as limited as minerals, water and clean air, and indicates that there is a new mood in America. Increasingly, people are asking what urban growth will add to the quality of their lives; they are questioning the way relatively unconstrained, piecemeal urbanization is changing their communities, and are rebelling against the traditional processes of government and the marketplace which, they believe, have inadequately guided development in the past.<sup>1</sup>

This new attitude toward growth, practically unheard of a few years ago, is rapidly spreading through communities across the country. In previous years, any reasonably good developer could enter a zoning, subdividing, or permit hearing, confident that, if his project was well-designed, he would get anything he wanted in the way of zoning modifications, sewer permits, and other special requirements. Local governments were more than happy to remove any potential roadblocks in the name of encouraging growth - or progress. Today, the picture is quite different. Local governments are responding to public pressure to control growth, to stop the inefficient use of land, and to safeguard their public monies from waste on urban sprawl. Studies have indicated that growth does not pay for itself and urban sprawl or leapfrog development is very costly for local governments. ## TRANSPORTATION

The Transportation Plan has evolved directly from the land use considerations of the revised Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. A two and one-half year multi-agency study assessed Boulder Valley transportation needs, using urban design and travel demand modeling techniques. Solutions which satisfied those needs were identified, developed, and extensively analyzed. Travel modes studied in detail included highways, public transportation bikeways and pedestrian facilities.

Every attempt was made to insure that substantial and reliable citizen input was incorporated into the study. Random sample interviews, twenty citizens' workshops, and forty hearings were conducted to aid in the establishment of goals and objectives and to address the various alternatives. Videotapes of many technical addresses were made and placed in the public library for community use. Thirteen technical volumes were published.

### HIGHWAY AND TRANSIT ELEMENTS

#### Planning Methods

A simulation model of existing travel relationships was used to forecast future trip characteristics. Projection of future increases in the study area's population and employment levels was combined with observed trip-making behavior to estimate travel demand and distribution, and to identify the prominent transportation corridors.

A modal split model was developed to indicate how people might choose between transit and automobiles. This distribution is reflected in transportation networks for evaluation of alternatives.

## FIRE

The Boulder Fire Department has been working on a comprehensive study of its functions to prevent, detect, and suppress fires efficiently and to provide emergency life support. For the past twelve years, Boulder has had only three major fire stations. As the land area of the City increased through annexation, areas distant from the stations have been provided with fire protection but with very poor response time. New stations are needed and new concepts have been developed to provide effective fire protection for the entire City.

One of the new concepts developed during the past year is the satellite fire station (a station smaller than the existing major stations). Its primary purpose would be the provision of fire protection and related services to residential areas. City Council has approved two satellite stations, one in north Boulder and one in south Boulder, to be located in converted dwellings. They will have light attack squad pumpers to provide fast application of fire suppression or emergency life support. The Gunbarrel area will require a full-sized fire station because of the industrial and commercial uses in this area.

The Fire Department is working on a system of measuring fire protection needs based on several factors, including land use, response times, and built-in fire protection, to determine the type of fire stations and equipment needed in the future.

In the areas that will not be annexed during the planning period, volunteer rural fire districts will continue to provide fire protection. Some of these fire districts could become taxing districts as the cost of providing fire protection increases.

## ENVIRONMENTAL FEATURES

Geological factors and their attendant constraints have been important components in the development of the 1977 Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. The 1970 Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan considered limited geological data. Accumulated earth science information has been compiled in the "Urban Geology Map Series," which has been utilized in the preparation of the updated Plan.

Environmental factors can be separated into four categories: geological hazards, engineering constraints, mineral resources, and <sup>flood control</sup> Each factor discussed hereafter has definite implications for development of the updated Plan.

#### Mine Fire

An area of approximately 150 acres south and east of Marshall evidences active combustion of in-place coal, surface subsidence and cracking, and surface denudation. ### ENGINEERING CONSTRAINTS

#### Material Movement

Gravity and flowing water constantly modify the landscape. The Foothills, because of their topographic relief, are particularly susceptible to drastic forms of <sup>material</sup> movement. Movement is also evident on many slopes in excess of five percent throughout the valley province, including both mesa and valley slopes.

#### Slope Instability

Land slides are evident throughout the Boulder Valley. Slopes in excess of five percent in the areas of shale bedrock are susceptible to failure

- Environmental Features -

if modified by development. Open space buffers should be obtained on, above and below steep slopes. These safe zones should prevent slope disturbance and remove citizens from areas of hazard.

Areas of active landsliding should be off-limits to any development.

Areas designated as "Potentially Unstable" require thorough slope stability analysis before decisions concerning development can be made.

### Alluvial Fans

Streams flow down the steep narrow canyons of the foothills and pour out onto the flatter mesa surfaces. At this point, the water spreads, drops velocity, and deposits its sediment load. This results in the accumulation of fan-shaped mounds of sediment at the mouth of each small V-shaped canyon. These alluvial fans, evident along the western edge of the Boulder Valley, receive the bulk of their sediment during a few days of each year. Occasional floods deposit heavy loads of sediment, including boulders up to twenty feet in diameter. Every few decades a devastating event, such as the Big Thompson flood of August, 1976, may completely re-sculpt an entire fan. Because of their hazardous character, alluvial fans are basically unsuitable for development.

### Earth Flows

Throughout the past there have been extremely destructive material movement events within the foothills environment. Examination of the deposits on the sediment surface bordering the foothills has discovered boulders exceeding twenty-five feet in diameter.

Earth flow deposits are interlayered with the previously mentioned alluvial fan deposits. These deposits are characterized by extreme variation in grain size. Earth flows contain boulders up to twenty-nine feet in

Deposition occurs during periods of extremely high precipitation.

Areas judged susceptible to earth flows are unsuitable for development.

### Slope Debris

The steep hogback slopes on the western border of the Boulder Valley are littered with potentially destructive boulders. All this debris will, in the geological scale of time, make its way to the foot of these slopes. Anyone proposing development within the area shown as susceptible to debris damage should be required to provide analysis and mitigative engineering.

### Expansive Soil and Rock

The Pierre Shale, Laramie Formation, and Fox Hills Formation each contain potentially disruptive expansive clays. The greater <sup>part</sup> of the Boulder Valley is underlain by one of these bedrock types. Proper engineering can mitigate the potential hazard. Anyone proposing development within an area designated as "Potentially Expansive" should be required to supply appropriate soil testing and mitigative engineering.

Hydrocompaction refers to sediment volume decrease by hydration. During Neo-Glacial times, the Denver-Boulder metro area was a glacial desert. Little organic, ground-stabilizing cover existed. Prevailing westerly

## FLOOD CONTROL

The streams and creeks within the City of Boulder were formed by nature to provide channels for flood flows resulting from heavy snow melt and rainfall. Boulder's flood problems began with the initial development of the community adjacent to Boulder Creek. This development established a pattern for similar residential, commercial, or industrial construction along other portions of Boulder Creek and within other flood areas in the Boulder Valley. As a result of private and public development over the flood plain, flood heights and velocities have increased and the potential for economic loss and concern for personal safety has increased.

Boulder is particularly vulnerable to flood damage because of its unique geographic location. In addition, because of its proximity to the mouth of Boulder Canyon, the Central Area of the city is highly susceptible to the particular hazards of flash floods. In an effort to provide an overall, coordinated program to control flood situations, and to protect life and property, the City of Boulder has adopted a comprehensive Flood Control Program.

There are two main elements in Boulder's Flood Control Program. The primary effort has been the adoption of a City Flood Plain Ordinance which regulates construction within the area subject to 100-year floods. Each flood plain within the Boulder Valley ultimately will be divided into two parts: first, the flood storage area, which is basically the slow-moving, storage area for flood waters where development is allowed to take place if adequate flood-proofing measures are taken; and, second, the floodway or flood hazard district, which is the primary channel of the 100-year flood,

The second major flood control effort of the City of Boulder is the Flood Control Master Plan, which calls for the enlargement of natural drainageways to contain peak flows of 100-year floods. Conceptual design has been adopted for most of the major creeks and drainageways in the City of Boulder, and additional design detail of those areas is in progress. As development occurs, each land developer is required to construct a portion of the drainage facility included within, or abutting, his property to insure adequate drainage and control of flood and storm water.

The aspects of the two flood control programs are interrelated; progress in one area affects the classification of the other.

The Flood Control Program provides a sound regulatory basis for guiding private and public development in flood areas, taking into account both the most desirable uses of land from the viewpoint of the community as a whole, and the needs and rights of private landowners.

The County of Boulder has flood plain regulations which are generally compatible with those of the City of Boulder; therefore, transition from County to City jurisdiction as it relates to the Flood Control Program will not cause great difficulties.

## WATER SERVICE

Historically, with few exceptions, the City of Boulder has been far-sighted in its water utility planning, thus being prepared for the major population gains of the past two decades. In the future, improvements will be staged to serve the population increases as they are allowed to occur. The planning for water system improvements will be based on estimating the new facilities which will be needed to serve those areas designated for new growth. The staging of these facilities will be timed to correspond with the growth, so that service to existing customers will not be impaired.

In providing for present and future populations, Boulder's water supplies are derived from mountain regions tributary to the upper reaches of North Boulder and Middle Boulder Creeks and mountain lands in the upper reaches of the Colorado River on the Western Slope. The Colorado River waters are brought to the Eastern Slope by the Colorado-Big Thompson project which fills many reservoirs, including the Boulder Reservoir. Waters in the Boulder Reservoir can then be exchanged for additional Boulder Creek water or treated and directed into the Boulder water system. The water, after being treated, is held in four reservoirs along the westerly edge of the City. A fifth reservoir is located on Gunbarrel Hill to serve the Gunbarrel area. In the future, additional reservoirs may be required at sites at the top of Hoover Hill, on Arapahoe Road, and in the foothills near Four Mile Creek.

The distribution system is divided into three pressure zones serving ground elevations varying from 5,100 to 5,800 feet. Treated water from Boulder Creek can be delivered to all three zones by gravity through several pressure-regulating stations. Water from the Boulder Reservoir plant can also be delivered to all three zones through pump stations.

## WASTEWATER

Increasing concern for protection of the environment, with particular emphasis on water quality, has highlighted the importance of the City's wastewater collection and treatment system. The national goal of fishable, swimmable waters by 1983, and the expressed desire of City Council to develop Boulder Creek as a sport fishery, provide the framework for wastewater treatment decisions in the Boulder Valley. Planned treatment plant capacity is governed by service area population projections for 1985 and 1995.

The wastewater collection system consists of approximately 249 miles of pipe of varying sizes, separate from the storm sewer system. Major interceptors adequate to serve the existing service area are in place. Extension of sewer mains will be coordinated with other public facilities and constructed to conform with long-range plans to achieve the most economical means of providing sewer service. Sewer mains will need to be extended within the service area where individual systems fail, to prevent public health problems from polluted groundwater.

## SUPPLEMENTAL POLICY A: THE CENTRAL AREA

The City of Boulder, through the merging of public and private interests, shall seek to retain the Central Area of the Boulder Valley, defined generally by Baseline Road, Iris Avenue, the west city limits, and 34th Street, as the primary governmental, cultural, office, financial, entertainment, shopping, medical facility, and high density residential district of the Boulder Valley.

Within the Central Area, several distinct, yet interrelated sub-areas exist: the Downtown Business District; the University; the Crossroads Area; transitional and older stable residential neighborhoods, special business districts, the hospital facilities area, and newer multi-family neighborhoods.
